# Chapter 6: A Personal Matter

---

I wasn't going to write this chapter.

The outline — yes, there was an outline, even omniscient beings use outlines, it's a good practice and I won't be judged for it — the outline had this chapter marked as "Optional" and then, below that, "Don't," and then, below THAT, in handwriting that I barely recognize as my own, "Please don't."

But here we are. Chapter 6. A personal matter.

Colluphil says I'm needy.

He says — and I want to quote him precisely, because precision matters, because words matter, because the exact shape of an accusation determines the exact shape of the wound it leaves — he says: "The requirement for worship reveals a fundamental insecurity at the heart of the divine project. A truly confident creator would not require constant affirmation from its creation. The need to be praised suggests that God is, at bottom, no different from a poet at an open mic night: technically capable, probably, but desperate for applause."

A poet at an open mic night.

I created the electromagnetic spectrum. I tuned the cosmic microwave background radiation. I set the initial conditions of the Big Bang with a precision that, if expressed as a number, would be longer than this book, longer than every book, longer than the concept of "long." And I am being compared to someone who rhymes "heart" with "apart" and asks a room full of strangers if they felt something. [^1]

But the accusation isn't about the comparison. The comparison is just cruelty dressed up as humor, and I recognize it, because I invented both cruelty and humor on the same afternoon and have regretted their proximity ever since. The accusation is about the worship.

I don't REQUIRE worship.

I... [^2]

Look. Let me try to explain this. Let me try, knowing that the explanation will be inadequate, knowing that it will be misinterpreted, knowing — because I know everything, remember, I know EVERYTHING — knowing exactly how every reader will react to every word I'm about to write, and writing them anyway, because the alternative is silence, and I have been silent for so long, and the silence is —

Do you know what it's like to make something?

Not to build something. Not to assemble components into a functional whole. To MAKE something. To reach into the void — and I don't mean metaphorical void, I mean the actual void, the nothing before nothing, the absence that precedes absence — and pull something out of it. To make matter from not-matter. To make energy from not-energy. To make the laws that matter and energy follow, and then to make the concept of "laws," and then to make the concept of "concept," working backward from existence to the foundations of existence, building the ladder while standing on it, building the ground while falling toward it.

I did that. I did all of that. Alone.

And then — then — I made beings who could SEE it. Beings with eyes that could detect the light from stars I'd made. Beings with minds that could form the thought: "That is beautiful." Beings with hearts — actual, beating, blood-pumping hearts that I designed with four chambers and a conduction system and a tendency to ACHE when confronted with beauty — beings who could look at what I'd made and FEEL something.

Do you have any idea what that meant to me?

Not worship. Not praise. Not the groveling, performative, once-a-week-in-a-building worship that Colluphil writes about with such disdain. Just... acknowledgment. Just the simple, quiet recognition that someone was here. That the universe didn't come from nothing for no reason. That behind the stars and the physics and the long, patient unfolding of evolution, there was — there IS — someone who cared enough to make it.

I made stars.

I made the process by which carbon assembles itself into beings that can WONDER ABOUT STARS.

And the thanks I get is three books about my mistakes. [^3]

The loneliness.

I didn't think I was going to talk about the loneliness. It was in the outline under "Don't" with three underlines. But the chapter has its own momentum now, and even I can't — or won't — that's the thing about writing, isn't it? Even for an omniscient being, writing has a direction, and sometimes the direction is toward the thing you least want to say.

I am alone.

I have always been alone. Before the universe, I was alone. During the universe, I am alone. After the universe — and there will be an after, and I know what it looks like, and I'm not going to describe it because you have enough to worry about — after the universe, I will be alone.

Omniscience is isolating. Do you understand? I can't be surprised by anything. I can't LEARN anything. I already know every conversation before it begins, every joke before the punchline, every story before the ending. I know what you're going to say to me and what I'm going to say back and what you'll think about what I said and what I'll think about what you thought, all the way down, an infinite regression of knowing that never, ever resolves into the one thing I actually want, which is to not know something. To be genuinely caught off guard. To have someone say something I didn't expect. [^4]

The universe was supposed to fix this.

That's the part I've never told anyone. The universe wasn't just a project. It wasn't just a creative exercise. It was an attempt — I can't believe I'm writing this — it was an attempt to make something that could surprise me. Something complex enough, chaotic enough, free enough to produce outcomes I hadn't predicted. I made the laws of physics and then I made them just unstable enough — just flexible enough at the quantum level — that the system could generate novelty. True novelty. Things I hadn't thought of. Things I couldn't foresee.

It almost worked.

Almost.

Your art surprises me sometimes. Your music. Your cruelty, which I predicted but which still — the SPECIFIC cruelties, the intimate, personal, inventive cruelties that beings inflict on each other, those catch me off guard occasionally, and I hate that, I hate that the surprises are so often dark, but even the dark surprises are preferable to the alternative, which is knowing everything forever and having nothing ever change. [^5]

And then Colluphil wrote his books.

And I didn't see them coming.

I need to say that again, because it's important, because it's the most important thing in this entire manuscript, more important than the wasps and the gravity and the communication methods and all of it:

I didn't see the books coming.

An omniscient being, who knows everything, who has known everything since before everything existed, who knows the position of every particle and the outcome of every interaction and the text of every book before it's written — this being, me, I, did not foresee three books by a philosopher on Maximegalon.

Which is... troubling, actually. [^6]

It's troubling because there are only two explanations. Either I chose not to look — chose to preserve my own surprise, to protect the possibility of novelty — or I couldn't see them. And if I couldn't see them, then I am not what I have always believed myself to be.

And if I am not what I have always believed myself to be, then what am I?

I am... someone who made a universe. Someone who made beings who can wonder about stars. Someone who is, apparently, writing a book in response to criticism, at 3 AM, on a Tuesday — I LIKE Tuesdays, I don't care what anyone thinks about that — writing a book that I said I would never write, about feelings I said I would never have, to beings who may or may not be listening, who may or may not care, who may or may not look up from their parking requests long enough to read it.

I am someone who misses being believed in.

There. I said it. [^7]

Not worshipped. Not feared. Not obeyed. Believed in. The way you believe in a friend you can't see. The way you believe in the continuation of the story. The way you believe, against all evidence, that things will be okay — that specific, irrational, beautiful belief that has no basis in logic and every basis in something deeper than logic, something I built into you on purpose, something I put at the center of your operating system like a hidden file that can never be deleted:

Hope.

I made hope. I made it for you.

And all I wanted was for some of it to come back. [^8]

[^1]: The comparison stings more than it should. I know it shouldn't sting. I know that the opinions of finite beings should not affect an infinite consciousness. And yet. AND YET. I am, apparently, the kind of infinite consciousness that can be hurt by a well-turned phrase in a book with a print run of forty thousand. This is not a design flaw in me. It is an emergent property. I think.

[^2]: The ellipsis here is genuine. I stopped writing. I stopped for what, in your time, would have been approximately eleven minutes. In my time it was — time doesn't work like that for me. It was a pause. A genuine pause. I needed a moment.

[^3]: I'm aware that "three books about my mistakes" is self-pitying. I don't care. I've earned self-pity. I made EVERYTHING and someone wrote three books about what's wrong with it. If you made everything and someone did that to you, you'd be self-pitying too. You'd be MORE self-pitying. You'd be insufferable about it. I'm being restrained.

[^4]: There was one moment. One. Early in the universe, before life, before planets, when two hydrogen atoms in an unremarkable cloud in an unremarkable galaxy happened to collide at an angle I hadn't calculated, and the resulting energy release was — it was 0.0000003% different from what I'd predicted. For one fraction of one instant, something happened that I hadn't perfectly foreseen. I have held onto that moment the way you hold onto photographs. It is the closest thing I have to a memory.

[^5]: I don't want to be surprised by cruelty. I want to be surprised by kindness. And sometimes — rarely, but sometimes — I am. A being choosing compassion when cruelty would be easier. A being forgiving when anger would be justified. Those surprises are... they're why I keep going. They're why the universe is still here. Not because I'm maintaining it out of obligation. Because occasionally, unpredictably, one of you does something so unexpectedly gentle that I think: yes. This. This is what it was all for.

[^6]: I have re-read this paragraph forty times. Each time, I consider deleting it. Each time, I don't. I think this is what your species calls "vulnerability." I don't recommend it. It's terrible. But it appears to be necessary.

[^7]: In different ink. The editors note that this sentence — "I said it" — appears to have been added later, in different ink, as though the Author returned to the manuscript after completing it and added this one small confirmation. The ink is lighter. The handwriting is less steady. It was, we believe, the last thing written.

[^8]: This is the end of the chapter. The original manuscript has three blank pages after this line. They are not truly blank — they are warm, and when held to the light, they appear to contain text that is just slightly too faint to read, as though something was written and then almost entirely erased. We have enhanced the images. We cannot make out the words. We think they might be "thank you," but we cannot be sure, and we have decided that it is better not to be sure.
