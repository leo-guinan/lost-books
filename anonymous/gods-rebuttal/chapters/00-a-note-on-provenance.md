# A Note on Provenance

### *From the offices of Conditions Press, Maximegalon*

---

The manuscript now published as *God's Rebuttal* was discovered on the desk of Professor Oolon Colluphil at 3:47 AM on a Tuesday morning, Maximegalon Standard Time. The precise time is known because the clock on Professor Colluphil's wall, which had not worked in six years, started again at that exact moment, ran for approximately eleven seconds, and then stopped permanently, its hands fixed at 3:47.

The desk was locked. The office was locked. The building was locked. The planet, for that matter, was experiencing one of its periodic electromagnetic security sweeps, during which unauthorized material transport is, according to every physicist we consulted, "impossible, or at the very least, extremely impolite."

Security footage from the corridor shows nothing. This is not to say it shows an empty corridor. It shows *nothing*. For a period of approximately ninety seconds, the cameras recorded a perfect, uniform absence — not darkness, not static, but a positive and assertive *nothing*, as though reality had briefly excused itself from that particular hallway and hadn't bothered to send a replacement.

The manuscript was found face-down on the desk, as though placed there by someone who had changed their mind about leaving it but couldn't quite bring themselves to take it back. The first page bore a slight impression, as from a hand resting on it — a hand that, based on pressure analysis, was either very large or very tired, or both.

Professor Colluphil reports that he found the manuscript when he arrived at his office at 7:15 AM. He states that the room smelled of ozone — "the sharp, clean kind, like after lightning, not the industrial kind" — and of something else he could only describe as "regret, but the old kind. The kind that's been sitting in a cupboard for a very long time."

The manuscript was warm. Not hot. Warm. The warmth of something recently held. It remained warm for six days.

We subjected the paper to every analysis available to modern publishing forensics:

**Carbon dating** returned results that were, in the words of the laboratory, "inconclusive, but older than expected." When pressed on what "older than expected" meant in quantitative terms, the lead analyst said, "Older than the paper. Older than trees. Older than the concept of 'older.' We would prefer not to discuss this further," and then took early retirement.

**Paper stock analysis** confirmed that the paper does not match any known manufacturer, any known fiber source, or any known manufacturing process. The fibers appear to be cellulose, but arranged in a pattern that one materials scientist described as "organized with intention" and another described as "smug." The watermark, when held to light, appears to show a different image to each viewer. The publisher saw a small, sad-looking cloud. The editor saw an eye. The typesetter saw nothing, but reported a strong feeling that something was looking back.

**Ink analysis** revealed a substance chemically similar to iron gall ink, of the kind used in manuscripts of great antiquity, but with trace elements that do not appear on the periodic table. We consulted three chemists. Two said the results were "anomalous." The third said, "Something wrote this that doesn't use pens," and refused to elaborate.

**Handwriting analysis** was inconclusive. The script is confident, fluid, and bears no resemblance to any known hand, living or dead. It was written in a single sitting — there are no variations in pressure or ink density that would indicate pauses, rest, or the passage of time. Whoever wrote this wrote the entire manuscript, all 89 pages, without stopping. Without, apparently, thinking. As though it had all been composed long ago and was only now, reluctantly, being committed to paper.

There are, however, signs of hesitation. On seven occasions, words have been crossed out and replaced. On three occasions, entire sentences have been struck through so forcefully that the pen tore the paper. On one occasion — in Chapter 7 — there is a mark that appears to be where the pen was simply held against the page, unmoving, for a very long time. The ink pooled. It soaked through to the next page. The blot, when examined under magnification, is not random. It appears to be a very, very small question mark.

We have made every effort to identify the author. We have failed.

One Author, of course, has been ruled out — on account of not existing. This, we feel, simplifies the matter considerably, though not in any way that is particularly helpful.

The manuscript is published here in its entirety, with the author's footnotes preserved as found. We have added nothing. We have corrected nothing. We were, frankly, too frightened to try.

— **The Editors, Conditions Press**
*Maximegalon, who accept no liability for existential crises induced by the following text*
