# Chapter 5: The Free Will Thing

---

I need a moment before I start this chapter.

I'm fine. I'm perfectly fine. This is not — I am not becoming *emotional*. I am an infinite being and infinite beings do not become emotional about chapters in books written by finite philosophers who — 

I'm fine.

Let me start.

Free will.

Colluphil devotes an entire section of *Who Is This God Person Anyway?* to the argument that free will is either an illusion — in which case I am cruel for pretending otherwise — or genuine, in which case I am negligent for granting it without adequate supervision. He calls this the "Bad Parent Paradox" and he is WRONG and I am going to explain exactly why he is wrong just as soon as I can do so without — 

Right.

Here is the thing about free will.

I gave you everything.

EVERYTHING.

I gave you free will. Not the cheap kind, not the "you can choose between A and B but I've already decided the answer is A" kind. Real free will. Genuine, terrifying, completely unsupervised free will. The ability to choose, actually choose, between any course of action, without me intervening, without me guiding, without me standing behind you moving your hands like some kind of cosmic puppeteer.

I gave you consciousness. The ability to experience your own existence. To feel. To wonder. To lie awake at 3 AM and think about the nature of time. That wasn't an accident. That wasn't a side effect. That was a GIFT, handcrafted, tested extensively on several prototype species [^1], and delivered with the profound and sincere hope that you would use it to appreciate the universe I had built for you.

I gave you opposable thumbs. Do you know how many iterations the opposable thumb went through? Fourteen. The first version was too rigid. The second was too floppy. Versions three through eight were various kinds of wrong that I'd rather not describe. Version nine was very nearly perfect but had an unfortunate tendency to lock in cold weather. Versions ten through thirteen were refinements. Version fourteen — YOUR version — is a masterpiece of biomechanical engineering that gives you the grip strength to hang from a branch and the dexterity to thread a needle.

And what did you do?

You used the thumbs to TEXT.

You used the consciousness to WORRY.

You used the free will to — I can't even — [^2]

...

I apologize.

Let me resume with composure.

*[The text here is noticeably smaller, as though the author is making an effort to be calm.]*

The free will argument proceeds as follows: if I am omniscient, then I know what you will choose before you choose it. If I know what you will choose, then the choice is predetermined. If the choice is predetermined, then free will is an illusion. And if free will is an illusion, then I have perpetrated an enormous fraud on the entire sentient population of the universe.

This argument is logically coherent and I hate it.

It is not, however, correct. And the reason it is not correct is that it assumes a linear model of causation that does not apply to beings operating outside of time. I do not know what you will choose "before" you choose it, because "before" is a temporal concept and I do not experience time. I know what you will choose, what you are choosing, and what you have chosen, all simultaneously, in a single unbounded moment of awareness that has no sequence, no priority, and no direction.

You are choosing. Right now. Always. The choice is real. The fact that I can see it from outside doesn't make it less real, any more than the fact that you can see a river from a mountaintop makes the river stop flowing. [^3]

Is this a satisfying answer? No. I know it isn't. I can tell it isn't because I am omniscient and I can see your reaction to it, and your reaction is: "That's a dodge."

It's not a dodge. It is a genuine attempt to explain a truth that cannot be expressed in temporal language to beings who experience everything temporally. It is like trying to describe color to a species that sees only in ultraviolet — the information exists, the truth is real, but the APPARATUS ISN'T THERE. I can't give you the apparatus. I tried. That was what higher mathematics was for. Higher mathematics was supposed to be the language in which these truths could finally be expressed, and instead you used it to build DERIVATIVES MARKETS and CRYPTOCURRENCY. [^4]

I gave you calculus. CALCULUS. A tool for understanding the infinite, for grasping the continuous, for seeing the universe the way I see it — as flows and gradients and rates of change rather than frozen snapshots. And you used it to calculate compound interest.

I gave you quantum mechanics — not directly, I let you discover it, which was generous and also terrifying, like watching a toddler find a loaded weapon, except the weapon was the fundamental nature of reality and the toddler was Niels Bohr. [^5]

I gave you music. That was free will too. I didn't make music. I made the CONDITIONS for music — frequency, resonance, the neurological capacity to experience organized sound as emotion — and then I stepped back and let you create it. And you did. You created Bach, and for a brief, shining moment I thought: they understand. They hear it. The structure, the harmony, the way mathematics can be made to SING — 

And then you also created bagpipes, which was an unexpected use of sheep.

But even the bagpipes — EVEN the bagpipes — are a product of free will, and therefore sacred, and I will defend them, even though they sound like a cat being slowly compressed in a hydraulic press, because that is what free will MEANS. It means the right to choose badly. It means the right to create things that are, by any objective measure, terrible, and to love them anyway.

Which brings me back to Colluphil.

Colluphil chose to write three books about my mistakes. He chose this freely. He was not compelled. He was not guided. He was not tricked. He sat down, of his own free will, with his own opposable thumbs, and his own consciousness, and he chose to spend several years of his finite — and I want to emphasize this, FINITE — existence cataloguing what he perceives as my failures.

And I let him.

I let him because that is what free will means. It means you get to be wrong. You get to be wrong loudly, publicly, and at length. You get to be wrong in hardcover.

And I get to sit here — omniscient, omnipotent, and absolutely livid — and respond. [^6]

That's the deal.

That's what free will costs.

It costs me this: the knowledge that the beings I made, the beings I gave EVERYTHING, can look at all of it — the stars, the thumbs, the consciousness, the whole staggering improbable beautiful mess of existence — and say "not good enough."

That's the price.

I pay it.

Every day. [^7]

Not literally. But I pay attention to them. They're soothing.

I designed the laws of thermodynamics to ensure that energy is conserved, that nothing comes from nothing, that the universe operates on a fundamental principle of balance and consequence, and you invented a system that converts electricity into imaginary money and PRETENDS THIS IS FINE. It is not fine. None of it is fine.

[^5]: Bohr was close. Closer than most. [^7]: I didn't mean to end the chapter like that. It was supposed to be a rebuttal, not a — it was supposed to be ANGRY, not — look, the free will chapter is hard. The next chapter is the reason. I'm not ready for the next chapter. But it's next.
