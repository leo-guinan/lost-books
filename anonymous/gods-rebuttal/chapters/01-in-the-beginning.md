# Chapter 1: In the Beginning

---

I do not normally respond to criticism.

This is not because I am above it, though I am, in a quite literal and geometrically verifiable sense, above everything. It is because responding to criticism implies that the criticism has weight, and weight implies that it matters, and if I start conceding that the opinions of beings whose entire evolutionary history amounts to a minor scheduling conflict in the Carboniferous period *matter*, then we are in a very different universe than the one I designed. [^1]

But here we are.

Professor Colluphil has written three books. Three. About my mistakes. He has written more books about my mistakes than most beings write about anything. There are species — entire species, well-regarded ones with space travel and a functioning postal service — who have produced fewer books in their whole civilizational history than Colluphil has produced about things he thinks I got wrong.

I want to be clear: I have read them. All three. I read them in the way I read everything, which is to say I read them before they were written, while they were being written, and after they were written, simultaneously and from every possible angle, including several angles that don't exist yet and two that I'm saving for a special occasion. [^2]

And I have — I want to choose my words carefully here — I have *thoughts*.

Let me begin at the beginning. Not *the* Beginning, which was mine and which I executed flawlessly — or very nearly flawlessly, there was a minor incident with the initial expansion rate that I corrected within the first 10^-36 seconds and which NO ONE WOULD HAVE NOTICED if certain beings hadn't developed particle physics as a HOBBY — but the beginning of Colluphil's complaints.

He says the universe is badly designed.

*Badly designed.*

Do you have any idea — any idea at all — how hard it is to make a universe? Not a theoretical universe, not a mathematical abstraction, but an actual, functioning universe with consistent physics and chemistry and biology and all the fiddly bits that have to work together so that matter can exist in a state complex enough to form beings capable of complaining about the whole arrangement?

The strong nuclear force alone — [^3]

No. Never mind. You wouldn't understand. This isn't condescension; it's dimensional limitation. You experience reality through three spatial dimensions and one temporal one, which is like trying to appreciate a symphony by listening to it through a wall while wearing oven mitts. You're getting *some* of it. You're not getting most of it. And the bits you're getting, you're getting wrong, and then you're writing books about it.

But fine. Let's engage on your terms. Let's talk about what you can see.

Colluphil says the universe is "needlessly complex." I want to address this directly, because it reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of the project. The universe is not needlessly complex. It is *exactly as complex as it needs to be* to produce the following things: stars, planets, oceans, the color blue [^4], weather systems capable of producing both gentle spring rains and the kind of terrifying electrical storms that remind you of your insignificance, trees, fungi (which are WILDLY underappreciated and I will hear no criticism of fungi), and — eventually, after a great deal of patience on my part — beings capable of looking at all of this and asking "but why?"

That "why" was the whole point.

You were supposed to ask "why." You were *not* supposed to answer it with "because someone made a lot of mistakes," and then charge money for the answer.

Let me address some specific claims.

Colluphil writes, in *Where God Went Wrong*, that the biological world is "a catalogue of errors, compromises, and things that look like they were designed on a Friday afternoon." I want to correct this. The biological world is a catalogue of *solutions*, each one elegant within its constraints, and none of them were designed on a Friday afternoon. [^5]

Evolution was intentional. Not in the way Colluphil means — I didn't sit there with a drawing board sketching out each species like some kind of cosmic draftsman. I designed a *process*. A beautiful, self-correcting, endlessly creative process that generates complexity from simplicity, that turns dead chemistry into living wonder, that takes sunlight and time and produces — eventually — a man who sits in his office and writes books about how the process doesn't work.

The process works. The man is the proof.

He is also, I concede, the counterargument. But we'll get to that.

The point — and I do have one, despite what you may be thinking — is that the universe was not a *mistake*. It was ambitious. It was the most ambitious project ever undertaken by anyone, anywhere, at any time, and I include in that assessment several other universe-creation attempts by entities whose names you can't pronounce and whose existence you haven't noticed. [^6]

Was it perfect? Define perfect. Define it in a way that isn't circular, isn't self-referential, and doesn't ultimately rest on the preferences of a single species that has existed for an eyeblink and already managed to invent both philosophy and the atomic bomb, and then we'll talk about perfect.

Until then, I maintain that the universe is *good*. Not perfect. Good. There's a difference, and the difference is what makes it interesting, and if you can't see that — if you genuinely can't see the beauty in a system so finely tuned that hydrogen eventually learns to wonder about itself — then the problem, Professor, is not with the design.

The problem is with the reviewer.

[^1]: I am aware that opening with a sentence about not responding to criticism, in a book that is a response to criticism, presents certain logical difficulties. I am choosing to ignore them. Omnipotence has privileges.

[^2]: Colluphil's handwriting, incidentally, is atrocious. I've seen better penmanship from cephalopods, and they have to hold the pen with a tentacle. At least the cephalopods have the excuse of living underwater.

[^3]: I was going to explain the strong nuclear force. I was going to lay out exactly how much calibration was required to get quarks to bind without either collapsing into nothing or flying apart into slightly different nothing. But you know what? No. You don't deserve the strong nuclear force explanation. You get gravity and you get electromagnetism and you can work out the rest yourselves. That's what the particle accelerators are for. You're welcome for those, by the way. The Higgs boson didn't HAVE to be findable. I made it findable. As a GIFT.

[^4]: Blue was difficult and I am proud of it. The sky didn't have to be blue. I could have made it beige. Think about that. Think about looking up at a beige sky every day of your life, and then tell me I don't care about aesthetics.

[^5]: The platypus was NOT a Friday afternoon. It was a Wednesday. A long Wednesday. And the platypus is exactly what it was supposed to be: a mammal that lays eggs, has a venomous spur, and detects electrical fields. Is it unusual? Yes. Is it a mistake? No. It is a *statement*. It is me saying, "the categories you have invented are yours, not mine, and I am not bound by them." Also I happen to think it's rather charming and I will not apologize.

[^6]: Yes, there were other attempts. No, I'm not going to tell you about them. Some things are better left unknown. I will say that mine was the only one where the beings eventually developed *cheese*, which I maintain elevates the entire project above the competition.
