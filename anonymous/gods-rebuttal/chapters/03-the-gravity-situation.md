# Chapter 3: The Gravity Situation

---

Colluphil, in Chapter 7 of *Where God Went Wrong*, makes the following claim:

"The direction of gravitational pull — 'down' — appears to have been chosen entirely at random, with no consideration for the convenience of the beings who would subsequently be required to live with it."

I have read this sentence four hundred and seventy-one times. [^1]

I have read it forwards, backwards, in the original Maximegalese, in twelve translations of varying quality, and in the raw linguistic substrate that underlies all language everywhere. I have examined it from outside of time, where you can see a sentence the way you see a landscape — all of it at once, every word simultaneously present, every implication radiating outward like light from a particularly stupid star.

And I can confirm: it is wrong.

Down was not arbitrary. There was a REASON for down. The reason is comprehensive, elegant, and — I want to stress this — entirely logical. It proceeds from first principles through a chain of reasoning that touches on dimensional topology, the curvature of spacetime, the relationship between mass and energy, and a property of reality that you don't have a word for because you can't perceive it, on account of being three-dimensional beings trying to form opinions about an eleven-dimensional system. [^2]

Let me try to explain.

Actually, no. Let me try to explain why I can't explain.

Imagine you are a painting. A flat painting on a flat canvas. You have length and width. You can perceive other flat things — shapes, colors, the edge of the frame. Now imagine someone tries to explain "depth" to you. Not by showing you depth, because you can't see it. Not by giving you depth, because the canvas is flat. But by describing it, using only concepts available to a flat painting.

That's what explaining gravity to you is like. Except worse, because at least the painting has the good sense not to write books about how the frame was hung wrong.

Down is not a direction. Down is a *consequence*. It is what happens when mass tells spacetime how to curve, and spacetime tells mass how to move, and the whole arrangement produces an experience that your brains — your dear, limited, well-meaning brains — interpret as "things fall." [^3]

Things don't fall. Things follow geodesics through curved spacetime. The apple doesn't drop from the tree because something pulls it. The apple follows the path of least resistance through a geometry that mass has sculpted, and it arrives at the ground not because "down" is a real direction but because the ground is where the geometry leads.

I designed that. I designed the geometry. I made spacetime capable of curvature, and I made mass capable of causing that curvature, and I calibrated the relationship between them with a precision that — 

You know what, let me give you a number.

The gravitational constant. G. 6.674 × 10^-11 cubic meters per kilogram per second squared. Do you know what happens if that number is different? If it's larger — even slightly larger — stars burn too fast. They consume their fuel in millions of years instead of billions. There isn't TIME for planets to form, for chemistry to complexify, for life to emerge. The universe is a brief, bright flash and then nothing.

If it's smaller — even slightly smaller — stars don't ignite at all. Hydrogen drifts. Clouds of gas hang in the void like abandoned thoughts, never reaching the density needed to spark fusion. No stars. No planets. No heavy elements. No carbon. No you.

I threaded the needle. I found the value — the EXACT value — that gives you stars that burn long enough to make planets, planets that are heavy enough to hold atmospheres, atmospheres that are thick enough to support chemistry, chemistry that is complex enough to become biology, and biology that is stubborn enough to eventually produce a philosopher who sits in a warm room on a stable planet orbiting a well-behaved star and writes that gravity was "chosen at random." [^4]

At RANDOM.

The man is sitting in a gravitational field calibrated to fourteen decimal places, on a chair that exists because electromagnetism holds molecules together at a strength I specifically chose, breathing an atmosphere I designed with a composition I selected, and he thinks it was RANDOM.

Do you know how many possible values G could have taken? Infinite. Literally infinite. Not figuratively infinite, not "a very large number" infinite — ACTUALLY INFINITE. And out of that infinity of possible values, I chose the one that leads to you. To chairs. To atmospheres. To Tuesdays. To a man who writes books. [^5]

And his criticism is that things fall *down* instead of some other direction.

WHAT OTHER DIRECTION WOULD HE PREFER?

Up? He wants things to fall up? He wants to live on a planet where you drop a cup of tea and it hits the ceiling? He wants rain to fall upward? He wants to spend his life bolted to the floor so he doesn't drift into the stratosphere?

Sideways? Is that better? Things fall sideways? You're walking down the street and suddenly your hat accelerates horizontally at 9.8 meters per second squared and embeds itself in a wall? [^6]

Down works. Down is the direction that lets you put things on tables. Down is the direction that keeps oceans in their basins and atmospheres on their planets and philosophers in their chairs. Down is not arbitrary. Down is the only direction that makes any sense at all, given the geometry of the spacetime I designed, and if Colluphil can't see that, it's because he's looking at it from the wrong number of dimensions.

Which, to be fair, is the only number of dimensions he has.

Which, to be fair, is my fault.

Which is — 

I'm going to move on now. [^7]
