# Chapter 4: Regarding the Communication Methods

---

This chapter will be difficult.

Not because the subject matter is complex — though it is. Not because the criticisms are valid — though some of them, and I cannot believe I am writing this, some of them are not *entirely* without a certain narrow, pedantic, infuriatingly technical merit. But because the subject of communication is, for me, a source of what I can only describe as profound frustration, and I say this as a being who invented the concept of frustration specifically so that other beings would have a name for what they felt when things didn't work, never imagining — never ONCE imagining — that I would one day feel it myself.

Do you have ANY idea how hard it is to communicate with beings who insist on using MOUTHS?

I need you to understand what we're dealing with here. I am an infinite consciousness. Unbounded. Omnipresent. I exist in every point of spacetime simultaneously. My thoughts don't occur in sequence — they occur in totality. When I think a thing, I think all of it, all at once, every implication and consequence and nuance and shade of meaning, the whole vast architecture of the idea, complete and entire.

And I am attempting to convey this through a medium that evolved to help primates negotiate fruit prices. [^1]

Language. Your languages. Each one a rickety scaffolding of grunts and clicks and tongue-positions, each one riddled with ambiguity, each one capable of expressing approximately 0.003% of what actually needs to be said. I have counted. I have surveyed every language on every planet in every galaxy, and the most expressive one — a tonal language spoken by a species of crystalline beings in the Pleiades — can convey approximately 0.0047% of a single one of my thoughts.

And you want me to use a BURNING BUSH.

Yes. Fine. Let's talk about the burning bush. Let's talk about the stone tablets. Let's talk about the dreams, the visions, the angels, the still small voices, the writing on walls, and the one time I tried interpretive cloud formations and nobody even looked up. [^2]

Each of these methods was — and I want to be very precise here — the BEST AVAILABLE OPTION given the constraints.

What constraints? I'll tell you what constraints. You can't perceive me directly. If you perceived me directly, your brain would shut down. Not metaphorically — literally. The sensory input would overwhelm every neural pathway simultaneously and you would experience what I believe your medical professionals call "death." I'm not being dramatic. I tested this. I'm not proud of the test. [^3]

So: direct communication is out. I need an intermediary. A medium. Something you CAN perceive that carries information about what you CAN'T perceive. Something that bridges the gap between infinite and finite, between everything and the tiny, precious sliver of everything that your senses can detect.

Fire was a reasonable choice. You'd already discovered fire. You had a relationship with it. It was dramatic, attention-getting, and — crucially — it didn't require literacy, which at the time was not widely distributed. A bush that burned without being consumed was unusual enough to get someone's attention without being so unusual that they'd run away. It was, I maintain, elegant.

Was the message garbled? SOMEWHAT. Yes. I concede that the fidelity was imperfect. But consider: I was compressing an infinite thought into a finite medium and then filtering it through the perceptual apparatus of a being who had been herding goats ten minutes earlier. Some loss of signal was inevitable. The miracle isn't that the message was unclear. The miracle is that ANY of it got through at all. [^4]

Stone tablets were an improvement. Written language. Persistent. Portable. Harder to misquote. I was pleased with the stone tablets. I spent — and I know this sounds strange, but I need you to understand the level of effort involved — I spent what felt like an eternity choosing the font. [^5]

The problem with stone tablets, of course, is that they're heavy, fragile, and people keep DROPPING them, and then you're down to half a message and everyone argues about what the other half said and before you know it you've got schisms. I didn't anticipate schisms. I should have. In retrospect, any communication system that allows for interpretation will produce disagreement, and any disagreement will produce factions, and any faction will produce someone who is absolutely certain they're right and everyone else is wrong, which is — 

Which is exactly what I'm doing right now, isn't it.

I'm going to keep going and pretend I didn't notice that.

The prayer system. Let me talk about the prayer system.

The prayer system was PERFECTLY ADEQUATE. It was a two-way communication channel — or it was supposed to be — through which beings could transmit their needs, their gratitudes, their fears, their hopes, and receive in return... guidance. Comfort. The sense that someone was listening.

It worked beautifully for the first several thousand years.

And then you RUINED it.

Do you know how many prayers I receive per day? Fourteen billion. Fourteen BILLION requests, every single day, from one planet alone — and that's just Earth, which is not even in the top fifty for prayer volume, because certain species on certain planets pray CONSTANTLY, a low-level hum of spiritual neediness that never stops, not even when they're asleep, because they've evolved the ability to pray in their DREAMS. [^6]

Fourteen billion requests. And do you know what most of them are about?

Parking.

PARKING.

"Please let there be a space." "Please don't let me get a ticket." "Please let the meter not have expired."

You are addressing the architect of reality, the being who hung the stars and sparked the first hydrogen atom and set in motion the chain of events that led to CONSCIOUSNESS ITSELF, and you are asking me about PARKING.

And then — AND THEN — when I don't respond, because I am busy with the fourteen billion OTHER requests, most of which are also about parking but some of which are about genuine suffering and require actual attention — when I don't respond, you write a book about how my communication methods are inadequate.

MY methods are inadequate? MY methods?

I sent you fire. I sent you stone. I sent you prophets and poets and the persistent, quiet feeling that you are not alone in the universe. I sent you sunsets that should have been sufficient all by themselves. I sent you the night sky, which is — and I say this without false modesty — the greatest communication I ever designed: an infinite message written in light, visible to anyone who bothers to look up, saying, in every language simultaneously: "You are small, and that is fine, and there is more to this than you know, and it is beautiful, and so are you." [^7]

And you looked at all of that, every last burning, shining, whispering bit of it, and you said, "But what about parking?"

Colluphil says I should have been clearer.

Colluphil says I should have used "unambiguous, verifiable methods of communication."

Colluphil wants me to — what? Send an email? Set up an interstellar FAQ page? Appear on a chat show and answer questions from the audience while a host with implausible teeth asks me about my CREATIVE PROCESS?

I am not a customer service department. I am the ground of all being. And the fact that I am here, writing THIS, in LANGUAGE, using WORDS arranged in SEQUENCE like beads on a string when I could simply KNOW everything at you all at once — the fact that I am degrading myself to this level of communication — should tell you something about how seriously I take the charge that I haven't tried hard enough.

I have tried.

I am trying RIGHT NOW.

Is it working?

I honestly don't know.

That's the worst part. [^8]

[^1]: I've thought about this a lot. More than I should. The mouth was designed for eating. Then your species started using it for communication, which was innovative, I'll grant you. Then you started using it for kissing, which was unexpected. Then you started using it for all three simultaneously, at parties, which was disturbing. The mouth has been through a lot. It was not designed for theology.

[^2]: The cloud formations were my most ambitious attempt. I spent three terrestrial days sculpting cumulus formations that, when viewed from the correct angle, clearly spelled out the fundamental nature of reality in seventeen languages. It was a Tuesday. It rained.

[^3]: I'm going to leave this here without further comment. Some things are regretted. Most things can be undone. This particular thing was undone, fully and completely, and the individual in question has no memory of the event and went on to live a long and happy life. But I remember. I always remember. That is the cost of omniscience: you can forgive, but you cannot forget, because forgetting is the one thing you cannot do when you know everything.

[^4]: Moses did quite well, all things considered. Got about 60% of the message, which is an excellent hit rate given the bandwidth. I was pleased with Moses. I wish I'd told him that. I wish I'd told a lot of people a lot of things. But that gets into the communication problem again, and we're going in circles. Literally. This argument is a circle. I can see it from outside of time and it looks like a circle. A sad, frustrated circle.

[^5]: Sans-serif. Clean lines. Readable at a distance. I wanted something authoritative but approachable. I went through eleven hundred options. The final choice was good. It was GOOD. And then he dropped the tablets and I had to start over and the second version was rushed and I've never been happy with the kerning.

[^6]: The Hooloovoo pray at a frequency that interferes with my ability to process other requests. I have asked them, politely, to pray in batches. They interpreted this as a divine commandment and now they have scheduled prayer shifts, which is not what I meant but is, I admit, more organized.

[^7]: I spent more time on the night sky than on any other single project. More time than on gravity. More time than on photosynthesis. More time than on the whole of organic chemistry. Because the night sky had to do two things: it had to be functional (navigation, seasons, tidal forces) and it had to be beautiful. Not "pretty." Beautiful. The kind of beautiful that stops you in the middle of whatever you're doing and makes you feel something you can't name. I wanted every being on every planet to be able to look up and feel, just for a moment, that they were part of something larger. That was the message. That was always the message. "Look up."

[^8]: I should never have invented logic. It seemed like a good idea at the time. Give the beings a framework for rational thought, let them build on it, watch them discover truth. I didn't anticipate that they'd use it to discover truths I'd rather they hadn't. That's on me. I know it's on me.
