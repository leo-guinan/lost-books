# Chapter 2: On the Matter of Wasps

---

I know what you're thinking.

You're thinking: "Why is there an entire chapter about wasps?"

And the answer is: because Colluphil devoted four pages — FOUR — to wasps in *Some More of God's Greatest Mistakes*, and those four pages contain more errors per square inch than any other passage in the history of published criticism, and I include in that assessment a particularly scathing review of the Hrung Collapse that was written by someone who had been dead for thirty years and was working from memory. [^1]

Let me be unequivocal: wasps are good.

I'm not going to qualify that. I'm not going to say "wasps serve a purpose" or "wasps have their role in the ecosystem" in that mealy-mouthed way that people defend things they're embarrassed about. Wasps are GOOD. They are one of the better things I made, and I made quasars, so the competition is stiff.

Let's start with the basics, since Colluphil apparently couldn't be bothered.

Wasps pollinate. Did you know that? Of course you didn't. Nobody ever talks about the pollination. They talk about the stinging. They talk about the way wasps hover around your food with what you interpret as malice but which is actually *curiosity* — wasps are endlessly curious creatures, which is MORE THAN I CAN SAY for most of the beings who complain about them.

They pollinate figs.

FIGS.

Do you like figs? Have you ever eaten a fig? Have you ever bitten into that soft, sweet, impossibly complex little fruit and experienced the interplay of texture and flavor that took sixty million years of co-evolution to produce? You're WELCOME. That was wasps. That was wasps doing their job, which they have been doing with quiet dedication since before your species existed, and in return they get four pages of insults from a man who — I happen to know — has never once eaten a fig, because he's "not a fruit person." [^2]

NOT A FRUIT PERSON. I invented fruit. It's one of my better ideas — a mechanism by which plants bribe animals into carrying their genetic material to new locations, wrapped in sugar and color and fragrance, a tiny edible gift that says "thank you for your legs." And he's not a fruit person.

But I digress. Wasps.

Wasps are also predators. They control the populations of pest insects that would otherwise devour crops, defoliate forests, and generally make a mess of the biosphere I spent considerable effort assembling. A single wasp colony can remove THOUSANDS of caterpillars, aphids, and other insects from a garden in a single season. They are, functionally, a free and self-replicating pest control system, and nobody sends them so much as a thank-you note.

"But they sting," you say. "They sting and it hurts and they're aggressive and they ruin picnics."

Yes. They sting. They sting because they are small, and the world is large, and everything in it is trying to kill them, and they have a FAMILY to protect — a queen and larvae and a whole fragile paper architecture that they built with their own mandibles, chewing wood into pulp and sculpting it into geometric perfection, and when something enormous and clumsy blunders into their home, they defend it. [^3]

Is that so hard to understand? Is that really so alien? You lot build armies. You build walls. You build increasingly elaborate systems of surveillance and deterrence to protect your homes from threats real and imagined, and when someone breaks in, you don't politely ask them to leave. But when a wasp defends its nest — its CHILDREN — suddenly it's a design flaw.

The double standard is breathtaking.

Colluphil writes — and I'm quoting directly here, because I want you to see what I'm dealing with — "The wasp is perhaps the clearest evidence that the universe was designed by a committee, or possibly by a single entity in a very bad mood."

A very bad mood.

I was not in a bad mood when I made wasps. I was in an EXCELLENT mood. I had just finished the Hymenoptera order generally, which includes wasps, bees, and ants — three of the most successful, sophisticated, and socially complex groups of organisms on any planet anywhere, and yes I'm including the Silastic Armourfiends of Striterax, who for all their complexity never managed anything as elegant as a honeycomb.

I was *inspired* when I made wasps. I was working at the height of my powers. The wing venation alone — the way the forewing and hindwing lock together with a row of tiny hooks called hamuli, creating a single aerodynamic surface from two separate structures — that's not the work of a bad mood. That's *craftsmanship*. [^4]

And the colors. Colluphil doesn't mention the colors. Black and yellow, the universal warning palette, a signal so clear and so effective that dozens of other species copy it. I invented warning coloration and made wasps its flagship example, and mimicry rings developed around them, and an entire branch of evolutionary biology exists because of the patterns I put on wasps, and the thanks I get is "bad mood."

I want to say something here that I probably shouldn't say.

They remind me of myself.

The wasps.

Small. Angry. Widely misunderstood. Capable of causing disproportionate pain when provoked. Deeply committed to a project that nobody else appreciates. Building something intricate and beautiful with no expectation of thanks. [^5]

Disliked at picnics.

I shouldn't have said that. Forget I said that. The point is that wasps are an integral part of a complex and interdependent ecological system, and criticizing them in isolation reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of — 

No, actually, the point is that I LIKE wasps. I made them and I like them and I don't need to justify that to anyone, least of all to a philosopher who is "not a fruit person" and who has apparently never once sat quietly and watched a paper wasp build its nest, layer by patient layer, in the quiet of a summer afternoon, doing its work without complaint and without praise, because the work is the thing, the making is the thing, and whether anyone notices or approves is entirely —

I seem to have gotten off topic. [^6]

The wasps are fine.

Move on.
