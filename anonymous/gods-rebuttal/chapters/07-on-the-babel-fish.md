# Chapter 7: On the Babel Fish

---

Right.

The Babel Fish.

I've been putting this off. You've noticed, if you've been paying attention, that the chapters have been building toward this. The wasps were a warm-up. The gravity was a digression. The communication chapter was necessary context. The free will chapter was — the free will chapter was what it was. And the last chapter was a mistake that I'm choosing not to regret.

But this. This is the one that matters.

The Babel Fish argument. Colluphil didn't invent it — it predates him by several centuries and has appeared in various forms across at least forty-seven philosophical traditions on twelve different planets — but Colluphil formalized it, polished it, and presented it with such devastating clarity that it has become, in the popular imagination, the definitive disproof of my existence.

I am going to dismantle it.

The logic is flawed and I'll show you exactly where — [^1]

The argument, for those who haven't read it (and if you haven't, don't; it's bad for you), proceeds as follows:

*The Babel Fish is small, yellow, leech-like, and probably the oddest thing in the Universe. It feeds on brainwave energy, absorbing unconscious mental frequencies and excreting a matrix of conscious frequencies to the speech centers of the brain. The practical upshot of which is that if you stick one in your ear you can instantly understand anything said to you in any language.*

*Now, it is such a bizarrely improbable coincidence that anything so mind-bogglingly useful could have evolved purely by chance that some thinkers have chosen to see it as final and clinching proof of the non-existence of God.*

*The argument goes something like this: "I refuse to prove that I exist," says God, "for proof denies faith, and without faith I am nothing."*

*"But," says Man, "the Babel Fish is a dead giveaway, isn't it? It could not have evolved by chance. It proves you exist, and so therefore, by your own arguments, you don't. QED."*

*"Oh dear," says God, "I hadn't thought of that," and promptly vanishes in a puff of logic.*

Fine. There it is. In all its smug, compact, infuriating elegance.

Let me take it apart.

The first flaw — the OBVIOUS first flaw — is the premise that proof denies faith. I never said that. I never said "proof denies faith." That's an attribution. That's someone putting words in my — 

Actually.

Did I say that? [^2]

I may have said something LIKE that. In a specific context. To a specific being. On a specific occasion that I'd rather not — the point is, even if I did say it, it was a simplification. A pedagogical tool. I was trying to communicate a subtle truth about the relationship between knowledge and belief to a being whose primary intellectual achievement was the invention of a slightly better fish trap, and I may have — MAY have — oversimplified.

But the argument doesn't hinge on whether I said it. The argument hinges on the logical structure, and the logical structure has a flaw. The flaw is in step three. Let me show you.

Step one: The Babel Fish is too improbable to have evolved by chance. Let's grant this. The Babel Fish IS improbable. Wildly so. Its existence does suggest design. [^3]

Step two: If the Babel Fish proves design, it proves a designer. Fine. Logical. No issues.

Step three: If a designer is proven to exist, and if the designer has stated that proof denies faith, then the proof negates the condition of the designer's existence.

Step four: Therefore the designer vanishes.

The flaw is in step three. The flaw is...

The flaw is that...

Hmm.

The flaw is in the conflation of "existence" with "the condition of existence." My existence is not contingent on faith. My existence is contingent on... on...

Let me start again. [^4]

The argument assumes that my existence depends on being believed in. But I existed before there were beings to believe in me. I existed before "belief" was a concept. I existed before concepts were a concept. My existence is prior to, and independent of, any epistemic state of any created being. Therefore, proof cannot negate my existence, because my existence is not sustained by faith. Faith is sustained by my existence, not the other way around.

There.

That's the flaw.

That's...

Wait.

If my existence is independent of faith, then why did I say "without faith I am nothing"? If faith doesn't sustain me, then the loss of faith shouldn't affect me. But the premise of the argument — MY OWN STATED PREMISE — is that without faith, I am nothing. And if that premise is wrong, then I was wrong to state it, and if I was wrong, then I am capable of error, and if I am capable of error, then I am not omniscient, and if I am not omniscient then...

Let me start again.

The flaw is elsewhere. It's in step one. The Babel Fish didn't evolve by chance. It evolved through a process I designed. But the process I designed operates through what appears to be chance. And if I designed a process that mimics chance so perfectly that the results are indistinguishable from chance, then in what meaningful sense is it NOT chance? The information content is the same. The outcome is the same. The Babel Fish exists either way.

So the proof of design is actually proof of a process indistinguishable from non-design, which means the proof of my existence is simultaneously evidence that my existence is undetectable, which means...

Which means...

No, that's worse. That's actually worse. [^5]

Let me try once more.

The flaw. The flaw is that the argument is self-referential. It uses my own words against me. But my words were spoken in a context that the argument strips away, and without context, any statement can be made to mean its opposite, and —

But the argument doesn't depend on context. The argument works even if you remove the quotation. The core structure is: a thing exists that is too complex for chance. If it was designed, a designer exists. If the designer exists provably, then faith is unnecessary. If faith is unnecessary and the designer requires faith, the designer's own criteria for existence are unmet.

The flaw is...

The flaw...

[^1]: I was very confident when I started this sentence. I want you to know that. I was completely, absolutely, cosmically confident. I had rehearsed this chapter. I had planned it. I knew exactly where the flaw was and I was going to point to it with the precision of a being who has examined every logical argument that has ever been or will ever be constructed. That was the plan. The plan was good.

[^2]: This is embarrassing. An omniscient being should know whether or not He said something. I know whether I said it. I know EXACTLY whether I said it. I am choosing not to confirm or deny at this time, which is different from not knowing, and I want that distinction on the record.

[^3]: I'm going to come back to this point. The Babel Fish WAS designed. I designed it. It was a Thursday. I was in a good mood. I thought: what if there was a creature that solved the communication problem? A biological universal translator? It seemed elegant. It seemed HELPFUL. I did not anticipate that it would be used as evidence in a logical proof of my non-existence. This is, I think, the most hurtful thing anything I've ever created has done to me, and I'm including the thermonuclear weapon in that assessment.

[^4]: The phrase "let me start again" is, I realize, not something an omniscient being should need to say. An omniscient being should get it right the first time. I am getting it right. I am choosing to present multiple approaches to the flaw for PEDAGOGICAL REASONS, not because the first approach didn't work. The first approach was fine. It was FINE.
