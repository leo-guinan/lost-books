# Chapter 1: The Initial Brief

### *In which the original project proposal is compared, with increasing dismay, to the actual deliverables*

---

Let us begin, as all good Conditions analyses must, with the documentation.

Or rather, let us begin with the almost complete absence of documentation, which is in itself a finding of some significance. In any properly managed project, there exists a brief — a statement of intent, a specification, a document which says, in clear and unambiguous terms, "This is what we are going to build, this is why we are going to build it, and this is how we will know when we are finished." The universe has no such document.[^1]

[^1]: Various civilisations have claimed to possess the original brief, usually inscribed on tablets, scrolls, or in one memorable case, the back of a very old napkin. These documents invariably turn out to be either forgeries, shopping lists, or — in the case of the Jatravartids of Viltvodle VI — a recipe for a quite good casserole. The casserole, it must be said, is better designed than several of the things it shares a universe with.

What we have instead are inferences. We must, like archaeologists sifting through the rubble of a civilisation that never bothered to label anything, piece together the original intent from the evidence at hand. And the evidence, I must say from the outset, is not encouraging.

## The Proposal (Reconstructed)

Based on observable outcomes, the original project proposal appears to have contained the following key deliverables:

1. **A universe** — large, expanding, containing matter and energy in various configurations
2. **Physical laws** — consistent, elegant, and functional
3. **Life** — self-replicating, adaptive, and capable of appreciating the first two items
4. **Consciousness** — so that the life could look at the universe and think, "My, isn't this something"

On paper, this is not a bad brief. It has scope, it has ambition, and it has a clear success criterion: beings that can appreciate the thing they're in. One might even call it elegant.[^2]

[^2]: "Elegant" is a word academics use when something looks simple but is almost certainly hiding a tremendous amount of complexity underneath, like a swan, or a tax return.

The problems begin when we compare this proposal to what was actually delivered.

## The Deliverables (Observed)

What arrived was this:

1. **A universe** — *vastly* larger than necessary, mostly empty, and expanding at a rate that suggests it is actively trying to get away from something
2. **Physical laws** — broadly consistent but featuring several puzzling edge cases, including quantum mechanics, which appears to have been designed by a different department entirely[^3]
3. **Life** — self-replicating, yes, but also self-destructing, self-contradicting, and in many cases, self-loathing
4. **Consciousness** — so that the life could look at the universe and think, "My, isn't this something, and also, why does my back hurt?"

[^3]: The quantum mechanics division is widely suspected to have been a subcontractor hired late in the process after the original team quit. This would explain why it doesn't work properly with the rest of the physics, why nobody fully understands it, and why it behaves differently when you're looking at it — a quality it shares with interns and certain species of cat.

## The MVP Problem

In modern project management, there is a concept known as the Minimum Viable Product, or MVP. The idea is simple: rather than spending years perfecting every detail, you ship the most basic version that works and then improve it based on feedback.

The universe is, I would argue, an MVP that was shipped and then never updated.

Consider the evidence. The early design decisions — the fundamental forces, the basic particles, the general architecture — these are actually rather good. Quarks, for instance, are a lovely piece of work. They come in six flavours,[^4] they interact with each other in ways that are mathematically beautiful, and they form the building blocks for everything else. If the entire project had been executed to this standard, we would not be having this conversation.

[^4]: The flavours are Up, Down, Strange, Charm, Top, and Bottom. It is widely noted that these sound less like fundamental particles and more like the names of cocktails at a bar that is trying too hard. This is not, in itself, a design flaw, but it does raise questions about the naming conventions in use at the time.

But somewhere between quarks and, say, wasps, something went badly wrong.

## The Wasp Memorandum

I must pause here to address the matter of wasps directly, because it is impossible to conduct any honest assessment of Creation without confronting the fact that someone, at some point, sat down and designed an insect whose primary characteristics are aggression, pointlessness, and the ability to sting you multiple times without dying.[^5]

[^5]: Bees, by contrast, can only sting once and then perish. This is tragic but also, in a roundabout way, noble. The bee has consequences. The bee has skin in the game. The wasp is all upside, no accountability — a flying middle finger with a detachable stinger and no performance review.

The wasp serves no vital ecological function that could not be performed by something less openly hostile.[^6] It pollinates, but badly. It controls other insects, but selectively. It builds nests, but in your attic. The wasp is, to use the technical Conditions term, *surplus to requirements while simultaneously being a significant source of complaints.*

[^6]: Several entomologists have written to me disputing this claim. I have read their letters carefully and I note that all of them were written indoors, behind closed windows, which rather makes my point for me.

In any well-managed project, the wasp would have been flagged during quality assurance. A tester would have encountered it, been stung, and filed a bug report reading: "Encountered hostile flying entity. Unprovoked attack. No discernible purpose. Severity: High. Recommendation: Remove, or at minimum, disable the repeated-sting functionality."

That this did not happen suggests one of two things: either there was no quality assurance process, or there was a quality assurance process and the wasp passed it. I am not certain which possibility is more troubling.

## Version Control

The fossil record suggests that the project went through several major iterations, each of which was abandoned rather than refined. The dinosaurs, for example, represented a bold design direction — large, dramatic, undeniably impressive. They were then deleted entirely and replaced with mammals, which are smaller, fussier, and prone to anxiety.[^7]

[^7]: The transition was accomplished via asteroid, which, as a project management tool, leaves something to be desired. It is effective, certainly, but it lacks nuance. It is the Ctrl+A, Delete of extinction events.

This is not iteration. This is not learning from mistakes and gradually improving. This is throwing the whole thing out and starting again, which is what you do when you have no version control and have backed yourself into a corner.

A competent project manager would have branched the dinosaurs, run the mammals in a test environment, compared outcomes, and merged the better solution. Instead, what we got was a sixty-six-million-year hard reset, followed by a period of frantic improvisation that eventually produced, among other things, a primate that worries about mortgage rates.

## Summary of Findings

The Initial Brief, to the extent that we can reconstruct it, was ambitious and not without merit. The early architectural decisions — physics, chemistry, the fundamental constants — show genuine craft. But the implementation was rushed, the testing was non-existent, and the project was shipped in a state that, in any other context, would have resulted in a formal complaint, a refund, and a strongly-worded letter to the regulatory body.

That no such letter has been sent is largely because the regulatory body does not exist, which is, one might argue, yet another item that should have been included in the original brief.[^8]

[^8]: There is a Department of Conditions at the University of Maximegalon, but our jurisdiction is limited and our budget is, to use the technical term, laughable. We have filed several formal requests for expanded authority. These have been ignored, which is consistent with the broader pattern of non-responsiveness documented throughout this volume.

We shall proceed, in the following chapters, to examine the specific areas of failure in detail. I should warn the reader that it does not get better from here.

It gets, in fact, considerably worse.
