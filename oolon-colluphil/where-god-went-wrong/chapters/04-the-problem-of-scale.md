# Chapter 4: The Problem of Scale

### *In which 93 billion light years is considered excessive for the target demographic, and the resource allocation implications of empty space are examined*

---

The observable universe is approximately 93 billion light years in diameter.[^1]

[^1]: This number is already absurd, but it is made more so by the word "observable." The *observable* universe is 93 billion light years across. The actual universe may be significantly larger, possibly infinite, and certainly bigger than anyone asked for. It is as if you ordered a small flat white and were handed the Pacific Ocean. "I only wanted a small one," you might say, to which the barista would reply, "Yes, but look how much you're getting," which is exactly the kind of argument that sounds generous until you try to drink it.

A light year is the distance light travels in one year, which is approximately 5.88 trillion miles. Light is the fastest thing in the universe. Nothing moves faster. It is the absolute cosmic speed limit, the universal "that's as quick as it gets," the final word in velocity.

And yet, at this maximum possible speed, it takes light *93 billion years* to cross the universe.

The beings who live in this universe — the ones for whom, presumably, it was built — can barely manage a trip to the shops.

## The Commute Problem

Consider the typical sentient being. On Earth — which is, I must note, a perfectly ordinary planet in a perfectly ordinary solar system in a perfectly ordinary galaxy that contains approximately 200 billion other solar systems, most of which are doing nothing — the dominant species has a practical range of approximately fifty miles on a good day, after which it needs to stop for fuel, a lavatory, and an argument about whether this is the right road.[^2]

[^2]: The argument about whether this is the right road is a fundamental constant of travel across all known species. Even the Haggunenons of Vicissitus III, who can reshape their entire bodies at will, still argue about directions. They simply do it while turning into increasingly agitated shapes.

Even with technology — spacecraft, warp drives, improbability drives — the distances involved in crossing the universe are so vast that they render travel essentially meaningless for any being with a finite lifespan. You could set off for the nearest galaxy at the fastest speed any human technology has ever achieved, and you would arrive approximately 67,000 years after your departure and 66,920 years after anyone stopped caring.[^3]

[^3]: The Andromeda Galaxy is 2.537 million light years away. The fastest human spacecraft, the Parker Solar Probe, travels at approximately 430,000 miles per hour. At this speed, the journey to Andromeda would take roughly 6.7 million years. To put this in perspective, 6.7 million years ago, the ancestors of modern humans were small, anxious primates who spent most of their time in trees worrying about leopards. By the time the spacecraft arrived, the descendants of the people who launched it would have evolved into something unrecognisable, assuming they hadn't gone extinct, which, statistically, they almost certainly would have.

This is a resource allocation problem of staggering proportions.

## The Empty Space Audit

Let us conduct a simple audit. Of the observable universe:

- **Approximately 99.9999999999999999999958%** is empty space[^4]
- **Approximately 0.000000000000000000004%** is matter
- **Of that matter, approximately 80%** is dark matter, which no one can see, touch, or use
- **Of the remaining 20%,** most of it is hydrogen and helium, which are useful primarily for making stars and party balloons
- **The fraction that constitutes rocky planets capable of supporting life** is so small that expressing it as a percentage would require more decimal places than this page can accommodate

[^4]: I must stress that this is not an exaggeration. Space is called space because it is almost entirely composed of space. It is the most accurately named thing in existence, which is the only area in which it excels.

In any other context, this would be considered a catastrophic waste. If a company built an office building 93 billion light years across and then used one room on the fourth floor, the shareholders would have questions. If a government commissioned a road network spanning an entire continent and then only paved one cul-de-sac, there would be an inquiry. And yet here we are, in a universe that is almost entirely unused, and we are expected to be *grateful.*

## The Hostility Problem

It gets worse.

The empty space is not merely empty. It is actively hostile. The temperature of space is approximately 2.7 Kelvin, which is minus 270.45 degrees Celsius, which is — to use the technical term — *very cold indeed.* There is no air. There is no pressure. There is radiation, which will kill you. There are cosmic rays, which will also kill you. There are black holes, which will kill you in a way that is theoretically fascinating but practically undesirable.[^5]

[^5]: Death by black hole involves being stretched into a long thin strand in a process known as "spaghettification." The fact that this is the actual scientific term tells you everything you need to know about the relationship between physicists and the seriousness of the situation. You are being torn apart at the atomic level, and the word for it sounds like something that happens to pasta. This is the universe's sense of humour, and it is not funny.

The universe, in other words, is approximately 99.9999999% actively trying to kill the things that live in it.

This is not a habitat. This is a *hazard.* If the universe were a building, it would be condemned. If it were a workplace, the health and safety report would be the length of this book. If it were a product, it would be recalled — not because of a defect in one component, but because the entire design philosophy is "mostly lethal, with small pockets of marginal survivability."[^6]

[^6]: The Earth itself is one of these pockets, and even it is trying to kill its inhabitants approximately 40% of the time via earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis, hurricanes, disease, extreme temperatures, and the occasional asteroid. Earth is the "safe" option. Earth is the *good* planet. This is like describing a lifeboat as "comfortable" because it hasn't sunk yet.

## The Scale Defence

I am aware that some theological apologists have argued that the vastness of the universe is evidence of divine generosity. "Look how much you've been given!" they say, gesturing at the void. "All of this is for you!"

This is like being given a birthday present of ten thousand acres of Arctic tundra and being told to be thankful for the square foot that isn't frozen.[^7]

[^7]: My colleague Dr. Frang of the Department of Applied Gratitude (a department that consists entirely of Dr. Frang and a filing cabinet) has argued that gratitude should be proportional to intent, not utility. By this logic, we should be grateful for the universe because it was *meant* well. I would counter that "meaning well" is not an acceptable standard for a being claiming omnipotence. I mean well when I attempt to cook. The results are still, objectively, terrible, but I do not have infinite power and knowledge. I have a saucepan and a vague sense of optimism. The Almighty does not get to use the same excuse.

The scale of the universe is not generosity. It is not grandeur. It is not "designed to inspire awe," although it certainly does that, in the same way that receiving an electricity bill for a house you don't live in inspires awe.

The scale of the universe is a failure of scope management.

## What The Right Scale Would Look Like

A universe designed for its inhabitants would be exactly as large as it needed to be and not one light year more. It would contain the stars necessary for navigation, the planets necessary for habitation, and enough space between them to provide a sense of wonder without a sense of existential dread.

It would not contain 200 billion galaxies if only one of them is occupied. It would not have a diameter that takes light itself 93 billion years to cross. And it would absolutely not be expanding — getting bigger, every day, for no reason that benefits anyone, like a meeting that should have been an email.[^8]

[^8]: The expansion of the universe is accelerating. It is not merely big; it is getting bigger, *faster.* The universe is not just poorly scoped; it is actively adding scope while the project is underway. In project management, this is called "scope creep." In the universe, it is called "the fundamental nature of spacetime." The terminology is different. The problem is the same.

I do not wish to be uncharitable. Perhaps, when the universe was being designed, there was a genuine belief that it would all be used eventually. Perhaps there was an optimistic resource projection that assumed faster-than-light travel would be cracked by lunchtime. Perhaps the scale was aspirational rather than practical.

But aspirational targets are supposed to be revised when reality intervenes, and it has been fourteen billion years. The tenancy rate has not improved. Most of the universe remains not only unoccupied but unoccupiable.

At some point, one must simply acknowledge that the project was over-scoped.
