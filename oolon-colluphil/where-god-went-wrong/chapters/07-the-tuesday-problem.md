# Chapter 7: The Tuesday Problem

### *In which a detailed survey of the days of the week is conducted, a scoring rubric is applied, and Tuesday is found to have no satisfactory justification for its continued existence*

---

I am aware that this chapter will be controversial. I am aware that some will consider it trivial, or petty, or unworthy of inclusion in what is otherwise a rigorous Conditions analysis of cosmic design. To those people I say: have you ever really thought about Tuesday?

Not casually. Not in the vague, unfocused way one thinks about Tuesday when it arrives — that grey, shapeless feeling of "ah, it's Tuesday, is it" that settles over you like a damp towel. I mean really, properly, structurally thought about it. Considered its purpose. Examined its credentials. Asked it, as one might ask an employee at a performance review, "What exactly is it you do here?"[^1]

[^1]: This question, famously posed in the film *Office Space* on Earth, has never been satisfactorily answered by Tuesday. I have put the question to Tuesday directly, via formal correspondence to the Galactic Bureau of Temporal Affairs, and received no reply. The Bureau later informed me that my letter had arrived on a Tuesday and had therefore been "processed with all the urgency Tuesdays typically receive," which is to say, none.

I have. And I have found no answer.

## The Survey

In preparation for this chapter, I conducted a comprehensive survey of the standard seven-day week as observed across civilisations that use such a system.[^2] Each day was assessed against five criteria:

[^2]: Not all civilisations use a seven-day week. The Dentrassis use a nine-day week, the Belcerebons use a five-day week, and the people of Kria have a single day that repeats indefinitely, which they find both efficient and unbearable. The seven-day week is, however, the most common structure among civilisations that have strong opinions about days, which is to say, most of them.

1. **Purpose** — Does the day serve a clear function?
2. **Distinctiveness** — Can the day be distinguished from other days?
3. **Morale Impact** — How does the day affect the beings who must endure it?
4. **Cultural Significance** — Has the day accumulated meaning over time?
5. **Justification** — Can a compelling case be made for its existence?

Each criterion was scored from 1 (poor) to 7 (excellent). The results are as follows:

### Monday — Total Score: 19/35

Monday is universally disliked, but it has *purpose.* It is the beginning. It is the point at which the week starts and everything must be faced again. It is dreadful, but it is dreadful in a way that is structurally necessary. Monday is the cold shower of days — unpleasant but functional. Without Monday, the week would have no clear starting pistol, and everyone would simply drift into work at some point mid-week, looking confused.[^3]

[^3]: This already happens, but Monday provides the framework within which the confusion occurs, and that is, arguably, its contribution.

**Purpose:** 5 | **Distinctiveness:** 6 | **Morale Impact:** 2 | **Cultural Significance:** 4 | **Justification:** 2

### Wednesday — Total Score: 22/35

Wednesday is the middle. It is the hump, the peak, the halfway point after which the week tilts downhill toward the weekend. It is not exciting, but it is *informative.* When someone says "It's Wednesday," you know exactly where you are: in the middle of things, equidistant from the last weekend and the next. Wednesday is a compass bearing. It is navigation. It has earned its place.

**Purpose:** 5 | **Distinctiveness:** 4 | **Morale Impact:** 4 | **Cultural Significance:** 4 | **Justification:** 5

### Thursday — Total Score: 24/35

Thursday is the day before Friday, and this alone gives it a warm, anticipatory glow. Thursday is the moment before the moment. It is the inhale before the exhale. Many civilisations have their most enthusiastic social gatherings on Thursday evening, because the promise of Friday lends Thursday a recklessness it has not earned but thoroughly enjoys.[^4]

[^4]: On Earth, Thursday is also the day on which Arthur Dent's planet was demolished to make way for a hyperspace bypass, which gave Thursday a significance it had previously lacked. One might argue that this is the only truly notable thing to have happened on a Thursday, which is still one more notable thing than has ever happened on a Tuesday.

**Purpose:** 5 | **Distinctiveness:** 5 | **Morale Impact:** 5 | **Cultural Significance:** 5 | **Justification:** 4

### Friday — Total Score: 31/35

Friday is joy. Friday is release. Friday is the day the week finally admits it was a bad idea and lets you go. Friday requires no justification because it justifies itself. It is the only day of the week that is better than expected every single time it arrives, which is a remarkable achievement for something that happens fifty-two times a year.[^5]

[^5]: Friday evening is, by several measures, the happiest regular time interval in the sentient experience. It combines the relief of ending with the anticipation of beginning, the satisfaction of completion with the freedom of possibility. It is the temporal equivalent of finding money in a coat you haven't worn since last winter. No one has ever been disappointed by Friday evening. It is perfect. It is the one thing in this entire analysis for which I have no criticism whatsoever. Well done, Friday. Top marks.

**Purpose:** 7 | **Distinctiveness:** 7 | **Morale Impact:** 7 | **Cultural Significance:** 5 | **Justification:** 5

### Saturday — Total Score: 29/35

Saturday is the main event. It is the weekend in its purest form. It exists to be enjoyed, and it is enjoyed, universally, without reservation.[^6]

[^6]: Except by those who must work on Saturday, for whom Saturday is merely a Monday that everyone else is enjoying, which is arguably worse than Monday itself. Saturday, for these people, is not a day but an insult.

**Purpose:** 6 | **Distinctiveness:** 6 | **Morale Impact:** 7 | **Cultural Significance:** 5 | **Justification:** 5

### Sunday — Total Score: 23/35

Sunday is complicated. It begins well and ends badly. It carries within it the seed of Monday, which grows steadily throughout the day until, by evening, Sunday has become merely Monday's anteroom. Sunday afternoon, specifically, is a melancholy so universal that entire genres of music have been written about it. Nonetheless, Sunday has religious significance, cultural weight, and the full English breakfast, which together constitute an adequate justification.[^7]

[^7]: The full English breakfast is not, strictly speaking, a theological argument. But it is the closest thing to a religious experience that many people on Earth have on a Sunday, and I am not prepared to dismiss it.

**Purpose:** 5 | **Distinctiveness:** 4 | **Morale Impact:** 4 | **Cultural Significance:** 5 | **Justification:** 5

### Tuesday — Total Score: 11/35

And so we come to Tuesday.

Tuesday has no purpose. It is not the beginning (Monday), not the middle (Wednesday), not the end of anything (Friday). It occupies no structural position of significance. It is not a landmark. It is not a turning point. It is the day after Monday, which means it carries all of Monday's residual misery with none of Monday's grim sense of occasion.

Tuesday has no distinctiveness. Nothing happens on Tuesday that could not happen on any other day. No major holidays fall reliably on Tuesday.[^8] No cultural traditions centre on Tuesday. No one has ever said, "Thank God it's Tuesday," and if they have, they were being sarcastic or had confused it with Friday.

[^8]: Election Day in several Earth democracies falls on a Tuesday, which is widely considered to be one of the reasons voter turnout is so low. People cannot summon enthusiasm on a Tuesday. They can barely summon consciousness. Asking them to also summon civic engagement is optimistic to the point of delusion.

Tuesday has a devastating morale impact. It arrives after Monday, which means you have already endured one bad day only to be confronted with another that is, if anything, worse, because at least Monday had the decency to be Obviously Terrible. Tuesday is Subtly Terrible. It is the day that most closely resembles nothing. It is beige. It is the empty space between days that matter.

Tuesday has no cultural significance beyond being the day people forget. "What day is it?" "Tuesday." "Oh." That "oh" is Tuesday's entire cultural legacy.

Tuesday has no justification. None. I have searched. I have consulted colleagues across seventeen departments. I have run statistical analyses, historical surveys, and one rather desperate divination attempt involving tea leaves and a borrowed tortoise.[^9] There is no reason for Tuesday to exist.

[^9]: The tortoise, whose name was Gerald, was uncooperative. The tea leaves spelled out something that might have been "pointless" or might have been "pineapple." Neither interpretation helps Tuesday's case.

**Purpose:** 2 | **Distinctiveness:** 2 | **Morale Impact:** 2 | **Cultural Significance:** 3 | **Justification:** 2

## The Verdict

Tuesday scores 11 out of a possible 35. This is not merely the lowest score in the survey; it is a score so low that it raises questions about whether Tuesday was included in the original week design or was added later, perhaps by accident, perhaps as padding, perhaps because someone miscounted and was too embarrassed to correct the error.

A six-day week would function perfectly well. Better, arguably. The loss of Tuesday would shorten the working week, improve average morale, and remove from the calendar the one day that contributes nothing and costs everyone a full twenty-four hours of tepid, purposeless existence.

That Tuesday persists is not evidence of design. It is evidence of inertia. It is the cosmic equivalent of a meeting that no one cancelled because everyone assumed someone else had a reason for it being there.[^10]

[^10]: I have proposed, formally, to the Department of Temporal Affairs, that Tuesday be removed from the standard week and the resulting gap left empty as a warning to other days that underperformance has consequences. The proposal was received on a Tuesday. It has not been acted upon. I am not surprised.
