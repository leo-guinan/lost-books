# Foreword

### by Professor Reg Nullify
### *Chair of Applied Conditions, University of Maximegalon*

---

It is customary, when writing a foreword to a colleague's work, to offer a few measured words of endorsement — something along the lines of "this is an important contribution to the field" or "the author brings fresh insight to an enduring question." One is expected to be generous but restrained. Warm but not effusive. Supportive in precisely the way that signals to the academic community that one has read the book, or at least the table of contents, and found neither cause for alarm nor grounds for litigation.

I find myself in the unusual position of being unable to offer such conventional reassurance.

Not because the work is bad. It is, I must concede, devastatingly good. Professor Colluphil has produced a piece of Conditions analysis so thorough, so methodical, and so relentlessly well-argued that its conclusions are essentially impossible to refute. This is precisely the problem.

When Oolon first came to me with what he described as "a few observations about divine project management," I assumed he meant it in the usual academic sense — a paper, perhaps, to be presented at the Annual Symposium on Applied Conditions and then politely filed in the same cabinet where we keep the minutes from the committee on whether to form a committee.[^1] I did not expect him to return eighteen months later with a manuscript that, when stacked, measured the exact height of my desk lamp, and which contained within it a logical proof so airtight that the Almighty Himself would later cite it as grounds for non-existence.

[^1]: The committee voted 4-3 in favour of forming a committee. The committee then voted 3-4 against meeting. The matter was referred to a third committee, which has not yet been formed.

I have read this book three times. The first time, I laughed. The second time, I felt a creeping unease. The third time, I locked my office door and sat quietly for some while, staring at a patch of wall where I had always assumed a window might eventually be installed, and contemplated the nature of accountability.

The argument, in essence, is this: if the universe were a project — and Colluphil makes a compelling case that this is exactly what it is — then it would receive a decidedly unfavorable review. The specifications were ambitious, the execution was patchy, the documentation was almost entirely absent, and the end user was consulted at no point during the development process.[^2] The chapter on gravity alone should be required reading for anyone who has ever fallen down and thought, "This seems like it could have been handled differently."

[^2]: There are those who claim that certain prophets were consulted. Colluphil addresses this in Chapter 5, noting that "consultation" conducted via burning shrubbery and stone tablets does not meet the minimum standards established by the Galactic Consumer Rights Act (Section 47b: "Communication shall be conducted in a medium the recipient can reply to without specialist equipment or unusual courage").

I must also address the controversy that will inevitably surround Chapter 7, "The Tuesday Problem." I have been asked by several colleagues whether I endorse Colluphil's position on Tuesdays. Let me be clear: I do not endorse it, I do not reject it, and I do not wish to discuss it further. What I will say is that since reading that chapter, I have found Tuesdays noticeably more difficult to get through, and I hold Colluphil personally responsible for this.[^3]

[^3]: My therapist, Dr. Granz of the Maximegalon Institute for the Quietly Distressed, has confirmed that "Tuesday awareness" is now a recognised condition affecting seventeen members of the faculty. Treatment consists of not thinking about it, which, as anyone who has been told not to think about something can confirm, is essentially impossible.

Some will ask whether this book is an attack on faith. I would argue it is nothing of the sort. It is, rather, a performance review — conducted in good faith, with appropriate documentation, and a clear scoring rubric. If the Subject of the review finds the conclusions unflattering, He is, as Colluphil notes in the conclusion, welcome to submit a rebuttal through the appropriate channels.[^4]

[^4]: The appropriate channel is a form available from the Department of Applied Conditions, Room 12b, third corridor on the left past the fire extinguisher that doesn't work. The form must be submitted in triplicate, in person, during office hours, which are Thursdays between 2:15 and 2:45 PM, excluding bank holidays, days of divine rest, and Tuesdays.

That the Subject has thus far declined to do so is, depending on one's theological disposition, either deeply meaningful or exactly what one would expect from someone who has been getting away with it for approximately fourteen billion years.

I commend this work to you. Read it carefully. Read it with an open mind. And if, upon finishing it, you find yourself staring at a wasp and thinking, "Was this really the best you could do?" — well, you will not be the first, and you certainly will not be the last.

Professor Reg Nullify
Chair of Applied Conditions
University of Maximegalon

*Written on a Tuesday, regrettably.*
