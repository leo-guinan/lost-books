# Chapter 3: The Biological Error

### *In which the entertainment complex is found adjacent to the sewage works, teeth are identified as a planned obsolescence scandal, and the platypus is entered into evidence*

---

If the previous chapters have dealt with physics — the broad, architectural strokes of Creation — then this chapter must turn to the finer details. The biology. The plumbing, as it were.

I use the word "plumbing" advisedly, because it is in the plumbing that the most damning evidence of negligent design is to be found. And I do not merely mean this metaphorically. I mean it anatomically. I mean it *literally.* But we shall come to that.

Let us begin with a general principle. In any well-designed system, related functions are grouped together logically. The kitchen is near the dining room. The bedroom is away from the street. The lavatory is not in the middle of the dance floor.[^1] These are not radical propositions. They are basic spatial planning. They are the *minimum* one expects from anyone claiming to be in the design business.

[^1]: With the notable exception of the infamous Restaurant at the End of the University District on Ursa Minor Beta, which placed the toilets on a rotating platform in the centre of the dining area as an "artistic statement." The restaurant closed after three weeks. The statement, it was generally agreed, had been made.

## The Entertainment-Sewage Problem

I must now address, with as much delicacy as a Conditions analyst can muster, the arrangement of the reproductive and excretory systems in the majority of biological organisms.

They are, not to put too fine a point on it, right next to each other.

In many species, they share infrastructure. In some, they share *the same exit.*[^2]

[^2]: The cloaca, found in birds, reptiles, and amphibians, is a single opening that serves reproductive, urinary, and digestive functions simultaneously. It is the biological equivalent of a combined post office, launderette, and nightclub operating out of the same room. That it works at all is a testament to the resilience of nature. That it was designed this way on purpose is a testament to something else entirely.

This is the equivalent of building a luxury resort and placing the honeymoon suite directly above the sewage treatment plant, with a connecting door.

No competent designer would do this. No planning committee would approve it. If you submitted these blueprints to any regulatory body in the galaxy, they would send them back with a note reading "Please see us at your earliest convenience and bring your qualifications."

And yet here we are.

## The Teeth Situation

Human teeth — and I use humans as the example because they are the species most vocally upset about this, though the issue is widespread — human teeth are designed to last approximately half of the human lifespan.[^3]

[^3]: The average human lives to roughly 75 standard Earth years. The average human tooth begins its campaign of betrayal around year 35-40. This means teeth have a planned obsolescence rate that would make a consumer electronics manufacturer blush, and I remind you that these are people who deliberately make phones that slow down after two years.

This is not a manufacturing defect. This is not an edge case. The teeth are made of a material that dissolves in the presence of the very substances the organism is designed to consume. This is like building a boat out of material that dissolves in water, then acting surprised when it sinks. The teeth are working *exactly as designed.* They were simply designed badly.

Moreover, the replacement system is a scandal. Humans get two sets of teeth — one provisional set for childhood, and one supposedly permanent set that is nothing of the sort. After that: nothing. No third set. No emergency backup. No warranty.

Sharks, by contrast, grow new teeth continuously throughout their lives. Sharks have an essentially infinite dental plan. This means that somewhere in the design process, someone knew how to do teeth properly and *chose not to* for the majority of species.[^4]

[^4]: This is not a failure of imagination. This is preferential treatment. The shark has been given the premium dental package while everyone else is on the basic plan with a six-month waiting period and a deductible that covers nothing. If this were an insurance scheme, there would be a class action lawsuit.

## The Platypus Dossier

I must now discuss the platypus.

I am aware that the platypus is frequently cited in theological discussions, usually by people who think it is either evidence of God's playfulness or God's sense of humour. I reject both interpretations. The platypus is neither playful nor humorous. The platypus is evidence of what happens when a project is being finished on a Friday afternoon and the designer has mentally already left for the weekend.[^5]

[^5]: The Friday Afternoon Theory of Creation is not original to this work. It was first proposed by Professor Whelk of the Minor Conditions Department (now defunct, for reasons unrelated to this theory but related to a dispute about biscuit allocation that I am not at liberty to discuss). Whelk observed that the platypus exhibits all the hallmarks of work done in haste by someone who has stopped caring: the bill of a duck, the tail of a beaver, the venomous spur of something that should not have a venomous spur, and the reproductive system of a creature that appears to have been assembled from the parts bin after the good components had already been used.

Consider the specification sheet:

- **Body:** Mammal (fur, warm-blooded, produces milk)
- **Mouth:** Duck (bill, no teeth in adulthood)
- **Feet:** Webbed (aquatic, semi-aquatic)
- **Reproduction:** Lays eggs (reptile? bird? unclear)
- **Defence:** Venomous ankle spur (why?)
- **Detection:** Electroreception in the bill (impressive, but deeply suspicious)
- **Habitat:** Australia (a continent which is itself Exhibit A in any case against competent biological planning)[^6]

[^6]: Australia contains: the most venomous snake, the most venomous jellyfish, the most venomous octopus, the most venomous spider, spiders the size of dinner plates that are NOT the most venomous, a bird that can disembowel you with its feet, a tree that causes pain so severe victims have been known to seek death, and a mammal that lays eggs. Australia is not a continent. It is a warning.

This is not a coherent organism. This is a creature designed by a committee that had lost the original specification sheet and was working from memory, badly, while arguing about something else.

## The Giraffe's Laryngeal Nerve

If the platypus is a Friday afternoon, the giraffe's laryngeal nerve is a Monday morning after a long weekend during which several key decisions were made without consulting the relevant department.

The recurrent laryngeal nerve connects the brain to the larynx. In a sensibly designed giraffe, this nerve would travel directly from one to the other — a distance of perhaps a few inches. Instead, it travels from the brain, *all the way down the neck,* loops around the aorta near the heart, and then travels *all the way back up the neck* to the larynx. A journey of approximately fifteen feet to cover a distance of inches.[^7]

[^7]: This is the navigational equivalent of driving from your house to your next-door neighbour's house by way of another city. It is the routing decision of someone who does not have to pay for the petrol.

The explanation, from an evolutionary perspective, is perfectly clear: the nerve was inherited from fish, in which the route made sense, and as the neck elongated over millions of years, the nerve simply got longer rather than being rerouted. This is what happens when there is no design review process. This is what happens when you never go back and refactor.

In software development, this is called *technical debt.* In biology, it is called *how things are.* In a Conditions analysis, it is called *evidence.*

## The Knee

I will be brief about the knee because the knee does not deserve more than a paragraph and arguably does not deserve even that. The human knee is a load-bearing joint designed to support the full weight of an upright biped for seven or eight decades. It consists of bones, cartilage, ligaments, and a fluid-filled sac, all of which begin deteriorating almost immediately upon use. It bends in one direction only. It is vulnerable to injury from virtually every angle. It makes noises.[^8]

[^8]: The medical term for knee noises is "crepitus," which sounds like something that should be treated with antibiotics but is in fact considered normal. The knee is making these noises because it is falling apart. We are told this is fine. It is not fine.

The knee is not a joint. It is a *compromise* between several incompatible requirements, none of which have been fully met.

## Conclusion

The biological evidence is, I must confess, the most difficult to assess dispassionately. Physics can be discussed abstractly. Gravity can be analysed with equations. But when one is discussing the placement of vital organs, the structural integrity of load-bearing joints, and the existence of an animal that is simultaneously a mammal, a bird, a reptile, and a mistake, one's professional detachment begins to fray.

I have attempted, throughout this chapter, to maintain the measured tone appropriate to a work of Conditions analysis. If I have occasionally lapsed into what might be characterised as exasperation, I can only say that the subject matter invites it.[^9]

[^9]: I wrote the first draft of this chapter sitting in my dentist's waiting room, which I concede may have affected my objectivity. The dentist subsequently informed me that I needed two fillings and a crown. The bill was 300 Altairian dollars. My exasperation, I feel, was adequately justified on multiple grounds.
