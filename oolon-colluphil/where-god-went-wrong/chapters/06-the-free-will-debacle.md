# Chapter 6: The Free Will Debacle

### *In which the policy of giving beings free will and then becoming upset when they use it is examined, and the analogy of the cat and the vase is developed at length*

---

Free will is, by most theological accounts, a gift. It is the ability to choose, to decide, to act according to one's own judgement rather than according to a pre-written script. It is, we are told, what separates conscious beings from automatons, what makes moral choices meaningful, and what gives existence its peculiar, anxious flavour.

It is also, by every available measure, the single worst management decision in the history of Creation.

## The Gift

Let us be clear about what free will is, in practical terms. It is the delegation of decision-making authority to beings who have no training, no experience, no oversight, and no particular aptitude for making decisions. It is, to use a corporate analogy, like giving every employee in the company full administrative access to every system on their first day and then stepping out for an indefinitely long lunch.[^1]

[^1]: The lunch, in this case, has lasted approximately fourteen billion years. This is a long lunch even by the standards of the Frogstar World B civil service, where lunch breaks are famously measured in geological epochs. At some point, one stops waiting for management to return from lunch and begins to suspect that management is not coming back.

The beings to whom this gift was given are, let us remember, the same beings who:

- Cannot agree on a temperature at which a room is comfortable
- Have started wars over the correct way to prepare a hot beverage
- Routinely walk into glass doors
- Invented the snooze button, which exists solely to enable the repeated, deliberate failure to get out of bed[^2]

[^2]: The snooze button is, I would argue, the purest expression of free will ever devised. It is the freedom to choose, every nine minutes, to do the wrong thing. It is free will distilled to its essence: the ability to make a decision you know to be bad, in full knowledge of its consequences, and to make it again immediately afterwards. If God wanted evidence that free will was a mistake, He need look no further than the bedside table.

These are the beings entrusted with the power of autonomous choice.

## The Cat and the Vase

The situation is best illustrated by analogy.

Imagine you have a cat. The cat is, as cats are, a creature of independent will, moderate intelligence, and comprehensive indifference to the consequences of its actions. Now imagine you place, on a high shelf in the cat's presence, an expensive vase. The cat looks at the vase. The vase sits on the shelf. You know what is going to happen. The cat knows what is going to happen. The vase, if it had opinions, would know what is going to happen.

The cat pushes the vase off the shelf. The vase shatters.

Now: who is responsible?

The cat? The cat is a cat. It pushed the vase because it is a cat and that is what cats do. It did not push the vase out of malice. It did not push the vase to make a philosophical point. It pushed the vase because the vase was there and pushing things off shelves is, for a cat, a calling.[^3]

[^3]: Studies of cat behaviour have confirmed that cats push things off shelves with a consistency that suggests not mischief but *vocation.* The cat is not being naughty. The cat is fulfilling its purpose. Blaming the cat for pushing the vase is like blaming water for being wet. The water did not choose to be wet. The cat did not choose to be a creature that pushes things off shelves. These are design decisions, and the designer is responsible for them.

The person who placed the vase on the shelf? Yes. Obviously. Unambiguously. If you put a breakable object within reach of a creature you know will break it, the ensuing breakage is not the creature's fault. It is yours. You had the information. You had the power to prevent it. You chose to set up the conditions for failure and then — and this is the crucial part — you *watched it happen.*

This is free will.

God made beings with the capacity for every conceivable wrong decision. He placed them in an environment full of things to make wrong decisions about. He gave them impulses, desires, insecurities, and a prefrontal cortex that doesn't fully develop until age 25[^4] — well after most of the worst decisions have already been made. And then, according to most theological traditions, He expressed *disappointment* when the wrong decisions were made.

[^4]: Twenty-five. The part of the brain responsible for risk assessment, long-term planning, and impulse control is not fully operational until a quarter of the way through the average human lifespan. This means that every decision made before age 25 — including career choices, romantic commitments, tattoo selections, and haircuts — is being made with incomplete equipment. This is not a design choice one makes if one is hoping for good outcomes. This is a design choice one makes if one is running an experiment to see what happens, and the answer is: mostly regret.

This is the cat and the vase. Except the cat is all of conscious life, the vase is everything, and the person who put the vase on the shelf is omniscient, which means He didn't just *suspect* the cat would push it — He *knew.*

## The Entrapment Argument

In law, there is a concept known as entrapment. Entrapment occurs when an authority figure induces someone to commit an offence they would not otherwise have committed, and then punishes them for it. It is considered, in every legal system I am aware of, to be fundamentally unjust.[^5]

[^5]: Except in the legal system of Blagulon Kappa, where entrapment is not only legal but is the primary method of law enforcement. The police on Blagulon Kappa spend most of their time leaving expensive items unattended in public places and then arresting anyone who picks them up. Crime rates are high. Trust is low. Everyone is miserable. I mention this only because it is the closest existing parallel to the free will situation.

The free will arrangement is, I would argue, entrapment on a cosmic scale.

Consider: God creates beings with the ability to sin. He places them in an environment containing sin. He gives them desires that incline them toward sin. He gives them a holy text that is vague about which things are sins and which are merely discouraged.[^6] He then observes as they sin and responds with floods, plagues, fire, brimstone, and — in His more restrained moments — stern disapproval communicated via intermediary.

[^6]: The distinction between "sin" and "merely discouraged" has been the subject of more theological debate than any other question in history, which is itself evidence of a specification problem. If the rules were clear, there would be no debate. The rules are not clear. There is debate. The debate has lasted millennia and shows no sign of resolution. In a well-managed project, ambiguous requirements are clarified by the project owner. In this project, the project owner has been unreachable since approximately forever.

This is not justice. This is a setup.

## The Omniscience Paradox

The problem is compounded — immeasurably, irrecoverably compounded — by the claim of omniscience.

If God knows everything, He knew, before creating any being, every choice that being would make. Every sin, every crime, every poor decision, every regrettable haircut — all of it was known in advance. Every soul that would be condemned was known to be destined for condemnation before it was created.

This means God created beings *specifically to fail.* Not accidentally. Not through lack of foresight. Deliberately, knowingly, with full awareness of the outcome, He created beings whose choices He had already foreseen and whose punishment He had already determined.[^7]

[^7]: There are theological responses to this paradox, most of which involve redefining "omniscience" to mean something other than "knowing everything" or redefining "free will" to mean something other than "the ability to choose." These redefinitions are ingenious, creative, and — I say this with professional respect — entirely unconvincing. You cannot claim to know everything and then act surprised. You cannot claim to have given someone a choice and then punish them for choosing. You can do one or the other. You cannot do both. This is not theology. This is logic. And logic, unlike burning bushes, does not go away when you stop looking at it.

This is not giving someone a test. This is giving someone a test you already know they will fail, having designed them to fail it, and then marking them harshly.

In any educational institution, this would result in a formal review. In any workplace, it would result in a tribunal. In the universe, it is called "the plan," and we are expected to find it comforting.

I do not find it comforting.

I find it exactly as reassuring as a cat finds a shelf full of vases — which is to say, it makes me want to push something off it.
