# Chapter 2: The Gravity Miscalculation

### *In which DOWN is revealed as an arbitrary and frankly lazy decision, and the committee meeting where UP was considered and rejected is reconstructed from surviving minutes*

---

Of all the fundamental forces, gravity is the one most people feel they understand. Things fall down. This is obvious, intuitive, and so deeply embedded in the experience of being alive that questioning it feels faintly absurd, like asking why water is wet or why the photocopier in Room 12b has never once worked properly.[^1]

[^1]: The photocopier in Room 12b has been broken since the department moved into the building in Year of the Bewildered Gnu. A repair request was submitted. The repair request was lost. A second repair request was submitted regarding the lost repair request. This too was lost. The photocopier remains, in the corner, humming gently, as a monument to institutional persistence in the face of comprehensive failure.

But "obvious" and "well-designed" are not synonyms. Many things are obvious that are also, upon inspection, completely arbitrary. Driving on the left side of the road is obvious if you are from certain planets. It is not, however, the result of rigorous engineering analysis. It is the result of someone, long ago, making a choice and everyone else being too committed to change it.

Gravity is the driving-on-the-left of the fundamental forces.

## The Down Problem

Let me state the issue plainly: things fall *down.* Not up, not sideways,[^2] not in a gentle spiral that allows you time to consider your options — *down.* Straight down. Always down. With a commitment to down-ness that borders on the fanatical.

[^2]: Sideways is discussed at length in Appendix C of this work, which, due to a printing error, appears to have been bound into a completely different book — a romance novel entitled *Passion on Procyon IV.* Readers who have purchased that book expecting a love story and instead received a 4,000-word analysis of lateral gravitational theory are invited to contact Conditions Press for a refund, though they should be aware that the refund process also moves sideways, which is to say, it never quite arrives.

The question is: why down?

The answer, I am forced to conclude after extensive analysis, is: no particular reason.

Down was chosen because it was simple. It required only one direction, one rule, one universal constant. It was, to use a project management term, the path of least resistance — which is ironic, because gravity *is* the path of least resistance. The design principle and the design outcome are the same thing, which is either deeply elegant or deeply lazy, and I know which way I'm leaning.[^3]

[^3]: Down, obviously. We are all, always, leaning down. This is the problem.

**Item 1: Selection of Gravitational Direction**

*The Chair opened the meeting by noting that a direction was needed for the gravitational force and that the current situation — in which matter simply sat wherever it was put, like a cat on a warm laptop — was unsustainable.*

*Three proposals were put forward:*

**Proposal A: DOWN**
Submitted by: Unknown
Rationale: Simple. One direction. Everything goes the same way. Easy to implement.
Concerns raised: Arbitrary. Why down? Why not some other direction?
Response to concerns: "Because it's down. That's what down means."[^6]

[^6]: This is a tautology, which is a type of argument in which the conclusion is contained in the premise, like a snake eating its own tail. It is not a valid form of reasoning. It is, however, extremely common in committee meetings, performance reviews, and theology.

**Proposal B: UP**
Submitted by: Also Unknown
Rationale: Would make the experience of existing considerably more exciting. Beings would float upwards unless held down. Architecture would be fascinating. Hats would be unnecessary.
Concerns raised: Impractical. Beings would float off the surface of their planets and into the vacuum of space, where they would die. Also, the hat industry would collapse, with significant economic consequences.
Response to concerns: "Could we give them very heavy shoes?"
Resolution: Rejected, 4-1, on the grounds that it would "cause too many problems," a phrase which, applied to UP, is somehow considered decisive, but applied to the entirety of biological existence on Earth, is apparently just how things are.

**Proposal C: SIDEWAYS**
Submitted by: A member who asked not to be named[^7]
Rationale: Novel. Interesting. Would create a universe in which nothing is where you left it and everything slowly migrates towards the nearest wall.
Concerns raised: Multiple. Extensive. Loud.
Response to concerns: The proposer was asked to leave the meeting and not come back.
Resolution: Not voted on. Minutes note: "We do not speak of sideways."

[^7]: The proposer of sideways has been variously identified as a minor deity, a consultant, and — in one tradition — an intern who got into the meeting because the door was open and there were biscuits. The biscuit theory is, I feel, the most plausible. Many of history's most consequential decisions have been made by people who were only in the room because of biscuits.

*The committee voted 4-1 in favour of DOWN. The dissenting vote was not recorded, which is standard practice when the dissenter has been asked to leave.*

## The Consequences of Down

Having chosen down, the designer then implemented it with a thoroughness that can only be described as excessive. Everything falls down. Rain falls down. Apples fall down. People fall down, frequently injuring themselves in the process. Civilisations fall down, metaphorically. Soufflés fall down, both literally and to the great distress of their makers.

The only things that go up are things that are *actively fighting gravity* — birds, aeroplanes, hopes — and they all come down again eventually. Gravity is not just a force; it is a worldview. It is pessimism expressed as physics.[^8]

[^8]: Several of my colleagues have objected to this characterisation, arguing that gravity also holds planets in orbit, keeps atmospheres from drifting off, and generally maintains the structural integrity of the universe. I accept these points. But I note that a prison also provides structure, and we do not typically praise the architect.

## The Strength Problem

There is a further issue, which is that gravity is — and this is the technical term — *pathetically weak.*

Of the four fundamental forces, gravity is by far the weakest. The electromagnetic force is approximately 10^36 times stronger. The strong nuclear force is even stronger than that. Gravity is the runt. The afterthought. The one that was added at the end because someone realised they'd forgotten to include a way for things to stay on the ground.[^9]

[^9]: A magnet the size of a coin can lift a paperclip off a table, thereby defeating the gravitational pull of the *entire planet.* Let that settle in. The gravitational output of a body 12,742 kilometres in diameter, with a mass of approximately 5.97 × 10^24 kilograms, can be overcome by something you got free with a magazine. If this were a performance metric, the planet would be on a formal improvement plan.

And yet, despite being the weakest force, gravity is the one that dominates at large scales. It is the one that shapes galaxies, collapses stars, and creates black holes — objects so gravitationally intense that not even light can escape them, which is what happens when you give a weak force an infinite amount of time and space to overcompensate.

## A Note on Falling

I wish to close this chapter with a personal observation.

I have fallen down stairs, off chairs, over kerbs, and — on one occasion I do not wish to discuss — off a perfectly stationary platform while delivering a lecture on this very subject.[^10] Each time, gravity has been entirely unsympathetic. It has not paused. It has not offered alternatives. It has not, at any point, suggested that perhaps this particular fall could be redirected sideways, or upwards, or converted into a gentle hover while I collected my dignity.

[^10]: The lecture was entitled "Why Down Is Not The Only Option." The timing of the fall was, I must concede, unfortunate. The audience's applause was ambiguous.

This is not the behaviour of a well-designed force. This is the behaviour of a policy implemented by someone who was never going to be subject to it personally.

Down is not a design choice. It is a default setting that was never revisited.

And like all default settings, it tells you far more about the designer than about the design.
