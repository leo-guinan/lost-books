# Chapter 5: The Communication Breakdown

### *In which the customer support infrastructure is audited and found to be significantly worse than that of a cable company, which is saying something*

---

Let us consider, for a moment, the communications strategy of an omniscient, omnipotent, omnipresent being.

Such a being, by definition, knows everything, can do anything, and is everywhere simultaneously. It has, in other words, no technical barriers to communication whatsoever. It could, if it chose, speak directly and clearly to every conscious being in the universe at the same time, in their own language, at a volume and in a format perfectly calibrated to their individual comprehension level. It could send a galaxy-wide email. It could take out an advertisement. It could simply *appear* and say, "Hello, I'm God, here are the rules, any questions?"

Instead, what the historical record shows is this: burning shrubbery, stone tablets, dreams, vaguely worded prophecies, and an almost pathological reliance on intermediaries — most of whom were, to put it generously, not professionally trained in message delivery.[^1]

[^1]: The typical prophet was a person of no particular qualification who was going about their day — tending sheep, fishing, wandering in deserts — when they were suddenly and without warning given a message of cosmic importance and told to disseminate it. This is the communication equivalent of handing the launch codes to someone you met at a bus stop because they happened to be standing there. It is not a strategy. It is a liability.

## The Burning Bush Incident

The burning bush is perhaps the most frequently cited example of divine communication, and it is worth examining in detail because it encapsulates every problem with the entire approach.

The medium: a shrub, on fire, which is not consumed by the flames. The recipient: a shepherd, alone, in a desert. The message: extensive, detailed, and of considerable legal complexity. The witnesses: none.[^2]

[^2]: Zero witnesses. An omnipotent being chose to deliver the foundational legal code for an entire civilisation to a single person standing alone in a desert, with no recording equipment, no corroborating testimony, and no way to verify the account beyond "the bush was on fire and it talked to me." In any court in the galaxy, this testimony would be filed under "concerning" and the witness would be offered a glass of water and a referral.

Let us count the problems:

1. **The medium is frightening.** A bush that is on fire but not burning is not a reassuring sight. It is the kind of thing that, in most circumstances, would cause a sensible person to walk briskly in the other direction. Beginning a communication with something that triggers the fight-or-flight response is, from a user-experience perspective, a questionable design choice.

2. **The medium is non-replicable.** You cannot forward a burning bush. You cannot CC someone on it. You cannot save it to your desktop and refer back to it later. It is a one-time, one-audience, zero-documentation communication event.[^3]

3. **The audience is a single individual.** This individual must then relay the message to others, using only the spoken word and whatever their memory can retain. This is a game of galactic telephone, and anyone who has played telephone knows that the message "Please bring milk" becomes "Freeze the silk" by the third person.

[^3]: Modern divine communication, if it were to occur today, would presumably involve an email, a Slack message, and a follow-up meeting with an agenda. The email would say "Important: New guidelines attached." The Slack message would say "Hey, did you see my email?" The follow-up meeting would last forty-five minutes and accomplish nothing, but at least there would be a record.

## The Stone Tablet Problem

The stone tablets represent an upgrade from the burning bush in that they are at least *physical.* They can be carried. They can be stored. They can be shown to people as evidence that something happened.

They are also made of stone, which is heavy, fragile when dropped, and impossible to edit. They are version 1.0 with no update mechanism.[^4]

[^4]: Several theological traditions record that the first set of tablets was, in fact, broken and had to be replaced with a second set. This is not an encouraging start. The definitive moral code for all of humanity was delivered on a medium so impractical that it was destroyed within days of receipt. If this were a courier service, it would have a one-star rating and a comments section full of people writing in capital letters.

In an age when even the most primitive civilisations had access to papyrus, parchment, and ink — media that are lighter, more portable, and considerably easier to reproduce — the choice of stone tablets suggests either a profound misunderstanding of the target audience's logistical capabilities or an aesthetic preference for drama over functionality. Both are concerning.

## The Prayer Ticket System

The other direction of communication — from beings to God — is managed through what I can only describe as a prayer ticket system, and it is the worst-performing customer service operation I have ever encountered, and I have dealt with the Sirius Cybernetics Corporation.[^5]

[^5]: The Sirius Cybernetics Corporation's customer service department has a hold time of approximately six hours, a resolution rate of 12%, and an automated message that says "Your call is important to us" in a tone that makes it clear your call is not important to them. God's customer service is worse. God's customer service has no hold music, no estimated wait time, no ticket number, and no way to confirm that your request was received, is being processed, or even entered the system. The Sirius Cybernetics Corporation at least has the decency to *pretend* they're listening.

Here is how prayer works, as far as anyone can determine:

1. The supplicant formulates a request
2. The supplicant transmits the request via internal monologue, often with hands clasped and eyes closed, which is the universal posture of someone who is not entirely confident their message is being received
3. The request enters... something. A queue, presumably. Perhaps a void.
4. A response is generated. Or not. There is no way to tell.
5. If the desired outcome occurs, the prayer is deemed answered. If it does not, the prayer is deemed "answered in a way that is not yet understood," which is the theological equivalent of "your feedback has been noted"[^6]

[^6]: "Your feedback has been noted" is a phrase used across the galaxy by organisations that have no intention of acting on your feedback. It is the corporate equivalent of a smile that doesn't reach the eyes. It means "We have heard you. We do not care. Please go away." That the Almighty has essentially adopted this as His standard response protocol is, I feel, significant.

The response rate is, statistically, indistinguishable from chance. Prayers for recovery from illness succeed at approximately the same rate as not praying for recovery from illness. Prayers for good weather succeed at the rate weather would have occurred anyway. Prayers for the smiting of enemies are fulfilled only in cases where the enemy was going to have a bad day regardless.[^7]

[^7]: A comprehensive study conducted by the Department of Applied Theology at the University of Maximegalon surveyed 4.7 billion prayer events across seventeen planets and found a positive correlation of 0.003 between prayer and outcome — a figure so low that the lead researcher described it as "statistically meaningless but theologically hilarious." The study was never published because the journal's editor was struck by lightning during the peer review process, which was cited by some as evidence of divine displeasure and by others as evidence of precisely the kind of arbitrary, poorly-targeted response system the study was critiquing.

## The Comparison to Cable Companies

I have been asked by my editor whether comparing divine communication to cable company customer service is "perhaps a bit strong." I would argue it is, if anything, too generous.

A cable company:
- Has a phone number you can call
- Employs people whose job it is to respond to you
- Maintains records of your previous interactions
- Occasionally fixes the problem
- Has a complaints procedure

God:
- Has no phone number
- Employs no customer service staff[^8]
- Maintains no records that anyone can access
- May or may not fix the problem; impossible to determine
- Has no complaints procedure whatsoever

[^8]: Some have argued that priests, rabbis, imams, and other religious figures constitute a form of customer service staff. I would counter that these are unpaid volunteers with no direct line to management, no access to the system, and no authority to resolve complaints. They are, at best, an FAQ page. A well-meaning, often sincere, occasionally helpful FAQ page — but an FAQ page nonetheless.

The cable company, for all its faults, is at least *trying.* It is trying badly, with insufficient staff and outdated systems, but the *intent* to communicate is there. The divine communication strategy does not even rise to this level. It is not a system that works poorly. It is the absence of a system, dressed up in mystery and called "faith."

## A Modest Proposal

If the Almighty wished to communicate effectively — and this is a large "if," given the evidence — the following minimum standards would need to be met:

1. A clear, unambiguous initial message, delivered to all beings simultaneously
2. A mechanism for two-way communication that does not rely on internal monologue
3. Response times measured in hours, not millennia
4. A tracking system so supplicants can monitor the status of their requests
5. An escalation path for urgent matters

These are not unreasonable expectations. They are the bare minimum for any service provider. That they have not been met — that they have not even been *attempted* — tells us everything we need to know about the communication priorities of the management.[^9]

[^9]: I submitted these recommendations formally to the University's Department of Applied Theology, who forwarded them "upward." I was not told how far upward. I have not received a response. The silence continues to be, as they say, deafening. Also, normal.
