# Chapter 4: The Speed of Light — The Universe's Bandwidth Cap

---

The speed of light is 299,792,458 metres per second.

This sounds impressive. It is not.

Oh, certainly, by the parochial standards of everyday experience, it is fast. Faster than a Dentrassi chef's knife. Faster than bad news. Faster than the speed at which a Vogon poetry recital empties a room, which was previously thought to be the upper limit of velocity¹. Light moves quickly. No one disputes this.

What I dispute — what anyone with a map of the universe and a basic understanding of scale should dispute — is that 299,792,458 metres per second is *fast enough*.

The nearest star to Earth's sun is Proxima Centauri. It is 4.24 light-years away. This means that even traveling at the absolute maximum speed the universe permits — the theoretical ceiling, the hard cap, the cosmic speed limit posted on every stretch of spacetime — it would take over four years to reach the nearest interesting thing in any direction.

Four years. To reach the *neighbours*.

The galaxy is 100,000 light-years across. The observable universe is 93 billion light-years in diameter. And the fastest anything can move is a speed that makes the commute to the next star system take longer than most university degrees². The universe is, in effect, a very large museum in which all the exhibits are roped off and the gift shop is closed.

This is not physics. This is a bandwidth cap.

God, in this analogy — and I am increasingly convinced it is not an analogy — is an Internet Service Provider. He has built an enormous network. He has filled it with extraordinary content — stars, nebulae, pulsars, black holes, alien civilisations, all the wonders of creation. And then He has throttled the connection so severely that you can barely access any of it. You can *see* it. You can look up at the night sky and observe billions of points of light, each representing something magnificent and unreachable. It is the cosmic equivalent of a streaming service that lets you browse the catalogue but buffers forever when you try to watch anything³.

"But," the theologian protests, "the speed of light is a fundamental constant! It is woven into the fabric of spacetime! It cannot be otherwise!"

Cannot? Or *will not*?

Because let us be clear: there is no *physical* reason for the speed limit. There is nothing to hit. Space is, by definition, empty. It is the emptiest thing there is. The speed of light is not a safety measure. It is not protecting anyone from cosmic collisions in the vast, echoing void between stars. It is an *arbitrary limit* imposed on a system where no limit is necessary⁴.

If the speed of light were the speed limit on a motorway, and you asked the authorities why the limit was set where it was, and they said "because that's just how fast things go," you would not accept this answer. You would demand to see the engineering assessment. You would want to know about the road conditions, the accident statistics, the traffic flow analysis. And when they told you that the road was perfectly straight, infinitely long, and completely empty in every direction, and the speed limit was still 70, you would begin to suspect that the authorities were not interested in your travel experience.

The speed of light is, I submit, a policy decision disguised as a natural law.

And it gets worse. Because the speed of light is not merely a limit on travel — it is a limit on *information*. Nothing can travel faster than light. Not matter, not energy, not data. This means that when you look at a star that is one hundred light-years away, you are seeing it as it was one hundred years ago. You are not observing the universe. You are observing the universe's *history*⁵. The night sky is not a window. It is an archive. And like all archives, it is out of date.

This creates a situation of magnificent absurdity. The universe is full of things happening — stars are being born, civilisations are rising, supernovae are detonating in cascades of unimaginable beauty — and we cannot see any of it in real time. We are watching the universe on delay. We are, cosmically speaking, watching the replay. The live broadcast is happening right now, all around us, and we will not receive it for decades, centuries, or millennia, depending on how far away it is.

Imagine attending a concert where the sound reaches you forty years after the musicians play. Imagine reading a newspaper that reports events from before you were born. Imagine a telephone system where you speak and your voice arrives at the other end after everyone involved has died. This is the communications infrastructure of the universe. This is what God built. This is, according to theologians, *the best possible system*⁶.

The theological defence of the speed of light typically invokes the argument that limitations breed creativity. That constraints are necessary for growth. That if everything were accessible immediately, nothing would have value.

This is the argument of someone who has never waited four years for a file to download.

I will concede that constraints can, in certain contexts, inspire innovation. The sonnet is a constrained form. The haiku is a constrained form. The hyperspace bypass is a constrained form, in the sense that it constrains you to not having a planet anymore. But there is a difference between a creative constraint and an infrastructure failure, and the speed of light is very firmly the latter⁷.

Moreover, the limit is curiously selective. Light travels at 299,792,458 metres per second in a vacuum. In water, it slows down. In glass, it slows further. In a diamond, it crawls at a mere 124,000 kilometres per second. The speed limit, in other words, is variable — it changes based on the medium. This is like having a motorway speed limit that changes depending on the colour of your car. It is arbitrary. It is inconsistent. It is, at best, poorly administered⁸.

And then — and this is the detail that elevates the whole thing from mere negligence to what I can only describe as deliberate provocation — the universe is *expanding*. Space itself is stretching. Galaxies at the edge of the observable universe are receding from us faster than the speed of light, not because they are moving through space faster than light, but because the space between us is inflating. This means there are galaxies we can see but will never, ever reach. The light from them reached us, but we cannot reach them. They are visible but inaccessible. They are in the catalogue but out of stock. Permanently⁹.

The universe is, in summary, very large, very beautiful, and very nearly impossible to experience. It is a library with no doors. A gallery with roped-off exhibits. A restaurant where you can read the menu but the kitchen closed four billion years ago.

The speed of light is not a law of physics. It is a design choice. And it is, like so many of God's design choices, one that prioritises aesthetics over functionality, mystery over access, and the experience of looking over the experience of *arriving*.

I have submitted a formal speed increase request to the relevant cosmic authorities.

I do not expect a response. The response, after all, would be limited to the speed of light.
