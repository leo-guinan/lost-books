# Foreword: A Note to My Critics

### by Oolon Colluphil

---

I have been informed, by a great many people, that the publication of *Where God Went Wrong* was irresponsible, dangerous, and theologically unconscionable. I have received four hundred and twelve letters of formal complaint, seventeen death threats¹, three attempted excommunications², and one quite strongly-worded fruit basket.

I wish to address all of my critics simultaneously, as it saves time and they are, broadly speaking, making the same error.

You are wrong.

Not wrong in the interesting, productive sense — the kind of wrongness that leads to discovery and growth. Wrong in the way that a man who insists the building is not on fire while standing in the building which is, observably, on fire, is wrong. Wrong with conviction. Wrong with footnotes. Wrong in peer-reviewed journals. The most impressive wrongness in the galaxy, and I include in this assessment the Belcerebon people of Kakrafoon, who were once so spectacularly wrong about the direction of gravity that their entire civilisation fell upward and was never seen again.

Since publication, I have been called a "menace to theology"³, a "vandal of the sacred," and — in one particularly memorable editorial in the *Sidreal Daily Mentioner* — "the worst thing to happen to faith since evidence." I have framed that one. It hangs above my desk at the University of Maximegalon, where I now hold a tenured position, which means you cannot get rid of me. I want to be very clear about that. *Tenured*. I am here permanently. Like the common cold. Like regret. Like the universe itself, I will be around long after everyone has stopped enjoying me⁴.

Several of my critics have suggested that I am unqualified to assess the works of the Almighty. This is a curious objection. One does not need a degree in architecture to observe that a building has fallen down. One does not need to have designed a bridge to notice that it collapses when you walk on it. And one most certainly does not need to be God to observe that the human knee is an insult to engineering⁵.

The Reverend Halfrunt of Betelgeuse wrote a particularly lengthy rebuttal in which he argued that my criticisms were "born of insufficient faith." I would like to point out that if a restaurant serves you a meal that is, by any reasonable measure, terrible — if the soup is cold, the bread is stale, and the main course is actively hostile — the correct response is not to have more faith in the chef. The correct response is to send it back. This book is me sending it back. Again. With additional notes.

Others have argued that I have no right to judge divine works because I cannot understand the "Grand Design." To them I say: if there is a Grand Design, it is the kind of Grand Design that would get you expelled from any reputable design school in the galaxy. It is the kind of Grand Design where the blueprint was clearly drawn during a lunch break, on a napkin, possibly by someone who had already had quite a lot of the Ol' Janx Spirit⁶.

I have also been accused of arrogance. This I reject entirely. Arrogance is an unjustified sense of superiority. My sense of superiority is *entirely* justified. I have data. I have charts. I have the appendix — both the organ (useless, see Chapter 2) and the section at the back of this book (devastating, see Appendix A).

To those who read *Where God Went Wrong* and wrote to tell me that I had "said everything that needed saying" — I appreciate your support, but you were, regrettably, optimistic. We had barely scratched the surface. The divine catalogue of errors is so vast, so comprehensive, so magnificently thorough in its inadequacy, that one book could never contain it. A library could not contain it. The Library of Maximegalon — which contains every book ever written, every book that will ever be written, and several books that have actively refused to be written — struggles to contain it⁷.

So here we are again. I did not want to write this book. I wanted to retire to a small planet somewhere in the vicinity of Betelgeuse and spend my remaining years in quiet contemplation of how everything is terrible. But the mistakes kept coming. They demanded documentation. They *insisted* upon it.

I have tenure now. I have nothing to lose but my patience, and I lost that somewhere around Chapter 4 of the last book.

Let us begin.

—Oolon Colluphil
Maximegalon, Year of the Bewildered Donkey

The ligaments alone — four of them, each with its own agenda, none of them communicating with the others — suggest a design committee that never met. One designs for stability, another for flexibility, a third for what can only be described as "creative self-destruction," and the fourth appears to exist solely to make the other three worse. I devoted nine pages to this in Book 1 and received letters arguing I had been "unfair to the knee." Unfair. To the KNEE. The knee that buckles, pops, swells, and eventually requires replacement — as if it were a dishwasher part and not the central structural component of bipedal locomotion. I stand by every word, though admittedly I do so with increasing difficulty, because my own knees have begun their inevitable decline. I trust this lends the argument a certain empirical weight.
