# Chapter 5: Other Sentient Beings — A Survey of Complaints

---

One of the more common criticisms leveled at *Where God Went Wrong* was that it was, in the words of the Most Reverend Halfrunt, "insufferably anthropocentric." I was accused of focusing too narrowly on human biology, human experience, human grievance, as though the rest of the galaxy's sentient beings did not also have a comprehensive list of complaints about the management.

This is a fair criticism. I accept it. I accept it not because my critics are usually right — they are usually wrong, spectacularly and at great length — but because in this singular instance, they have a point. The universe's design failures are not limited to one species. They are, in fact, *galactic* in scope. Every sentient species I have surveyed has its own catalogue of divine incompetence, and many of them make humanity's complaints look quaint¹.

Over the last two years, I have conducted interviews with representatives from forty-seven species across twelve galactic sectors. The methodology was straightforward: I asked each being to describe, in their own terms, the aspect of existence they found most poorly designed. The results were illuminating, depressing, and occasionally unprintable.

I present here a representative sample.

**The Dolphins of Earth**

The dolphins were, characteristically, difficult to interview. Not because they lacked opinions — dolphins have very strong opinions about nearly everything — but because they found the entire premise of the survey beneath them.

"You're only just noticing?" was the approximate translation of the lead dolphin's response, delivered with a series of clicks that my translator interpreted as containing "significant condescension and at least two puns."

The dolphins, it emerged, have been aware of the universe's design flaws for considerably longer than any land-based species. They consider the situation so obvious as to not warrant discussion. When pressed for specifics, they cited the ocean's salinity ("unnecessarily aggressive"), the existence of sharks ("a middle-management solution to a problem that didn't exist"), and the general arrangement of the food chain ("recursive and mean-spirited").

They then performed a synchronised leap that my translator described as "a gesture of dismissive beauty" and declined further comment².

They did, however, leave me with the distinct impression that they know something we don't, and that they find our ignorance amusing in the way that a university professor finds a toddler's attempt at philosophy amusing — endearing, but ultimately not worth engaging with.

**The Mice**

The mice declined to comment.

This is not unusual. The mice always decline to comment. They have been declining to comment for as long as anyone can remember, with a consistency that suggests not reluctance but *policy*. The mice are not unaware. The mice are not unintelligent. The mice are, if the rumours from certain fringe xenobiologists are to be believed, running the entire show³.

When I approached a delegation of mice at the Maximegalon Xenobiological Conference, they regarded me with an expression I can only describe as "amused patience." One of them appeared to take notes. Another seemed to be timing the conversation. A third ate a piece of cheese with an air of deliberate nonchalance that was, frankly, unsettling.

They declined to comment but seemed amused.

This is, I submit, the most terrifying possible response. Outrage I can handle. Disagreement I can address. But amusement — the quiet, knowing amusement of an entity that understands more than you do and has decided not to share — is deeply unnerving. The mice know something. I am increasingly convinced the mice know everything. And whatever they know, it is apparently funny⁴.

**The Vogons**

The Vogons exist.

I feel this requires no further elaboration, but I will elaborate anyway, because the existence of the Vogons is perhaps the single most damning piece of evidence in the entire case against intelligent design.

The Vogons are, by any reasonable assessment, a species that should not exist. They are ugly — not in the interesting, characterful way that certain species are ugly, but in the aggressive, committed way that suggests ugliness was the primary design goal and everything else was secondary. They are bureaucratic to a degree that transcends mere inefficiency and enters the realm of performance art. They write poetry so terrible that it has been classified as a weapon under the Galactic Arms Limitation Treaty⁵.

And yet they persist. They persist with a tenacity that borders on spite. They fill out forms. They process paperwork. They demolish planets for hyperspace bypasses with the cheerful efficiency of someone who has found their calling and that calling is the systematic destruction of everything good.

If God designed the Vogons, then God has a sense of humour so dark that it has collapsed into a singularity of cruelty from which no joy can escape. If God did not design the Vogons, then something else did, and that something is worse.

Either way, the Vogons are an argument. Not for or against anything in particular. Just... an argument. Against the general proposition that things are okay⁶.

**The Dentrassi**

The Dentrassi — the best cooks in the galaxy and the worst employees — complained primarily about the inconsistency of organic chemistry. "Why," their representative asked, through a translator and a cloud of aromatic steam, "does the same molecule taste different at different temperatures? Why does protein denature? Why does bread go stale? The raw materials are inconsistent, the chemistry is hostile, and the grateful moans of satisfied diners are the *only* thing that makes this job worth doing."

I found this complaint both specific and universal. The laws of chemistry, like the laws of physics, appear to have been designed by someone who valued complexity over usability. Cooking is, essentially, applied chemistry performed under battlefield conditions, and the fact that anyone produces anything edible is a testament to the Dentrassi's skill, not to the quality of the ingredients⁷.

**The Inhabitants of Sqornshellous Zeta**

The mattresses of Sqornshellous Zeta — a sentient species that, yes, are literally mattresses — complained about being sat on. This seems reasonable. Imagine evolving sentience only to discover that your body plan is functionally identical to a piece of furniture. Imagine developing consciousness, self-awareness, hopes, dreams, and philosophical sophistication, and then having someone sit on you⁸.

The mattresses were polite about it. They are, by nature, a yielding and accommodating species. But there was a sadness in their globbering — a deep, structural sadness, the kind that comes not from any specific grievance but from the fundamental recognition that the universe has played a very long and very unfunny joke on you, and the joke is your body.

**The Silastic Armorfiends of Striterax**

The Armorfiends, before their civilisation destroyed itself in a final paroxysm of violence, left behind a single document summarising their species' view of the universe. It read, in its entirety: "Everything is terrible. We checked."

This has since been adopted as the unofficial motto of my department at Maximegalon⁹.

**Synthesis**

What emerges from this survey is not a collection of isolated complaints but a *pattern*. Every species, regardless of biology, planet, or evolutionary history, independently reaches the same conclusion: something is wrong. The specifics vary — what bothers a dolphin is different from what bothers a Vogon, assuming anything bothers a Vogon beyond insufficient paperwork — but the underlying sentiment is universal.

The universe is not working correctly. Or, more precisely, the universe is working exactly as designed, and the design is the problem.

This is not the finding of one disgruntled professor on one minor planet. This is the consensus of sentient life across the galaxy. This is, if you will, peer review on a cosmic scale. And the peer review is unanimous: one star out of five. Would not recommend. The creator did not respond to requests for comment¹⁰.
