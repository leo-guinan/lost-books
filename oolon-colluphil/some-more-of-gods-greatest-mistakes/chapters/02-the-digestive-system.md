# Chapter 2: The Digestive System — Thirty Feet of Regret

---

Consider the sandwich.

A simple thing. Two pieces of bread. Some filling. Perhaps a leaf of lettuce if you're feeling ambitious, though lettuce itself raises questions I'll address shortly¹. The sandwich is, by any reasonable measure, one of the more straightforward objects in the universe. It is not complex. It is not mysterious. It does not require a degree in advanced physics to understand. You pick it up. You eat it.

And then your body — that magnificent, allegedly designed temple of biological engineering — processes this sandwich through *thirty feet of tubing*.

Thirty. Feet.

I want you to sit with that for a moment. Thirty feet of intestinal pipeline to extract nutrients from a cheese sandwich. This is not efficiency. This is not even a reasonable approximation of efficiency. This is what happens when you design plumbing while drunk and then refuse to admit the mistake because you've already installed it and the warranty has expired².

The digestive system, taken as a whole, is perhaps the most damning evidence in the entire case against intelligent design. Not because it doesn't work — it does work, after a fashion, in the same way that a Rube Goldberg machine "works" — but because it works so *badly*, so *circuitously*, so *dramatically* that the only reasonable conclusion is that it was designed by someone who was either showing off or had never actually eaten food.

Let us begin at the beginning. The mouth.

The mouth is a multipurpose orifice that is expected to eat, breathe, speak, kiss, and occasionally whistle, all with the same equipment. Imagine a device that was simultaneously a food processor, an air intake, a communication system, and a musical instrument. You would say: this is too many functions for one hole. You would be right. The mouth agrees with you. This is why people choke³.

Choking. The act of eating going wrong because the breathing apparatus and the eating apparatus share a hallway. In what conceivable design philosophy does this make sense? In no other engineering context would you route the fuel line through the air intake. Cars do not do this. Spaceships do not do this. Even Sirius Cybernetics Corporation products — and I remind you, their fundamental design philosophy is that the user is an inconvenience — do not do this⁴.

Moving past the mouth, the food enters the oesophagus, a tube that conveys it to the stomach via peristalsis — a series of muscular contractions that work perfectly well unless you happen to be lying down, upside down, or experiencing any of the seventeen common conditions that cause acid reflux, in which case the system reverses itself and you experience the sandwich a second time, in the wrong direction, at three in the morning.

The stomach itself is a masterpiece of contradictory engineering. It contains hydrochloric acid. Strong hydrochloric acid. Acid that can dissolve metal. And what is the stomach made of? *Tissue*. The stomach dissolves things using acid that would, if given the opportunity, dissolve the stomach. The only thing preventing this is a thin layer of mucus that must be constantly replenished, like a man frantically repainting a bridge that is being dissolved by the river it crosses⁵.

Sometimes the mucus fails. When the mucus fails, you get an ulcer — which is to say, the stomach begins to digest itself. The stomach. Digesting. *Itself*. If you commissioned an architect to build you a house and the house began eating its own walls, you would not describe this as "intelligent design." You would describe it as a lawsuit.

Below the stomach, we enter the intestinal labyrinth. The small intestine — approximately twenty feet of coiled tubing through which nutrients are painstakingly extracted — is "small" only in diameter. In length, it is roughly the height of a two-storey building. It has been folded, compressed, and crammed into your abdominal cavity like an angry cobra stuffed into a shoebox. The surface area, when fully unfolded, would cover a tennis court. Nobody needs a tennis court inside their abdomen⁶.

And then we come to it. The organ that I have been waiting two books to properly address.

The appendix.

The appendix is a small, finger-shaped pouch attached to the large intestine. It does nothing. It has no function. It sits there, quietly, taking up space, like an intern who has run out of tasks but is too embarrassed to leave. For years it does nothing. For decades it does nothing. And then, one day, for no reason that medical science has been able to determine, it *explodes*⁷.

The appendix is not merely useless. Useless would be forgivable. We all have useless things — that drawer in the kitchen, the instructions that came with the furniture, the entire planet of Vogsphere. Uselessness is passive. The appendix is *actively hostile*. It is a feature that nobody requested, nobody uses, and nobody can cancel, and when it finally decides to make its presence known, it does so by trying to kill you.

If God were a software developer, the appendix would be an undocumented background process that consumes no resources for years and then suddenly corrupts your entire hard drive. It would be recalled. There would be a patch. There would be a class-action lawsuit. Instead, we get surgery.

I have not yet mentioned hiccups.

Hiccups are involuntary spasms of the diaphragm that serve no purpose whatsoever. They do not help digestion. They do not protect the airway. They do not accomplish anything except making you look ridiculous and interrupting every sentence you try to speak for anywhere between thirty seconds and, in one documented case on Sqornshellous Zeta, forty-seven years⁸.

The hiccup is a vestigial reflex inherited from amphibian ancestors who used a similar mechanism to pump water over their gills. We do not have gills. We have not had gills for approximately three hundred and seventy million years. And yet the reflex remains, like a notification from an app you deleted three phones ago. The body has not cleared its cache. The body, it appears, has never cleared its cache. The body is running on legacy code that nobody has audited since the Devonian period, and it shows.

And then there is the matter of fibre. The digestive system requires fibre to function properly. Fibre, which the digestive system cannot digest. You must eat something indigestible in order for the digestive system to digest other things. This is like requiring a car to carry a passenger it can never drop off in order for the engine to work. It is not design. It is a workaround. The entire digestive system is, fundamentally, a series of workarounds built on top of other workarounds, held together by mucus and optimism⁹.

I could continue. I could discuss the gallbladder, another organ that works perfectly well until the moment it decides to produce stones — *stones*, inside a soft tissue organ, as though the body had confused itself with a quarry. I could discuss lactose intolerance, a condition in which the majority of the galaxy's sentient beings cannot process the primary product of their own species' mammary glands. I could discuss the fact that the human body cannot synthesize Vitamin C, a nutrient so essential that its absence causes your connective tissue to dissolve and your teeth to fall out — a feature that guinea pigs and fruit bats share and that literally every other mammal has managed to retain¹⁰.

But I think the point is made. Thirty feet of tubing. Acid that eats its own container. An organ that does nothing until it tries to kill you. A reflex from when we had gills.

This is not a digestive system. This is a cry for help.
