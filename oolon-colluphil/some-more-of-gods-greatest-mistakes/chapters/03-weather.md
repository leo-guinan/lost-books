# Chapter 3: Weather — Terms and Conditions Nobody Agreed To

---

I would like to see the Conditions of Service Agreement.

I would like to see the document — the contract, the terms, the fine print — that every sentient being on every planet allegedly signed, or at least tacitly accepted, agreeing to the weather. Because I did not agree to this. I was not consulted. I was not given an opt-out clause, a cooling-off period¹, or even a basic summary of what to expect. I was simply born onto a planet and told, implicitly, that the sky would be doing things to me for the rest of my life and that my options were to either tolerate it or move indoors.

This is not acceptable. If a landlord behaved this way — randomly flooding your apartment, occasionally setting the ceiling on fire, periodically hurling ice at your windows — you would have legal recourse. You would call the authorities. You would, at the very minimum, leave a scathing review. But when God does it, we call it "weather" and sell umbrellas.

Let us examine the portfolio of atmospheric grievances.

**Hurricanes**

A hurricane is a rotating storm system that can span hundreds of miles, produce winds in excess of 150 miles per hour, and flatten everything in its path. It is also, from a user experience perspective, a catastrophic failure in environmental design.

The hurricane serves no discernible purpose. It does not clean the atmosphere. It does not redistribute resources equitably. It does not accomplish anything that could not be accomplished more efficiently by a stiff breeze and some light reorganization. What it does is destroy homes, uproot trees, and rearrange coastlines with the casual indifference of a toddler reorganizing a bookshelf².

If weather were software, hurricanes would be a known bug that the developer refused to patch. "Working as intended," the release notes would say, while cities sank beneath the waves. This is not working as intended. If this is working as intended, the intentions need to be investigated.

**Rain on Oceans**

It rains on the ocean.

I need you to understand how profoundly stupid this is.

The ocean is water. Rain is water. It is water falling on water. This is a delivery system shipping a product to a location that is already entirely composed of that product. It is like mailing sand to a desert. It is like delivering a lecture on theology to a theologian — technically possible, but what exactly is the point³?

Approximately 78% of all rainfall on Earth falls on the ocean. Seventy-eight percent. More than three-quarters of all the rain that falls — rain that could be irrigating crops, filling reservoirs, watering gardens, doing literally anything useful — falls on a body of water so vast that the addition is, statistically, meaningless. The ocean does not need the rain. The ocean has never needed the rain. The ocean is, by definition, the last place in the world that needs more water⁴.

This is not distribution. This is not a system. This is someone leaving the tap running.

**Snow**

Snow is beautiful. I will grant that. It is one of the few atmospheric phenomena that is genuinely aesthetically pleasing — a soft, white blanket that transforms landscapes into scenes of serene and picturesque wonder.

It also kills people.

Snow kills people by freezing them, burying them, collapsing their roofs, making their roads impassable, and — in a particularly cruel touch — being so beautiful while it does so that the victims are expected to enjoy it. "Isn't the snow lovely?" people say, as hypothermia sets in. "Look how it sparkles!" they observe, as their car slides off the road into a ditch. Snow is nature's attractive nuisance — a hazard dressed in evening wear⁵.

The cruelty of snow is not in its existence but in its design. It falls as a light, crystalline powder — each flake a unique and intricate work of microscopic art, which is nice — and then compacts into ice, which is lethal. The transition from "charming" to "deadly" takes approximately six hours, or less if someone drives on it. This is the atmospheric equivalent of a dinner party guest who arrives with flowers and by midnight has set the kitchen on fire.

Furthermore, snow melts. It melts into water, which then floods everything that survived the snow itself. The beautiful white blanket becomes a brown, freezing deluge. This is planned obsolescence in precipitation form — the product is designed to self-destruct, and the self-destruction is worse than the product⁶.

**Hail**

And then there is hail.

Hail is ice falling from the sky. Not gently, like snow. Not usefully, like rain. Hail falls from the sky with the velocity and temperament of a thrown brick, and it is — I want to be absolutely clear about this — *completely inexplicable*.

Rain, you can argue, serves a purpose. Snow, you can at least enjoy aesthetically before it kills you. But hail? Hail is assault. Hail is the weather reaching down and hitting you. There is no charitable interpretation of hail. There is no theological framework in which hail makes sense. It is ice, falling from the sky, at speed, onto your head, your car, your crops, and your will to live⁷.

Hailstones can be the size of golf balls. Sometimes larger. There are recorded instances of hailstones the size of grapefruits, which means that the sky occasionally hurls frozen objects the size of fruit at the ground and everyone is simply expected to carry on. If a person did this — if a person stood on a rooftop and hurled grapefruit-sized balls of ice at pedestrians — they would be arrested immediately. When the atmosphere does it, we call it a "weather event" and adjust our insurance premiums.

The mechanism by which hail forms is, if anything, even more absurd than the end result. Water droplets are carried upward by storm updrafts, freeze, fall, are carried upward again, accumulate more ice, fall again, are carried upward *again* — the droplet bouncing up and down inside the cloud like a ball in a cosmic pinball machine, gathering mass and malice with each pass, until finally the updraft can no longer support it and it plummets to Earth with the singular purpose of ruining someone's greenhouse⁸.

**Lightning**

I will say one brief word about lightning: it is an electrical discharge of up to one billion volts that strikes the ground at random, sets things on fire, and kills an average of two thousand beings per year on Earth alone.

The theological defence of lightning is that it "maintains the electrical balance of the atmosphere." This is like defending arson on the grounds that it "maintains the thermal balance of the neighbourhood." The process works. The process is also terrifying, random, and occasionally fatal. That it achieves a useful atmospheric function is incidental to the fact that it is, fundamentally, the sky trying to electrocute you⁹.

**Fog**

I will also mention fog, which is not dangerous in itself but is deeply suspicious. Fog is a cloud that has given up on being high and settled for being in the way. It reduces visibility, dampens spirits, and makes everything look like the opening scene of a murder mystery. It serves no function that I have been able to identify except to make the already difficult business of navigating the world slightly more difficult, while also being atmospheric in the literary sense, which suggests that whoever designed weather had a flair for drama but no interest in practicality¹⁰.

In summary: the weather is an unregulated, unaccountable system of atmospheric chaos that nobody agreed to, nobody can opt out of, and nobody can complain about effectively because the complaints department is, apparently, the sky, and the sky isn't listening.

I would like a refund.

I would like, at minimum, an acknowledgment that the system is flawed and a commitment to future improvements.

I would like to see the Conditions of Service Agreement.

I suspect it doesn't exist. I suspect the weather is what you get when you design an atmosphere without a plan, without testing, and without any mechanism for user feedback. I suspect the weather is, like so much else in this universe, a first draft that went to production.
