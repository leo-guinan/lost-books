# Chapter 1: We Weren't Finished

---

There is a particular kind of optimism that I find simultaneously touching and infuriating. It is the optimism of the reader who finished *Where God Went Wrong*, set it down on their bedside table¹, and thought: "Well, that about covers it."

It does not cover it.

It does not begin to cover it. It does not approach covering it. If "covering it" were a planet, *Where God Went Wrong* would be a small and slightly confused satellite orbiting at a great distance, squinting through a telescope, and saying "Is that it? Over there? The big round thing that looks like a comprehensive catalogue of errors?"

No. That's just the moon. The planet is behind you. And it's bigger than you thought. And it's on fire.

I understand the impulse. Book 1 was thorough. It addressed the giraffe's laryngeal nerve — a detour of such breathtaking pointlessness that I still lose sleep over it². It documented the human eye, which is wired backwards. It explored the existence of the mosquito, an organism whose entire purpose appears to be delivering misery in a package too small to satisfyingly destroy. These are real problems. They deserved attention. They received attention.

But we had barely scratched the surface.

The surface itself being another mistake³.

You see, the difficulty with cataloguing God's errors is not that they are hidden. Quite the opposite. They are *everywhere*. They are so pervasive, so thoroughly embedded in the fabric of reality, that most people don't even notice them anymore. They have become background noise. The universe hums with low-grade incompetence and everyone has simply turned the volume down and learned to live with it.

I cannot do this. I have tried. I spent a very pleasant weekend on Santraginus V, attempting to "let go" and "accept the universe as it is," as my therapist recommended⁴. By Sunday afternoon I had filled three notebooks with observations about the unnecessary cruelty of sunburn and had to be escorted from the beach by concerned lifeguards.

The problem, fundamentally, is one of scope. In Book 1, I focused primarily on biological design failures — the body as a machine built by someone who had read about machines but never actually used one. This was, in retrospect, too narrow. The mistakes extend far beyond biology. They are meteorological. They are cosmological. They are temporal, spatial, gastrointestinal, and — in the case of the Babel fish — paradoxical in ways that make my head hurt, which is itself a design flaw I address in Chapter 6.

Let me give you a sense of what we're dealing with.

In the time it takes you to read this paragraph, approximately forty-seven things will go wrong with your body that you won't even notice⁵. Cells will divide incorrectly. Nerve signals will misfire. Your immune system — which I charitably described in Book 1 as "enthusiastic but confused" — will attack something benign while ignoring something dangerous. Your spine, that heroic and doomed column of compromise, will compress slightly further. And somewhere in your digestive tract, a process of such baroque complexity will be underway that, if you understood it fully, you would never eat again.

Meanwhile, outside your body, weather will happen. Not sensible weather. Not weather that has been thought through. Weather that appears to have been designed by a committee of sadists working under a tight deadline with no quality assurance process whatsoever⁶. Somewhere on your planet, it will be raining on an ocean. Think about that. It is raining. On water. The water is receiving more water. This is not efficient. This is not clever. This is a system running without adult supervision.

Further afield, light — the fastest thing in the universe — will be plodding along at a speed that, while impressive by local standards, is cosmically laughable. The nearest interesting star system is four years away *at the speed of light*. Four years. You can't even get a planning application through the Galactic Council in four years⁷. The universe is, effectively, a very large room in which everything is too far away to reach, and the lights are too slow to show you what you're missing. This is not a design choice. This is a bandwidth problem.

And through it all, sentient beings across the galaxy are waking up, looking around, and independently reaching the same conclusion: something has gone terribly wrong, and nobody is answering the complaint line.

I have spent the last three years since Book 1's publication collecting these complaints. I have interviewed beings from forty-seven planets. I have consulted experts in meteorology, gastroenterology, cosmological physics, pain management, and existential despair. I have read the theological rebuttals. I have read them *carefully*⁸. I have weighed them. I have found them wanting.

The result is the book you are now holding — or the book you are now reading on your Sub-Etha device, or the book that is being read to you by a slightly judgmental audiobook narrator, or the book you have stolen, in which case I hope you enjoy it because the universe certainly won't provide you with much else to be happy about.

This book will cover what Book 1 could not. The digestive system. Weather. The speed of light. The existence of other sentient beings and their own catalogues of grievance. Pain. Aging. And the Babel fish — that miraculous, impossible, wonderful little creature that, in the process of being the single best argument for God's existence, accidentally and definitively proves He doesn't exist.

It is going to be, in the words of my publisher, "quite a ride."

My publisher is an optimist. I find this simultaneously touching and infuriating.

Let us continue.
