# Chapter 9: Conclusion — A Formal Request for a Refund

---

We have arrived at the end.

Not the end of God's mistakes — that would require a library, and the library would need its own library, and that library would need counselling — but the end of this particular volume's attempt to document them¹.

Over the preceding eight chapters, we have examined the digestive system (a thirty-foot Rube Goldberg machine that occasionally tries to dissolve itself), the weather (an unregulated atmospheric chaos engine that rains on oceans), the speed of light (a bandwidth cap imposed by a cosmic ISP with no customer service), other sentient beings (unanimously dissatisfied), pain (a smoke alarm with no off switch), aging (planned obsolescence without a returns policy), and the Babel fish (God's suicide note, disguised as a translation device).

We examined these in addition to the biological design failures catalogued in *Where God Went Wrong* — the backwards-wired eye, the giraffe's laryngeal nerve, the human knee, the mosquito, the platypus, and all the rest.

And I want to make something very clear.

These mistakes are not random.

This is the most important observation in either book, and I have saved it for last because I wanted you to have the full weight of evidence before you heard it. The mistakes are not random. They are not scattered, isolated failures in an otherwise competent system. They are not anomalies. They are not exceptions.

They are *consistent*.

The digestive system fails in ways that are consistent with the respiratory system's failures. The weather fails in ways that are consistent with the laws of physics' failures. Pain fails in ways that are consistent with aging's failures. And the Babel fish fails — or, more precisely, succeeds so catastrophically that it destroys its own creator — in ways that are consistent with everything else.

There is a pattern. The pattern is: *nothing works quite right, and everything that doesn't work quite right doesn't work quite right in the same way*.

The same way being: it sort of works, enough to sustain hope; it fails, enough to sustain disappointment; and it is just complex enough that you can convince yourself it was intentional, if you squint and have a degree in theology².

This consistency eliminates the possibility of random failure. Random failure would produce variety. Some things would work well. Some things would work badly. Some things would work in completely unexpected ways. But that is not what we observe. What we observe is a uniform, galaxy-wide, cross-disciplinary *mediocrity* — a baseline level of "not quite good enough" that pervades every system, every species, every natural law.

This leaves us with two possible explanations.

**Explanation One: Incompetence**

God exists, but He is not very good at His job.

This is the more charitable interpretation. It assumes good intentions. It assumes that the Creator genuinely wanted to build a universe that worked — a universe of beauty, elegance, and functional efficiency — and simply lacked the skill to pull it off. He aimed for perfection and landed on "adequate, with reservations." He wanted to build a cathedral and built a shed that leaks.

This interpretation has a certain poignant appeal. Who among us has not attempted something ambitious and fallen short? Who has not designed a bookshelf that wobbles, a meal that disappoints, a life that doesn't quite match the brochure? God, in this reading, is not malevolent. He is merely in over His head. The universe is not a crime. It is a well-intentioned C-minus³.

I would find this interpretation comforting if the failures were not so specifically calibrated to cause maximum frustration. An incompetent creator would produce *chaotic* failure. What we observe is *systematic* failure. The back fails at exactly the age when you need it most. Pain intensifies at exactly the moment when you've already received the message. Hail falls at exactly the size to destroy a greenhouse but not kill you outright — leaving you alive to be frustrated. This is not incompetence. Incompetence is not this precise⁴.

**Explanation Two: Performance Art**

God exists, and this is all deliberate.

The failures are intentional. The frustrations are scripted. The inconsistencies are consistent because they are part of a unified artistic vision — a grand, cosmic performance piece in which the medium is suffering, the audience is everyone, and the artist has no intention of explaining the work because explanation would diminish its impact.

This interpretation is supported by the evidence more than I would like to admit. The Babel fish paradox, in particular, has the hallmarks of deliberate artistic construction — the perfect setup, the devastating punchline, the elegant self-destruction. The weather has a dramatic flair that transcends mere dysfunction. Pain has a theatrical intensity that mere biological necessity cannot explain. Even aging has a narrative arc — the body as tragic protagonist, peaking early, declining slowly, ending in quiet dissolution.

If the universe is performance art, it is the most ambitious, most committed, and most infuriating performance art in history. It has been running for thirteen point eight billion years. The audience has been captive since birth. No interval has been offered. And the artist — the artist — has never once appeared on stage to take questions⁵.

I do not enjoy this interpretation. Performance art should be optional. This is compulsory. Compulsory performance art is not art. It is punishment.

**A Third Possibility**

There is, of course, a third possibility, which is that there is no God, there is no design, and the universe's consistent mediocrity is simply the natural result of physical processes that have no intelligence, no intention, and no interest in whether anyone is satisfied.

This possibility is, in many ways, the most distressing of all, because it means there is no one to complain to.

I refuse to accept this. Not because the evidence doesn't support it — it rather does — but because I have spent two books and a significant portion of my academic career constructing complaints, and I am not prepared to discover that there is no complaints department. The complaints exist. They are documented. They are footnoted. They *demand* a recipient⁶.

**The Formal Request**

And so I come, at last, to the purpose of this chapter, this book, and perhaps my entire body of work.

I would like a refund.

I would like a full refund for the universe as delivered, on the grounds that it does not match the description, does not perform as advertised, and contains numerous defects that were present at time of manufacture and have not been addressed despite repeated notification.

Specifically, I am requesting:

1. A full replacement of the digestive system, or at minimum, a reduction in total tubing to something reasonable (ten feet, maximum) and the removal of the appendix.

2. A comprehensive weather patch, including but not limited to: the elimination of hail, the redirection of ocean rainfall to locations that actually need it, and a binding commitment to forecast accuracy.

3. An increase in the speed of light to something practical. I am not unreasonable. I am not asking for instant travel. I am asking for a speed that makes the nearest star system reachable within a long weekend.

4. A pain system with an acknowledge button, a volume control, and a proportionality calibration that ensures stubbed toes do not register as mortal injuries.

5. An extension of peak bodily performance to at least age seventy, with optional renewal thereafter.

6. A formal explanation of the Babel fish paradox, ideally one that does not result in the explainer ceasing to exist.

7. An apology⁷.

I submit this request in full knowledge that it will not be granted. The complaints department, if it exists, has never responded to any communication in the history of sentient life. The silence is total, universal, and — if you'll permit one final theological observation — *consistent*.

But the request is on the record. It is documented. It is, as of this printing, in bookshops across the galaxy. And if there is a God — if, despite the Babel fish paradox, despite the evidence, despite every footnote in these two volumes — if there is a God, then He now knows, formally and in writing, that His work has been reviewed, and the review is not favourable.

One star. Out of five.

Would not create again⁸.
