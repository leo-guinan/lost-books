# Chapter 6: Pain — The Feedback Mechanism That Won't Shut Up

---

Pain, we are told, is useful.

Pain, the argument goes, is the body's warning system — a sophisticated alert mechanism that notifies you when something is wrong, prompting you to take corrective action. Without pain, you would leave your hand on the hot stove. Without pain, you would walk on a broken leg. Without pain, you would not know that something was damaging you until the damage was irreparable.

This is a reasonable argument. It is also, when examined for more than thirty seconds, *completely insufficient* to explain what pain actually is and what pain actually does¹.

Because yes — pain as a warning system makes sense. A brief, sharp signal that says "the stove is hot, remove your hand" is useful. A clear notification that says "your leg is broken, stop walking on it" serves a legitimate purpose. A gentle alert that says "this food has gone bad, stop eating it" is a perfectly reasonable design choice.

But that is not what pain does.

Pain does not send a brief, clear notification and then stop. Pain sends a notification, and then it sends it again. And again. And again. It sends it louder. It sends it at three in the morning. It sends it when you already know. It sends it when you have already taken corrective action and are sitting perfectly still, trying very hard not to move, having already done everything in your power to address the situation, and the pain says: "Yes, but have you considered that this HURTS?"

Yes. I have considered that. I considered it the first time you told me. I considered it the four hundred and seventh time you told me. I am currently considering it right now, which is why I am not sleeping, not eating, not thinking about anything else, and contemplating whether consciousness itself was a good idea².

Pain is not a warning system. Pain is a warning system designed by someone who has never heard of an "acknowledge" button.

Every competent alarm system has an acknowledge function. The fire alarm goes off. You hear it. You acknowledge it. The alarm stops, or at least reduces in volume, because the system understands that you have received the message and are dealing with it. This is basic design. This is Alarm Systems 101. Even the Sirius Cybernetics Corporation — *even them* — build an acknowledge button into their alerts³.

The human pain system has no acknowledge button. There is no way to tell your body "message received, thank you, I am aware of the situation, please stop screaming." The nerves fire, and they continue firing, and they will continue firing until either the stimulus is removed, the nerves themselves are destroyed, or you die. Those are your options. Receipt of message. Destruction of messenger. Death.

This would be bad enough if pain were proportional — if the intensity of the pain corresponded to the severity of the damage in some useful, informative way. But it doesn't. Pain is wildly, almost comically disproportionate.

Consider the migraine.

A migraine is a headache. That is its medical classification. A *headache*. The word "headache" suggests something minor — an inconvenience, a nuisance, the neurological equivalent of a slightly annoying neighbour. The reality of a migraine is closer to the neurological equivalent of a home invasion⁴.

A migraine can produce pain so severe that the sufferer cannot open their eyes, cannot tolerate light, cannot tolerate sound, cannot tolerate the *concept* of light and sound, and would, if given the option, voluntarily disassemble their own skull to make it stop. A migraine is the body's pain system operating at maximum volume for no reason. Nothing is broken. Nothing is damaged. No corrective action is possible. The brain is simply *hurting itself*, like a smoke alarm that goes off because it has decided, independently and without evidence, that something might be on fire.

If migraines served a purpose — if they were the body's way of signaling some important physiological need — then they would at least be explicable. They do not serve a purpose. They are, neurologically speaking, a malfunction. They are the pain system misfiring. They are evidence that the pain system was not designed, but rather *happened*, through a process of haphazard evolutionary accumulation that left the wiring exposed and the circuit breakers missing⁵.

And then there is the matter of stubbing your toe.

Stubbing your toe at three in the morning, in the dark, on the way to the bathroom, is an experience so universal, so consistently agonizing, and so thoroughly pointless that it serves as the clearest possible demonstration of everything wrong with the pain system.

The damage is negligible. A toe has been struck against a solid object. The impact is minor. The tissue damage is minimal. On the scale of bodily injuries, a stubbed toe ranks somewhere between "papercut" and "sneezed too hard." And yet the pain — the searing, electric, all-consuming pain that shoots up your leg, through your spine, and into your brain like a lightning bolt made of pure hatred — is *extraordinary*⁶.

Why? Why does a stubbed toe hurt so much? The toe is not broken. The toe is not bleeding. The toe is not in danger. The toe is simply upset, and it wants you to know about it, immediately, at maximum volume, at three in the morning, when you are least equipped to deal with it emotionally or philosophically.

The answer, if you can call it an answer, is that the foot contains an unusually high density of nociceptors — pain-sensing nerve endings — in a very small area, with very little padding to absorb impact. The toe is, effectively, a exposed nerve cluster mounted on the end of your foot at precisely the height of every piece of furniture ever manufactured. It is a pain antenna. It is *optimized for suffering*⁷.

This is not a design choice anyone would make on purpose. This is not a design choice that survives a single round of user testing. "We've placed the most sensitive pain receptors at the lowest point of the body, in an unprotected extremity, at the exact height of table legs, door frames, and bedposts." "Won't that cause enormous amounts of unnecessary pain?" "Yes." "Why?" "We don't know. Next item on the agenda."

The theological defence of pain usually invokes "character building." Pain makes us stronger, the argument goes. Pain teaches us. Pain gives life meaning by providing contrast — without suffering, we cannot truly appreciate joy.

To which I say: this is the argument of someone who has never had a kidney stone⁸.

A kidney stone is a small crystalline deposit that forms in the kidney and must pass through the urinary tract to exit the body. The pain this produces has been compared, by people who have experienced both, to childbirth. It has been described as "the worst pain a human being can experience." It serves no purpose. It teaches nothing. It does not build character. It builds resentment. It builds a very specific and very justified resentment toward whoever designed a system in which minerals can spontaneously crystallize inside your organs and then force their way out through a tube that was absolutely not designed to accommodate them.

Pain is not a feature. Pain is a bug that has been retroactively justified as a feature by theologians who have, by and large, not experienced enough of it to appreciate how absurd their justification sounds.

The warning function could be accomplished by a brief signal. The educational function could be accomplished by memory. The "character building" function could be accomplished by literally anything other than the sensation of being stabbed from the inside by your own calcium deposits.

Pain, as currently implemented, is a feedback mechanism with no off switch, no volume control, no proportionality, and no respect for the fact that the user is already aware of the problem and would very much like to address it in peace.

It is, in short, another mistake. A loud one.
