# Chapter 7: The Aging Problem — Planned Obsolescence, Divine Edition

---

The human body reaches peak performance at approximately age twenty-five.

This is not a controversial claim. It is supported by extensive research in biology, sports science, neurology, and the general observation that twenty-five-year-olds can do things that fifty-five-year-olds merely remember doing. At twenty-five, the bones are dense, the muscles are responsive, the joints are functional, the brain is sharp, and the entire system operates with a fluidity and resilience that, in retrospect, seems almost suspicious — as though someone wanted you to see how well things *could* work before showing you how they actually will¹.

Because after twenty-five, everything starts to go wrong.

Not all at once. That would be too honest. The decline is gradual, insidious, almost polite in its early stages — a slight stiffness here, a minor ache there, the occasional inability to read small print that you were reading perfectly well last year. It creeps. It sidles. It insinuates itself into your life with the patient determination of a bureaucracy, and by the time you fully realise what is happening, it has been happening for years and there is no appeals process².

The body, in short, is subject to planned obsolescence. And I use the term deliberately, because the parallels between biological aging and corporate product degradation are too precise to be coincidental.

Consider: a product is manufactured to a high standard. It works beautifully. It performs all its functions with impressive efficiency. And then, after a predetermined period — not because of external damage, not because of misuse, but *by design* — it begins to fail. Components degrade. Systems slow. The warranty expires at exactly the moment you need it most. And when you contact customer service, you are told that the product was "never intended to last forever" and that perhaps you should consider an upgrade, except there are no upgrades available, just the same model in worse condition, forever, until the whole thing stops working entirely.

This is aging. This is the divine product lifecycle.

The back is perhaps the most eloquent example. The human back is a column of thirty-three vertebrae, separated by discs of cartilage, supported by muscles and ligaments, and tasked with the single most important structural job in the entire body: keeping you upright. It performs this job adequately for roughly twenty-five years. After that, it begins what can only be described as a slow and dignified collapse³.

Discs herniate. Vertebrae compress. Muscles atrophy. The spine, that proud and noble column, develops curves it was not designed to have, aches it cannot explain, and a general disposition best described as "resentful." Your back goes out. And unlike a product that goes out and can be returned, the back cannot be returned. There is no returns desk. There is no exchange policy. There is only a chiropractor, who will crack your spine with an enthusiasm that borders on the recreational and charge you handsomely for the privilege of a temporary and largely illusory improvement⁴.

Why does the back fail? Because it was designed — or, more accurately, *evolved* — for quadrupedal locomotion and has been pressed into service for bipedal use without a proper refit. The human spine is, essentially, a horizontal support beam that has been turned vertical and told to cope. It is coping. Badly. It is the structural equivalent of using a ladder as a bridge — it sort of works, you can sort of cross, but the engineers would like a word.

And the back is merely the beginning.

The eyes. Oh, the eyes. The eyes begin to lose their ability to focus on close objects around age forty — a condition called presbyopia, from the Greek words meaning "old eye," which is at least honestly named. Your lenses stiffen. Your ciliary muscles weaken. And reading — that fundamental activity, that cornerstone of civilisation, that thing you need to do in order to read this book — becomes impossible without corrective lenses.

The knees, which I addressed briefly in the Foreword and at length in Book 1, accelerate their decline. The cartilage wears thin. The synovial fluid decreases. The joint that once bent and straightened with fluid grace now creaks, pops, and occasionally buckles, like a hinge on a door that the landlord refuses to oil⁵.

The hearing diminishes. The metabolism slows. The hair falls out, or turns grey, or — in a display of particularly poor planning — does both simultaneously in different locations, leaving you bald on top and grey everywhere else, as though the body cannot decide whether to abandon the project entirely or simply lose interest in the colour scheme.

The teeth. The teeth are worth a special mention, because teeth are bones that the body has decided to mount *outside the skeleton*, exposed to the elements, in the one opening that is also responsible for eating, breathing, and speaking. They are the most vulnerable structural components in the entire body, and the body does not replace them. You get two sets. The first set falls out when you are a child. The second set is expected to last the remaining sixty to eighty years of your life, during which time they will be subjected to constant chemical assault (food, drink, stomach acid), mechanical stress (chewing, grinding, the occasional ill-advised attempt to open packaging), and bacterial colonisation (plaque, which forms continuously and must be manually removed twice daily forever or your teeth will rot from the inside).

Two sets. For eighty years of chemical warfare. This is not engineering. This is a dare⁶.

The most galling aspect of aging is not any individual failure but the *coordination*. Everything declines together. The eyes go, and the hands that might hold a magnifying glass become arthritic. The knees fail, and the cardiovascular system that might compensate for reduced mobility weakens simultaneously. The brain — that magnificent organ, the seat of consciousness, the one part of the system you truly need — begins to slow, to forget, to lose the very words you need to describe what is happening to you.

It is as though the body has a project plan for its own dismantlement — a carefully scheduled teardown, with each system degrading in sequence to ensure maximum cumulative misery. The heart weakens so you can't exercise. The joints stiffen so you can't move. The senses dull so you can't enjoy what remains. And the brain fogs so you can't even properly complain about it⁷.

This is planned obsolescence. Not the accidental kind, where a product wears out because it was cheaply made. The deliberate kind, where the product was designed from the outset to fail on a timeline. The body has a use-by date. It was stamped at manufacture. And nobody — *nobody* — was consulted about whether they found the timeline acceptable.

The theological defense of aging is, predictably, that it gives life "meaning." That mortality makes each moment precious. That the limitation of time creates urgency and purpose and beauty.

To which I respond: there are other ways to create meaning. You could, for instance, create beings who appreciate beauty without needing to be threatened with extinction. You could create urgency through inspiration rather than decay. You could build bodies that maintain their peak condition throughout their lifespan and create meaning through *experience* rather than *fear*.

But that would require effort. That would require a second draft. And God, as we have established throughout these two volumes, does not do second drafts⁸.

He ships the first version. He ignores the bug reports. And He lets the product decay according to schedule, confident that the users will find a way to describe it as "beautiful" because the alternative — admitting that the whole thing is a manufacturing defect — is too depressing to contemplate.

I have contemplated it. It is depressing.

But it is also true. And the truth, unlike the body, does not deteriorate with age.

Though my ability to articulate it clearly apparently does. Where was I? Ah yes. Everything falls apart. In conclusion: everything falls apart⁹.
