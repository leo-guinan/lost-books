# Appendix A: Acknowledgments

---

A book is never written alone, except when it is, which this one was, in a small flat, with a cat, over the course of eighteen months during which no one visited, no one called, and no one was thanked, because there was no one to thank.

Nevertheless:

I would like to thank my editor, Vroomfondel Oat III, who read the manuscript with the expression of a man defusing a bomb and who suggested, on multiple occasions, that I "tone it down." I did not tone it down. He published it anyway. This is either courage or a very specific form of professional resignation. Either way, I'm grateful.

I would like to thank the staff of the Maximegalon City Library, who allowed me unrestricted access to the theology section despite what I did to it the last time, which I maintain was an accident. Books do not normally catch fire when you disagree with them. I blame the binding.

I would like to thank the owner and staff of The Condition restaurant, where Chapter 4 was written and where Chapter 5 was nearly written before I was asked to leave for "making the other diners uncomfortable with my muttering." The muttering was about the giraffe's laryngeal nerve. I stand by it.

I would like to thank Dr. Petula Hendrix of the Sidereal Institute of Theological Studies, who refused to speak to me, refused to read my manuscript, refused to acknowledge my existence, and in doing so, provided me with an excellent model for the behavior of the deity I was writing about.

I would like to thank the Babel fish in my left ear, without whom I could not have conducted research in fourteen different languages. The fish has no opinion about its role in the proof that disproved God. Fish generally don't. This is one of their more admirable qualities.

I would like to thank the government of Maximegalon, which did not ban this book. They considered it. They held meetings about it. They formed a committee, and the committee formed a subcommittee, and the subcommittee published a report recommending that the book be "examined further," which in governmental terms means "ignored until everyone forgets about it." I appreciate their inaction. It is the most effective thing a government has ever done for literature.

I would like to thank the man in the shack, whose name I never learned, whose location I will not reveal, and whose tea was, despite its alarming color, excellent. He will not read these acknowledgments. He will not read this book. He has better things to do, or more precisely, he has nothing to do, which is — as I have come to understand — the better thing.

I would like to thank the seven deities who objected to this book's publication. Your objections were noted, filed, and used as evidence in Chapter 3. I hope this does not sour our relationship, such as it was, which was largely one-sided and involved a great deal of shouting on your part and a great deal of ducking on mine.

I would like to thank the students of my Philosophy 301 seminar at the University of Maximegalon, who asked better questions than I deserved and who mostly stopped attending after Week 6, which is fair, because Week 6 is when I started talking about the knee.

I would like to thank my cat, Mr. Wensleydale, for sitting on every draft of every chapter and thereby preventing me from editing too much. His editorial philosophy — that the best thing to do with a manuscript is sit on it and purr — is not entirely without merit.

I would like to thank the universe, which did not need to contain me and indeed shows no particular enthusiasm for the arrangement, but which has been, on balance, more interesting than the alternative, which is nothing, which I have now spent three books arguing is also what's in charge of it.

I would like to thank my mother, who always said I would "amount to something," and my father, who always said I would "come to no good." They were both right. These are not contradictory statements when applied to philosophers.

I would like to thank time, without which this book could not have been written, though with less of it the book would have been shorter, which my editor informs me would have been preferable.

I would like to thank silence, which taught me more than any book.

I would like to thank the river in the valley, which does not know it was thanked and would not care if it did, and which is still going, as rivers do, regardless.

I would like to thank the stars, which are very far away and very old and very beautiful and which will be there long after I am gone, not because anyone put them there but because physics is more reliable than intention, and hydrogen is more reliable than prayer.

And finally, I would like to thank whoever is responsible for this mess — assuming anyone is, which, as we have established over approximately twelve hundred pages and three volumes and a considerable quantity of tea, they aren't. But if they were — if, somewhere, somehow, against all evidence and logic and reason, someone *were* responsible — I'd want them to know:

It wasn't all bad.

The tea was good. The rain was good. The cat was good.

The fish were doing fine.

---

*O.C.*
*Maximegalon*
*The day before yesterday, or possibly tomorrow; time has become somewhat optional.*
