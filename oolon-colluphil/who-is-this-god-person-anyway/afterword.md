# Afterword

## A Final Note from the Publisher

---

It is with a mixture of concern, bewilderment, and legally mandated transparency that Conditions Press provides the following update regarding Mr. Oolon Colluphil.

Mr. Colluphil submitted the final manuscript of *Who Is This God Person Anyway?* on a Tuesday. We know it was a Tuesday because our receptionist noted the day in her log, along with the observation: "Mr. C. came in. Looked tired but peaceful. Left manuscript on desk. Took a biscuit. Didn't seem like a man who was about to disappear."

He disappeared on the Wednesday.

Not dramatically. There was no flash of light, no clap of thunder, no puff of logic (though we would have appreciated the symmetry). He simply wasn't there anymore. His flat was empty — not cleared out, but *empty*, in the way that a place is empty when the person who filled it has gone and taken with them whatever invisible thing made it theirs. The furniture was still there. The books were still there. His reading glasses were on the bedside table, folded neatly, as if they'd been told they wouldn't be needed anymore.

His cat, Mr. Wensleydale — a large orange tabby with a temperament that can best be described as "philosophically indifferent" — was found sitting on the kitchen table, next to an empty teacup and a note.

The note read:

> *Gone to see if there's anything interesting where God used to be.*
> *Forwarding address: 42, Nowhere, The Void.*
> *Don't worry about the cat. He worries about nothing, which, as I have spent three books arguing, is the correct amount of things to worry about.*
> *— O.C.*
> *P.S. The milk in the fridge has gone off. Sorry about that.*

We have attempted to contact Mr. Colluphil at the address provided. Our letters have been returned, not marked "addressee unknown" or "insufficient address" or any of the usual postal designations, but marked simply with a small drawing of a shrugging figure. The postal service has no explanation for this. Neither do we.

His bank account remains open but unused. His library card was returned — slipped under the library door in the night, with all fines paid, which his librarian described as "the most unsettling thing about the whole business." His subscriptions have not been cancelled. Every month, the Maximegalon Review of Philosophical Studies arrives at his flat, where Mr. Wensleydale sits on it.

We do not know where Mr. Colluphil is.

We do not know if he is alive, dead, or in some third state that his own philosophy might have predicted but that our legal department is not equipped to classify. Our insurance company has asked us to describe his status as "unlocatable," which is accurate in the way that describing the ocean as "damp" is accurate — technically correct, entirely insufficient.

What we know is this: he wrote three books. The first explained where God went wrong. The second catalogued further mistakes. The third asked who was making them, and concluded — with an argument that has not, to date, been refuted by anyone, divine or otherwise — that no one was. No one was making them, because no one was there to make them. The mistakes were simply what a universe looks like when no one is running it, the way a garden looks when no one tends it: wild, strange, and — if you know how to look at it — beautiful.

He was not, by any conventional measure, a successful philosopher. His books sold modestly. His lectures were poorly attended, partly because of their content and partly because he had a habit of forgetting which lecture hall he was supposed to be in and delivering the entire talk to an empty room, which he said was "excellent practice for addressing the Almighty." He had no academic chair, no tenure, no professional accolades. He had a cat and a flat and a teacup and an idea, and the idea turned out to be bigger than all the rest of it put together.

We miss him. This surprises us. We are a publishing house, not a family. But he was the kind of author who made you feel that the words mattered — not because they were clever or because they were profitable, but because they were *true*, in the way that rain is true, in the way that the sound of a river is true, in the way that a cat sitting on a manuscript is true: plainly, obviously, without needing anyone to believe in them.

The cat is fine. He is sitting on a fourth copy of the manuscript. He has developed a habit of staring at the chair where Mr. Colluphil used to sit, not with longing, but with the patient, steady gaze of a creature who understands that sometimes people are here and sometimes they aren't, and the chair is comfortable either way.

We think Mr. Colluphil would have approved of the cat's philosophy.

We think, in fact, that it might be the same one.

*Vroomfondel Oat III*
*Managing Director, Conditions Press*
*Maximegalon*
