# Chapter 8: What Now?

*In which God is gone, the universe remains, and the author surveys the wreckage.*

---

So.

God does not exist. We have proven this. The proof is valid. The theologians are silent, or screaming, or both — theology, like quantum mechanics, permits superposition of states — and the universe continues to rotate on whatever axis it rotates on (no one is quite sure; it used to be God's finger, apparently, but that's out), and we are left with a question that is considerably harder than the one we just answered.

The question is: what now?

It is a deceptively simple question. Two words. Three syllables. A child could ask it. A child frequently does ask it, usually about thirty seconds after something enormous has happened, while the adults are still standing around looking stunned. The child has processed the event already. The child wants to know what comes next. The adults are still on "what just happened."[^1]

What just happened is: God vanished. In a puff of logic. Cleanly, completely, and — I want to stress this — *politely*. There was no mess. No cosmic tantrum. No dying-of-the-light. Just a small displacement of air, a faint smell of ozone and old theology, and then: nothing. The spot where God used to be — or where we used to imagine God to be, which is the same spot, it turns out — is now just a spot. It has no special properties. It does not glow. It does not hum. It is, like every other spot in the universe, mostly empty space between mostly empty atoms doing mostly nothing.

What now?

Let me tell you what has not changed.

The universe is still here. It is still thirteen point eight billion years old, or thereabouts, depending on how you count and whether you trust the people doing the counting. It is still mostly vacuum. The bits that aren't vacuum are still mostly hydrogen. The bits that aren't hydrogen are still mostly heavier elements forged in the hearts of dying stars, which is poetic in a way that suggests the universe doesn't need a poet.

The laws of physics still work. Gravity still pulls things together. Entropy still pulls things apart. Light still travels at the speed of light, which is the fastest anything can go and which is, frankly, not fast enough for a universe this size, but there we are. No one is in charge of the speed limit anymore, but it doesn't seem to matter. The speed limit enforces itself.

Life still exists. On this planet, on millions of others, in forms ranging from single-celled organisms to vast, galaxy-spanning intelligences that still can't figure out what to have for dinner. Life continues to evolve, to adapt, to find new and creative ways to convert sunlight into arguments. The absence of God has not caused life to falter, any more than the absence of an architect causes a building to collapse once it's already standing.

Suffering still exists. This is, I know, the one that matters. People hoped — quietly, desperately, in the small hours of the night when hope is the only thing between you and the ceiling — that if God existed, suffering had a purpose. A reason. A plan. That the pain was going somewhere. That it was *for* something.

It isn't. It was never for anything. Suffering is a byproduct of consciousness, and consciousness is a byproduct of evolution, and evolution is a byproduct of chemistry, and chemistry is a byproduct of physics, and physics is a byproduct of nothing. The chain of causation stretches all the way back to the beginning and finds, at its origin, not a hand but a fluctuation. Not a purpose but a perturbation. Not a "why" but a "how," and even the "how" is debatable.

I am sorry. I know this is not what people wanted to hear. People wanted to hear that suffering has meaning. That the pain is temporary but the purpose is eternal. That someone, somewhere, is keeping score and will eventually make it right.

No one is keeping score.

Nothing will make it right.

We are left with a broken product and no returns policy.

The universe does not come with a warranty. It does not come with a customer service line. It does not come with a thirty-day satisfaction guarantee or a money-back offer or even a comment card. You cannot escalate to a manager. There is no manager. The manager position is vacant, as we established in Chapter 2, and it has always been vacant, and the "MANAGER ON DUTY" sign behind the counter is a relic from a time when we hadn't yet looked behind the counter and discovered that there was nobody there.

But.

*But.*

Here is a thing I did not expect, and which I include here not because it is philosophically rigorous but because it is true:

The universe, without God, is not worse. It is not colder, or darker, or emptier. It is exactly the same universe it was before the proof, because the proof did not change the universe. The proof changed our *description* of the universe. The map was updated. The territory was always this.

And the territory — the actual, physical, godless territory — contains:

Stars. More stars than anyone will ever count, burning with a light that has traveled for billions of years to reach the back of your eye, where your imperfectly wired retina will detect it, and your imperfect brain will interpret it as beauty, and for a moment — a single, ridiculous, unsupervised moment — a small mammal on a small rock will look up at the light of a dead star and feel *wonder*.

No one designed that moment. No one planned it. No one is watching it happen. And it is, I think, more beautiful for that. Not less. More.

Because a sunset painted by God is a performance. A sunset that happens because of the rotation of a planet and the refraction of light through an atmosphere is just... a sunset. It isn't trying to prove anything. It isn't evidence of anything. It is simply light, doing what light does, because physics, which has no opinions and no feelings and no need for worship.

It is honest. The sunset is honest in a way that a designed sunset could never be.

And we are free, now, to see it honestly. Without looking for the painter behind the canvas. Without trying to decode the message. Without wondering what it *means*. It doesn't mean anything. It is a sunset. Look at it. Or don't. The sunset doesn't care.

What now?

Now we live in a universe with no manager, no plan, no returns policy, and no customer service line. A universe where suffering has no purpose and beauty has no author and the speed of light is inadequate and the human knee is a disaster and the giraffe's laryngeal nerve is fifteen feet too long.

Now we have only each other.

Now we have only the universe, which doesn't work properly but which is — and I say this as someone who has spent three books criticizing it — astonishing. Broken and astonishing. Flawed and astonishing. Empty of gods and full of light.

What now?

I don't know.

No one knows.

That's the point.

He had, in that moment, a more functional relationship with the concept of irreversible change than most theologians develop in a lifetime.
