# Chapter 6: The Argument from Personal Experience

*In which the author considers the testimony of those who claim to have felt God's presence, and finds it indistinguishable from the testimony of those who claim to have felt their phone vibrate when it hasn't.*

---

We have now demolished three arguments for God's existence: the ontological (thinking something doesn't make it real), the teleological (the universe looks designed in the way that a car crash looks driven), and the cosmological (which we addressed in Book One and which amounts to "something caused something, therefore God," a logical chain with roughly the structural integrity of a daisy).

There remains one argument that cannot be demolished by logic, because it does not rely on logic. It relies on something far more powerful, far more persuasive, and far more unreliable:

Feeling.

"I have felt God's presence," says the believer, and their eyes are sincere, and their voice is steady, and their experience is — to them — as real as the chair they're sitting on. "I have felt Him. In prayer. In moments of crisis. In the silence of a cathedral at dawn. I have felt something, and that something was God."

I do not doubt that they felt something.

I merely doubt what it was.[^1]

Let me describe a phenomenon that any owner of a portable communication device will recognize. You are sitting quietly. Your device is in your pocket. You feel a vibration — a distinct, unmistakable buzz against your leg. You reach for the device. There is no notification. No message. No call. The device is silent. It has been silent for hours.

You felt it vibrate. It didn't.

This is called "phantom vibration syndrome," and studies suggest that up to 89% of device users experience it regularly. Nearly nine out of ten people routinely feel something that is not happening. Their nervous system generates a sensation. Their brain interprets it as meaningful. Their hand reaches for the pocket. And they find nothing.

I propose that God is a phantom vibration.

I propose that the human brain — a three-pound organ that evolved primarily to keep a mid-sized primate alive on a savannah full of things that wanted to eat it — is spectacularly, magnificently, *prodigiously* bad at determining what is real.

This is not a flaw. This is a feature. On the savannah, it was better to mistake a shadow for a predator than to mistake a predator for a shadow. The first error costs you a moment of unnecessary fear. The second error costs you your life. Natural selection, therefore, strongly favoured brains that detect patterns, intentions, and presences even when they're not there. We are descended from the anxious ones. The ones who heard rustling and ran. The ones who saw faces in the clouds and assumed they were being watched.[^2]

We are, as a species, *wired to feel presences that don't exist*.

And we are surprised — somehow, still, after all these millennia — that we feel the presence of God?

Consider the conditions under which people most commonly report experiencing God's presence:

**In prayer.** The subject sits quietly, often in a darkened room, often alone, often with eyes closed, often repeating words or phrases in a rhythmic pattern. This is also a description of meditation, self-hypnosis, and certain forms of sensory deprivation. The brain, deprived of external stimulation, does what it always does: it *makes things up*. It generates patterns from noise. It finds signal in static. It feels a presence in an empty room, because an empty room, to a brain evolved for the savannah, is a room where something might be hiding.

**In moments of crisis.** The subject is under extreme stress. Adrenaline is flooding the system. The brain is in survival mode, scanning desperately for any source of help, any sign that it is not alone. Under these conditions, the brain will find help even if none exists. Soldiers under fire report conversations with dead comrades. Mountaineers near death report being guided by invisible companions. Sailors lost at sea report voices telling them which way to swim. These experiences are real in the sense that the brain really generates them. They are not real in the sense that dead comrades, invisible companions, and disembodied voices are actually present.

**In beautiful natural settings.** Sunsets. Mountains. Starlit skies. The subject stands before something vast and feels, correctly, small. The brain interprets this smallness as the presence of something larger. This is awe. Awe is a real emotion. It is a real neurological event. It is not evidence that the sunset was painted by someone.

**In church.** The subject is in a building specifically designed — by architects, not by God — to produce feelings of transcendence. High ceilings. Filtered light. Echoing acoustics. Ritual. Music. Incense. Community. Every sensory input has been carefully orchestrated to make the subject feel something. That they then feel something is not a miracle. It is acoustics.[^3]

The theologian says: "But these experiences are so powerful. So overwhelming. So *real*. Surely they must correspond to something."

They do correspond to something. They correspond to the brain doing what the brain does: manufacturing experience from raw sensation, finding meaning in noise, detecting agency where there is none. This is not a failure of the brain. This is the brain's primary function. The brain is a meaning-making machine. It makes meaning the way the liver makes bile — automatically, constantly, and not always usefully.

The question is not whether people have powerful experiences. They do. The question is whether powerful experiences constitute evidence.

They don't.

Powerful experiences constitute evidence that you are a human being with a human brain. That is all. A man who hears voices is having a powerful experience. We do not conclude that invisible people are talking to him. A woman who sees patterns in random data is having a powerful experience. We do not conclude that the data is trying to tell her something. A child who feels a monster under the bed is having a powerful experience. We do not send for an exterminator.

We send for a light.

And when we turn on the light, we find: no monster. No invisible people. No hidden message in the data. Just a brain, doing its job, in the dark, alone.

I want to be gentle here, because I know what these experiences mean to people. I know that for many, the feeling of God's presence is the most important thing in their lives. It gives them comfort. It gives them purpose. It gives them the sense that they are not alone in a universe that is — let's be honest — almost entirely empty, almost entirely dark, and almost entirely indifferent to their existence.

I do not want to take that away.

But I also cannot pretend that feeling something makes it real. I have felt many things. I have felt certain that I left the stove on. I have felt certain that someone was standing behind me in an empty room. I have felt, on one memorable occasion, certain that I could fly, following an ill-advised experiment with Gargle Blaster cocktails. In each case, the feeling was real. In each case, the thing I felt was not.

The argument from personal experience is not an argument. It is a report. It says, "I felt this." It does not say, "This exists." The distance between those two statements is the distance between the savannah and the stars, between the brain and the universe, between what we hope is true and what is.

People say they've felt God's presence.

People also say they've felt their phone vibrate when it hasn't.

I see no reason to take the first claim more seriously than the second.[^4]

The phone, at least, exists.

[^1]: I realize I am treading on sacred ground. This is, I am told, exactly the kind of ground one should not tread on. But sacred ground, in my experience, is simply ground that no one has been willing to dig up. One wonders what's buried underneath.

[^2]: The technical term for this is "hyperactive agency detection." The colloquial term is "being jumpy." The theological term is "faith." I leave it to the reader to determine which label they prefer.

[^3]: The great cathedrals of Earth — and their equivalents on a thousand other planets — are, in engineering terms, enormous machines for producing awe. They are brilliantly effective. But so is a roller coaster, and no one mistakes a roller coaster for evidence of God, although the screaming is similar.

[^4]: I anticipate the objection: "But God is not a phone." No. A phone is a small device that, despite its flaws, reliably connects you to other people. God is — allegedly — an infinite device that, despite His omnipotence, has never reliably connected anyone to anything. In terms of functionality, the phone wins.
