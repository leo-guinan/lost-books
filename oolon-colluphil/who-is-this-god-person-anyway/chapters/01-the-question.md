# Chapter 1: The Question

*In which the author, having spent two books cataloguing divine errors, pauses to consider who, exactly, has been making them.*

---

We have established, over the course of two previous volumes and approximately four hundred pages of increasingly uncomfortable evidence, that God has made mistakes.

Not small mistakes. Not the kind of mistakes you make when you're tired, or distracted, or trying to assemble flat-pack furniture after three glasses of wine. These are mistakes on a cosmological scale. Mistakes that stretch across light-years. Mistakes that have their own gravitational fields.

We have documented the platypus, which appears to have been designed by a committee that was never in the same room. We have examined the human sinuses, which drain upward, as if installed by a plumber who didn't understand which way was down. We have considered the universe's ratio of matter to dark matter and concluded that someone ordered far more darkness than was necessary, like a person panic-buying curtains.[^1]

And through all of this — through every error, every oversight, every catastrophic lapse in quality control — we have been asking the wrong question.

We have been asking: *Where did God go wrong?*

We should have been asking: *Who goes wrong like this?*

Because the pattern of errors tells us something. It tells us something important. It tells us something that, once you see it, you cannot unsee, like the face in the bathroom mirror at three in the morning that you suddenly realize has been looking at you slightly too long.

The errors are not the errors of incompetence. An incompetent God would make random mistakes — a leg here, a spare kidney there, perhaps an occasional planet that's slightly too close to its sun. We could forgive incompetence. Incompetence is, after all, very human, and if God made us in His image, we might expect Him to share our talent for getting things mildly wrong.

No. These are the errors of *absence*.[^2]

Consider: when you leave a house empty for long enough, it doesn't simply stay the way it was. Paint peels. Pipes freeze. The garden grows wild and strange. Foxes move into the kitchen. The house doesn't fall apart because someone broke it. It falls apart because no one is there.

The universe has foxes in the kitchen.

The universe has always had foxes in the kitchen.

And we have been standing in the ruins of this cosmic property, pointing at the water damage and the collapsed ceiling and the family of foxes living in what was once the scullery, saying, "The landlord really ought to fix this." We have been writing letters. We have been filing complaints. We have been leaving messages with the management company.

No one has called back.

No one has *ever* called back.

At some point — and I appreciate this is uncomfortable — we must consider the possibility that there is no landlord. That there never was a landlord. That the house built itself, badly, in the way that houses do when no one is watching, which is to say: it didn't build itself at all. It simply *happened*, the way weather happens, the way Tuesday happens, the way you find yourself at a party you don't remember being invited to, holding a drink you don't remember ordering, talking to someone who doesn't remember their own name.[^3]

"But," says the theologian, adjusting his robes and his expression of professional certainty, "someone must have started it all."

Must they? Why? Because things that exist must have been created? Very well. Then who created the creator? And who created *that* creator? And so on, turtles all the way down, except they're not turtles, they're gods, and each one is slightly more embarrassed than the last to be caught standing on the one below.

"But," says the theologian, now adjusting his robes more nervously, "the universe shows signs of design."

Yes. It does. It shows signs of design the way a car crash shows signs of driving. Something was clearly *intended*. What actually happened is a different matter entirely.

"But," says the theologian, who is now mostly robes and very little certainty, "people have *felt* God."

People have also felt that they were being watched in empty rooms. People have felt that their toast bore the likeness of a deity. People have felt, with absolute conviction, that this time the lottery numbers would come up. The human capacity for feeling things that aren't there is, if anything, evidence *against* the existence of God, because it demonstrates that we are exactly the kind of species that would invent one.

So let us ask the question.

Not "where did God go wrong" — we've covered that.
Not "what are God's greatest mistakes" — we've catalogued those.

Let us ask: *Who is this God person anyway?*

And let us consider the possibility — gently, carefully, the way you'd tell a child that their imaginary friend isn't coming to dinner — that the answer is: *nobody*.

Not nobody important. Not nobody worth mentioning. *Nobody*. The absence of a person. A gap in the shape of an answer. The silence after a question that no one was there to hear.

I don't say this with triumph. I don't say it with glee. I have spent a considerable portion of my career addressing someone who, it increasingly appears, isn't there, and there is nothing triumphant about talking to an empty room. There is only the slow, dawning realization that the room has always been empty, and the voice you thought you heard was an echo of your own.

In the chapters that follow, I will examine God as if He were applying for a job — because, in a sense, He is. He is applying for the position of Supreme Being, Creator of All Things, Author of Existence. It is the most important job in the universe. It is the *only* job in the universe, in the sense that all other jobs ultimately depend on someone having done this one first.

I intend to review His qualifications. His references. His personality. His track record.

And I intend to demonstrate — with whatever tools philosophy affords me, which admittedly aren't many, mostly just logic and a willingness to be unpopular — that the position is vacant.

That it has always been vacant.

That the universe is, and always has been, under *no management whatsoever*.[^4]

Which explains, now that I think about it, quite a lot.

[^1]: There is approximately five times more dark matter in the universe than regular matter. This is like building a house that is five parts foundation and one part house. You can live in it, technically, but you'll spend most of your time in the basement wondering where the windows went.

[^2]: I use the word "absence" advisedly. My editor suggested "negligence," which implies someone was there to be negligent. My lawyer suggested I use neither word. My cat suggested nothing, being a cat, but his expression conveyed a general sense of philosophical detachment that I found more helpful than either.

[^3]: This is, I am told, what most parties are like. I wouldn't know. I am rarely invited to parties. This may be because of my books, or it may be because of my personality. The two are, at this point, essentially the same thing.

[^4]: I can already hear the theologians sharpening their arguments. I invite them to sharpen away. Arguments, like knives, are only dangerous if someone is there to wield them, and I have increasing doubts about the swordsman.
