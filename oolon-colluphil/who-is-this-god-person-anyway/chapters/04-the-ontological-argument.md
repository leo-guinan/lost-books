# Chapter 4: The Ontological Argument (Dismantled Over Lunch)

*In which the author takes Anselm's proof to a restaurant and watches it fail to survive the appetizer.*

---

Of all the arguments for God's existence, the ontological argument is the most elegant, the most audacious, and the most completely wrong. It is wrong in the way that a beautifully constructed paper airplane is wrong when thrown off a cliff and expected to reach orbit — the engineering is admirable; the ambition is breathtaking; the result is a gentle downward spiral followed by a long silence.

The argument, first proposed by Anselm of Canterbury — a monk on a small, damp island on a small, damp planet, who nevertheless believed he had solved the largest question in the universe using nothing but his own mind, which is either very brave or very Canterbury — goes as follows:

1. God is that than which nothing greater can be conceived.
2. It is greater to exist in reality than merely in the mind.
3. Therefore, if God exists only in the mind, we can conceive of something greater — namely, a God who exists in reality.
4. But this contradicts premise 1.
5. Therefore, God exists in reality.

It is a beautiful piece of logic. It is the philosophical equivalent of pulling a rabbit from a hat. And like all magic tricks, it works only if you don't look too closely at the hat.[^1]

I decided to examine it over lunch.

Not metaphorical lunch. Actual lunch. At a small restaurant on the outskirts of Maximegalon City called "The Condition," which serves a seven-course tasting menu and has a wine list longer than most religious texts and considerably more internally consistent. I find that arguments are best examined alongside food, because food is real in a way that arguments frequently aren't, and because a good meal provides natural pauses in which to think, while a bad meal provides natural pauses in which to reconsider one's life choices, which amounts to the same thing.

---

**Course One: Amuse-Bouche**
*A single olive, stuffed with anchovy paste, on a very small plate.*

Anselm's first premise: God is that than which nothing greater can be conceived.

Very well. But who decides what is "great"? Anselm does. A medieval monk decides what constitutes the greatest conceivable being and then proves that this being exists. This is like deciding that the perfect restaurant would serve unlimited free wine, and then concluding that such a restaurant must exist because you can conceive of it.

The olive was excellent. It did not prove anything.

**Course Two: Soup**
*A consommé of clear broth with a single floating herb.*

Anselm's second premise: it is greater to exist in reality than merely in the mind.

Is it? Is it really?

I can conceive of a holiday that lasts forever, where the weather is always perfect and no one tries to sell me anything on the beach. This holiday is, in my mind, extremely great. If it existed in reality, it would involve sunburn, sand in uncomfortable places, and an eventual, crushing boredom. The real version would be *less great* than the imagined version.

Existence does not automatically improve things. Existence, in fact, frequently makes things worse. Anyone who has ever met their heroes, visited a place they'd only seen in photographs, or read the sequel to a book they loved will confirm this. Reality is where great things go to become merely adequate.[^2]

The soup was adequate. In my mind, it had been great.

**Course Three: Fish**
*Pan-seared Arcturan bass with a foam of something uncertain.*

The logical leap: if God exists only in the mind, we can conceive of something greater — a God who exists in reality.

But we've just established that existing in reality doesn't necessarily make something greater. So the leap doesn't work. We *can* conceive of a God who exists in reality, but we can also conceive of a God who doesn't bother. A God who looked at reality, with its entropy and its suffering and its Arcturan bass foam, and said, "No thank you. I shall remain theoretical."

If the greatest conceivable being can be conceived, it can also be conceived as *not bothering*.

And frankly, if I were the greatest conceivable being, I wouldn't bother either. Have you *seen* reality? It's mostly vacuum. The bits that aren't vacuum are mostly on fire. And the tiny fraction that is neither vacuum nor fire is populated largely by beings who argue about whether or not I exist, which is exactly the sort of thing I'd want to avoid by staying conceptual.

The fish was real. It wasn't great. Point proven.[^3]

**Course Four: Sorbet**
*A palate cleanser of lemon and existential doubt.*

Let us consider Gaunilo's island — the famous rebuttal. If Anselm's logic works for God, it should work for anything. The greatest conceivable island must exist, because an island that exists is greater than one that doesn't. The greatest conceivable sandwich must exist. The greatest conceivable parking space must exist.

They don't. We know they don't. I have looked for the greatest conceivable parking space on three different planets and it is always taken.

Anselm's reply to Gaunilo was, essentially, that the argument only works for God because God is a special case. This is the philosophical equivalent of saying, "The rules apply to everyone except my friend." It is not an argument. It is a favour.

The sorbet was sharp. So was Gaunilo. Anselm was not.

**Course Five: Meat**
*A slow-roasted something with a reduction of something else.*

The Cartesian version: I think, therefore I am. I can conceive of a perfect being. This idea must come from somewhere. I'm imperfect, so it can't come from me. Therefore, a perfect being must have placed the idea in my mind.

Descartes, who was in most respects a very intelligent man, here commits the error of assuming that imperfect beings cannot generate perfect ideas. But we do it constantly. I can imagine a perfect circle despite never having seen one. I can imagine a world without suffering despite living in one that is lousy with it. The human mind's entire *purpose* is to imagine things that don't exist. It's practically the only thing we're good at.

Also: if God placed the idea of Himself in our minds to prove His own existence, He has essentially written His own reference letter. Which brings us back to the CV problem.

The meat was overdone. So was Descartes.

**Course Six: Cheese**
*A selection of three cheeses, two of which I recognized and one of which appeared to be watching me.*

The modal ontological argument, as updated by Plantinga: it is *possible* that a maximally great being exists. If it is possible, then in some possible world, it exists. If it exists in some possible world, it exists in all possible worlds. Therefore it exists in this one.

This is the cleverest version of the argument, and also the most dishonest. It smuggles the conclusion into the first premise. "It is possible that a maximally great being exists" *already contains* the conclusion, because a "maximally great being" is defined as one that exists in all possible worlds. Saying "it's possible that a being which exists in all possible worlds exists" is just saying "it's possible that a necessary thing is necessary," which is circular, which is not an argument but a revolving door.

The cheese that was watching me blinked. I ate it anyway. It was not an argument for the existence of cheese. It was just cheese.

**Course Seven: Dessert**
*A chocolate fondant with a liquid centre, described on the menu as "a study in impermanence."*

And here we arrive at the fundamental problem with all ontological arguments: they attempt to think God into existence. They begin with a concept and end with a being. They treat logic as if it were a conjuring spell — arrange the words correctly, follow the steps precisely, and reality will rearrange itself to accommodate your conclusion.

But logic does not create. Logic describes. You can prove, logically, that a triangle has three sides. You cannot, logically, conjure a triangle into the room. The triangle must already be there. Logic can examine it. Logic cannot *summon* it.

If God exists, the ontological argument is a description of something real.

If God does not exist, the ontological argument is a very clever poem about nothing.

The fondant was excellent. Its liquid centre was real, warm, and present. It did not require a logical proof. It required only a spoon.

I think there may be a lesson in that.

---

I paid the bill. It was considerable but finite, unlike most theological arguments, which are considerable but go on forever.[^4]

On the way out, I passed a man at the bar who was arguing with his reflection in the mirror behind the bottles. He was making excellent points. His reflection was making them right back. Neither of them was getting anywhere.

I thought of Anselm.

I went home.
