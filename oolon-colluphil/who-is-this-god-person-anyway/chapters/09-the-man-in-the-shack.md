# Chapter 9: The Man in the Shack

*In which the author finds something he was not looking for.*

---

On a small planet — not the smallest, but small enough that you could walk around it in a lifetime if you didn't stop for meals, which you would, because the planet had excellent meals — there lived a man in a shack.

The shack sat on a hill above a valley. The valley had a river. The river had fish. The fish did not have opinions about anything, which made them, in the man's estimation, the most reasonable creatures on the planet.

The man's name is not important. He would have told you so himself. He would have said, "Names are what other people use to find you, and I am not looking to be found." He lived alone. He had lived alone for a long time — long enough that the solitude had stopped feeling like an absence and started feeling like a presence, the way silence in a room can become so complete that it starts to sound like something.

He did not believe in God.

He did not believe in God the way most atheists don't believe in God — not with arguments, not with proofs, not with cleverness or anger or the satisfied smile of someone who has won a debate. He didn't believe in God the way he didn't believe in the land beyond the mountains to the west: not because he had evidence against it, but because he had never been there, and the not-being-there was simply a fact, like the weather, like his own hands, like the fish.

He had not read my books. He had not read anyone's books. He had a small shelf with a few volumes on it — a manual for repairing the kind of stove he owned, a guide to the edible plants of the valley, and one novel that he had started three times and never finished because, he said, "it keeps trying to tell me something, and I'm not in the mood to be told."

He was not a philosopher. He was not a theologian. He was not a mystic or a hermit in the romantic sense — he did not live alone because he was seeking enlightenment. He lived alone because he liked it. He liked the sound of the river. He liked the way the light came through the window in the morning and made a rectangle on the floor that moved slowly across the room as the day went on. He liked that no one asked him questions.

I asked him questions. I'm afraid I couldn't help it. It's what I do.

"Do you think about God?" I asked.

He considered this. He was a man who considered things — not quickly, not with the anxious, performative speed of someone who wants you to know they're thinking, but slowly, the way a river considers a stone: by going around it, wearing it down, and eventually forgetting it was there.

"No," he said.

"Not at all?"

"No." He poured tea. The tea was made from something that grew by the river. It tasted like the color green, if the color green had been steeped in warm water and was slightly bitter. "Why would I?"

I explained my work. The trilogy. The proofs. The dismantling. The Babel fish. The puff of logic. I told him everything, and as I told him, I watched his face, expecting — I don't know what I expected. Surprise, perhaps. Interest. The satisfaction of hearing your own position articulated by someone who had spent a career arriving at it.

His face did nothing.

"That's a lot of work," he said, "to get to where I started."

And there it was. The thing I had come all this way — three books, thousands of pages, a lifetime of arguments — to find. Not an argument. Not a proof. Not a rebuttal or a revelation. Just a man sitting in a shack, drinking green tea, who had arrived at the end of the longest road in philosophy by never setting foot on it.

He didn't not-believe in God as a conclusion. He not-believed in God as a *starting condition*. The way you don't believe in a color you've never seen, or a sound you've never heard. It wasn't a position. It was simply the shape of his life, the way water is the shape of whatever you pour it into.

I stayed for two days. He didn't mind. He didn't especially welcome me, either. I was there the way the weather was there — a condition of the day, neither good nor bad.

In the morning he fished. He stood in the river with the water around his ankles and waited. The waiting was the thing. Not the catching. He caught a fish about once every three hours, and when he did, he looked at it with a mild interest — not the triumph of a sportsman, not the gratitude of a man thanking providence, just the interest of a person observing something that had happened.

"The fish don't know you're there," I said.

"I know," he said. "That's the best part."

In the afternoon he sat on the step of his shack and did nothing. I don't mean he was idle. Idle implies that there is something he should have been doing instead. He was not idle. He was complete. He sat on the step and the sun was warm and the valley was quiet and the river made its sound, and he was a man sitting on a step, and that was enough. It was the whole thing. There was nothing missing.

I have spent my career identifying what is missing. The missing designer. The missing purpose. The missing manager. The missing God. I have built a body of work on absence — on pointing at the empty chair and saying, "Look. No one is sitting there."

This man was not pointing at the empty chair. He had never noticed the chair. He was sitting on his own step, in his own sun, and the question of whether someone was sitting in some other chair somewhere else in the universe had simply never occurred to him, because he was busy being alive, which is a full-time occupation if you let it be.

On the second evening, I asked him the question that I had been asking, in one form or another, for my entire professional life.

"Doesn't it bother you? The emptiness? The idea that there's no one... watching?"

He was quiet for a while. The sun was going down. The light in the valley was turning gold, then amber, then something that wasn't quite a color — more a feeling, or a temperature, or the memory of warmth after the warmth has gone.

"Who said it's empty?" he said.

I opened my mouth.

"I don't mean God," he said. "I mean — look." He gestured at the valley. The river. The birds doing something complicated in the trees. The fish, presumably, doing something simple in the water. The sky, enormous, indifferent, streaked with the last light of a day that had been, by any reasonable measure, uneventful.

"It's full," he said. "It's completely full. There's a river and it's full of fish and the fish are full of whatever fish are full of. There are trees and they're full of birds. There's air and it's full of the sound of the river and the birds and the wind. There's me and I'm full of tea."

He smiled. It was a small smile — not a grin, not a beam, just the slight upward adjustment of someone who has said something that is true and is mildly pleased about it.

"People keep looking for the thing that's missing," he said. "They look and look and look. They build telescopes to look farther. They write books about what they can't find."

I felt this was directed at me. It was.

"What if nothing's missing?" he said. "What if this is just what a full universe looks like? And it looks like this — messy and broken and ordinary — and that's all right? What if the universe doesn't need anyone watching it, the same way the river doesn't need anyone watching it? It just goes."

"And us?" I said.

"We just go too," he said.

The sun finished setting. The stars came out — not all at once, but gradually, like a crowd assembling, each one arriving at its own pace, none of them coordinated, none of them planned, all of them there.

I left the next morning. The man was already at the river, standing in the water, waiting for a fish. He didn't say goodbye. Not because he was rude, but because he didn't experience my leaving as a departure. I was there, and then I was not there, and the river kept going.

I walked for a long time. Through the valley, over the hills, back toward civilization, back toward universities and arguments and people who would want to know what I had found.

I had found a man in a shack who didn't believe in anything and didn't need to. The universe was his, not because he claimed it, but because he was in it — the same way a fish is in a river, the same way a star is in the sky, the same way a man is in his life: completely, without remainder, without apology, without the need for anyone to be watching.

The universe didn't mind.

It never had.

---

I walked back to the spaceport, caught a shuttle to the nearest hub, and from there took a ship back to Maximegalon. The journey took three days. I spent most of it sitting by a window, watching the stars, thinking about nothing in particular.

When I got home, my cat was sitting on the manuscript of this book. He looked at me with the expression that cats have perfected over millennia — an expression that says, simultaneously, "I am aware of your existence" and "I have no strong feelings about it."

"I think I've finished," I told him.

He blinked.

"The book," I said. "The trilogy. The whole thing. I think I've said everything I needed to say."

He blinked again, then stood up, turned around three times, and sat back down on the manuscript.

"You're right," I said. "It could probably use a better ending."

But it couldn't. Because the only honest ending is this one: a man coming home from a long journey, sitting down in a quiet room, and realizing that the quiet was always the answer. Not the arguments. Not the proofs. Not the dismantling and the rebuilding and the puffs of logic. The quiet. The river. The fish. The stars coming out one by one, with no one to arrange them.

This is the end of the trilogy. Not with a bang. Not with a proof. Not with God vanishing in a puff of anything.

Just a man, and a cat, and a manuscript, and the sound of a universe that has no idea what it's doing but is doing it anyway, with considerable enthusiasm, and will continue to do it long after the man and the cat and the manuscript are gone.

Which is, when you think about it, the most miraculous thing of all.

The cat purred. Outside, it started to rain. The rain didn't mean anything.

It was good rain, though.
