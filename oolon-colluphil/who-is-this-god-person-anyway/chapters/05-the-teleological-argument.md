# Chapter 5: The Teleological Argument (Destroyed by a Giraffe)

*In which the author examines the argument from design and discovers that, if anything was designed, the designer was not sober.*

---

The teleological argument — from the Greek *telos*, meaning "purpose," and *logos*, meaning "the sort of word that makes people think you're clever" — goes like this:

The universe appears designed. Design implies a designer. Therefore, a designer exists.

It is the most popular argument for God's existence, and the most intuitive, because it appeals not to logic, which most people distrust, but to the gut feeling that someone, somewhere, must have *meant* all this. It is the argument of the person who looks at a sunset and says, "Someone must have painted that." It is also, unfortunately, the argument of the person who looks at a volcanic eruption and says, "Someone must have painted *that*," and then has to explain why the painter is trying to kill them.

I want to take the argument seriously. I want to engage with it on its own terms. And so I shall examine the evidence of design — the actual, physical, biological evidence — and see what it tells us about the alleged designer.[^1]

Let us begin with the giraffe.

The giraffe has a laryngeal nerve. This nerve connects the brain to the larynx, which is in the throat. The brain is also in the vicinity of the throat, being located, as brains generally are, in the head. The distance between the brain and the larynx, in a giraffe, is a few inches.

The laryngeal nerve does not travel a few inches.

The laryngeal nerve travels from the brain, down the entire length of the giraffe's neck, loops around the aorta near the heart, and then travels *back up* the entire length of the neck to the larynx. In a large giraffe, this means the nerve — connecting two points that are inches apart — takes a detour of approximately fifteen feet.

Fifteen feet. Of unnecessary nerve. In an animal whose most notable feature is *having a very long neck*, which makes this particular design flaw *as visible as it could possibly be*.

This is not design. This is what happens when you copy the blueprint from a fish — where the nerve routing made perfect sense because there was no neck to go around — and then just... stretch it. Without redesigning anything. Without reviewing the schematics. Without, apparently, looking at what you'd made.[^2]

Design implies a designer. *This* design implies the designer was drunk.

Or absent. Or — and I realize we keep arriving at this same conclusion, like a traveler who keeps finding himself back at the same crossroads — nonexistent.

But the giraffe is merely the appetizer. Let me introduce you to the human knee.

The human knee is a joint that bears the entire weight of the body during walking, running, jumping, climbing, and that thing where you stand up too quickly and everything goes briefly wrong. It is, structurally, the most important joint in the human body. It is also, structurally, a disaster.

The knee is held together by four ligaments, any one of which can snap during activities as strenuous as "turning slightly." It contains cartilage that wears away and cannot be regrown. It has a patella — the kneecap — that can dislocate if you look at it wrong. It is simultaneously load-bearing and fragile, which is a combination you normally only find in furniture from the cheapest possible catalogue.

If you submitted the human knee as an engineering project, you would fail. Not because the idea is bad — a hinge joint for bipedal locomotion is a reasonable concept — but because the execution is so poor that it constitutes evidence of either gross negligence or a fundamental misunderstanding of what knees are for.[^3]

And then there is the human eye.

The human eye is often cited by proponents of intelligent design as evidence of a brilliant creator. "Look at the eye!" they say. "So complex! So elegant! Surely only a master craftsman could have—"

The retina is installed backward.

The photoreceptor cells face *away* from the incoming light, toward the back of the eye. The light has to pass through a layer of blood vessels and nerve fibers before reaching the cells that detect it. This is like building a camera with the lens facing the photographer and the film facing outward. It works — the eye does, technically, see — but it works *despite* its design, not because of it.

Additionally, the backward installation creates a blind spot where the optic nerve passes through the retina — a hole in your vision that your brain quietly fills in with a guess, so that you never notice you're partially blind. Your own visual system is *lying to you* to compensate for a manufacturing defect.

And the eye is the *good* example. The eye is what intelligent design proponents *brag about*. If this is the showcase, I dread the inventory.

Let me take you through it. Briefly. I'll try to be restrained.

The human spine: designed for quadrupedal locomotion, repurposed for bipedal locomotion without a redesign, resulting in the single greatest source of chronic pain in human history. An entire medical specialty exists because the spine is bad at its job.

The human throat: eating and breathing share the same tube, meaning you can die from eating too fast. This is a design that kills its users. If it were a product, it would be recalled.

The appendix: a small pouch attached to the large intestine that serves no known purpose except to occasionally become infected and explode. It is the biological equivalent of a small bomb that the designer left inside the product and forgot about.

The recurrent laryngeal nerve — yes, again, but this time in humans: it still loops around the aorta, even though in humans the detour is only a few unnecessary inches. It's not as dramatic as in the giraffe, but it's the same fundamental error, repeated across species, without correction. This is not design. This is a copy-paste error propagated through millions of years by a developer who never does code review because there is no one to review it because *He works alone*.

The proponents of intelligent design look at all of this and say: "The designer works in mysterious ways."

No. There is nothing mysterious about this. The "designer" — if we must use the word — works in exactly the way that a blind, iterative, purposeless process works: by accumulating small changes over vast periods of time, keeping the ones that work well enough, and dragging along the ones that don't because there's no one checking. Evolution doesn't design. Evolution *accumulates*. And the result looks exactly like what you'd expect from accumulation without oversight: a universe full of things that mostly work, badly, with no warranty.

Design implies a designer.

The giraffe's laryngeal nerve implies no designer.

The human knee implies no designer.

The backward retina implies no designer.

The shared eating-breathing tube that periodically kills its users implies, if anything, an *anti-designer* — a being whose primary goal was to demonstrate what happens when you don't have a designer.

And the beauty? The sunsets, the fractals, the spiraling galaxies, the astonishing complexity of a single living cell? That beauty is real. I don't deny it. But beauty does not require intent. Snowflakes are beautiful, and no one designed them. Spirals appear in galaxies and in seashells and in the arrangement of seeds in a sunflower, not because someone put them there but because mathematics is beautiful and physics follows mathematics whether anyone is watching or not.

The universe is beautiful the way a forest fire is beautiful: genuinely, breathtakingly, and entirely without purpose.

We are left, as always, with the same conclusion. The argument from design doesn't prove God exists. It proves that if He does, He should be embarrassed.

And if He doesn't — if there is no designer, no craftsman, no architect — then the universe's flaws are not failures. They are simply features of a system that was never designed in the first place. And there is something honest about that. Something almost freeing.

The giraffe doesn't know its nerve is fifteen feet too long. It just lives its life, eating leaves from the tops of trees, unbothered by the absurdity of its own anatomy.

Perhaps there's a lesson in that too.

---

[^1]: I should note that I am not a biologist. I am a philosopher. But the nice thing about the teleological argument is that you don't need to be a biologist to refute it. You just need to look at a giraffe. Carefully. For a long time. Which I have done. The giraffe did not enjoy it.

[^2]: Evolutionary biologists call this "historical constraint." Theologians call it "mysterious ways." I call it "not reading the blueprint," which in my experience is the most common cause of design failures in any field, divine or otherwise.

[^3]: I have purchased furniture from such catalogues. A bookshelf I bought from Cheapside Furnishings on Barnard's Star collapsed under the weight of, ironically, my first two books about God's mistakes. The knee has a similar structural relationship with its intended load: technically rated for it, practically doomed.
