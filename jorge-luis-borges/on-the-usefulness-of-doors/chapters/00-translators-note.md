# Translator's Note

The difficulties of translating Borges are well established and do not require my elaboration. Others more skilled than I have mapped that particular labyrinth. What I wish to address here is a problem specific to this text, which I encountered on the first page and which pursued me, with increasing insistence, to the last.

The word is *umbral*.

In Spanish, *umbral* means threshold — the strip of wood or stone at the bottom of a doorway, the line between one room and the next, the place where you stand when you are neither inside nor outside. It is a precise, architectural word. But Borges uses it forty-seven times in this manuscript, and I am not confident it means the same thing twice.

In the first chapter, *umbral* is literal: the physical threshold of a door, the oldest technology. By the third chapter, it has become metaphysical — the threshold between one version of a life and another. In the fifth chapter, it is the surface of a mirror. In the sixth, it is a sound. In the seventh, it is the boundary between being and not-being, which is to say it is the oldest question dressed in carpenter's clothing.

I have translated *umbral* as "threshold" in most instances. In certain passages I have used "doorstep," "brink," "edge," or "limen," depending on the shade of meaning I believed Borges intended. I confess this belief is itself uncertain. A translator works in the space between two languages, which is a kind of hallway connecting two rooms, and the light in that hallway is never adequate.

There is a further difficulty. Borges writes in sentences that are syntactically simple and semantically inexhaustible. The Spanish is clear. The meaning is not. To translate the words is easy. To translate the silence between the words — the pause that Borges places before a revelation, the particular weight of a period at the end of a sentence that has changed the shape of the world — this I cannot claim to have accomplished.

I am aware that the act of translation is itself a door. One walks through it carrying a text in one language and emerges holding something in another language that resembles the original the way a reflection resembles a face — accurately, and yet reversed, and yet not the same, and yet not entirely different.

I have walked through this particular door as carefully as I could. I am not certain I have arrived in the right room. But then, as Borges himself suggests in these pages, certainty about rooms is a luxury afforded only to those who have never opened a door, and such people do not exist, and have never existed, because to be born is already to have walked through one.

— A. M. Estrada
Buenos Aires, 2025
