# V. The Door That Is a Mirror

A door and a mirror are the same object.

Both present a rectangular threshold. Both show you a space beyond. Both invite you to consider what it would mean to cross. But the mirror shows you yourself, standing at the threshold, and this is the difference that makes them opposites, or makes them identical — I have never been able to determine which.

A door is a mirror through which you can pass. A mirror is a door through which you cannot. The door transforms by letting you through. The mirror transforms by keeping you out. In the first case, you become the person on the other side. In the second case, you become the person who cannot reach the other side, which is a different kind of becoming, and perhaps a more honest one.

I have always distrusted mirrors. I have written of this before. They multiply the world unnecessarily. They create the illusion that there is more than one of everything, including more than one of the person looking, and this is a lie so fundamental that we have ceased to recognize it as a lie and have begun to call it decoration.

But a door tells the same lie, more subtly. A door says: there is another room. There is a space you have not been. There is a version of the world that begins on the other side of this wooden rectangle. This, too, is a multiplication — not of images but of possibilities, which is worse, because possibilities, unlike reflections, can be lived.

To see yourself in a mirror is to see the one person who cannot cross. To stand at a door is to be the one person who can. Between these two positions — the seeing and the crossing, the mirror and the door — is the self. The self is the hesitation. The self is the breath before you step through. The self is the space between the looking and the walking, and we live there most of our lives, neither crossing nor reflecting, suspended in the threshold of a door that is also a mirror, seeing ourselves about to move, never moving, always about to.

De Quincey wrote of a door in a dream that opened onto his own face. I believe he was describing not a dream but the ordinary condition of being conscious, which is to stand forever at a door that shows you yourself, unable to open it because you are the door.
