# IV. A Door in Buenos Aires

On Calle México, between Bolívar and Defensa, there was a blue door.

I say "was" not because the door has been removed but because I can no longer see it. My eyes, which once could distinguish the particular blue of that door from the blue of the sky above it and the blue of the shadows beside it, now distinguish nothing. The world has become, for me, a series of gradations of gold and amber, which is the palette of blindness, and which is, I sometimes think, the palette of memory, because memory too reduces the world to a few warm colors and discards the rest.

But I remember the door. It was set into a wall of white plaster that had been white for so long it had become the color of age itself — not yellow, not grey, but the color that white becomes when it has given up on being white. The door was not large. It had no knocker, no nameplate, no number. It was, in every respect, an ordinary door, except that I passed it nearly every day for eleven years and never opened it.

I do not know what was behind it. I have considered the possibilities. A courtyard, probably. Buenos Aires is a city of hidden courtyards — spaces concealed behind unremarkable facades, containing lemon trees, or fountains, or the particular silence that exists only in enclosed spaces in the afternoon. I have entered many such courtyards. They are always smaller than one expects and more beautiful.

But I did not enter this one. I cannot explain why. The door was not locked — or if it was, I never tried the handle to confirm it. I simply walked past it, day after day, year after year, and each time I walked past it I was aware that I was not opening it, which is a different thing from not noticing it, which is a different thing from choosing not to open it. I was doing none of these things. I was performing a fourth action, which has no name, which consists of knowing that a door exists and knowing that you will not open it and continuing to walk past it anyway, and this action, this unnamed thing, is perhaps the most common thing a person does in a life, and the least examined.

I have written about infinite libraries and impossible gardens. I have imagined labyrinths that contain all possible paths and books that contain all possible words. But the most vertiginous infinity I know is the ordinary one — the door I did not open on an ordinary afternoon, which leads to the life I did not live, which is as real as this one, and as lost.

Behind that blue door on Calle México, there is a version of me who entered. He knows the courtyard. He has seen the lemon tree or the fountain or the empty space. He has that knowledge, which I do not, and I have this paragraph, which he does not, and between us is a blue door that neither of us can open now — he because he has already passed through it, and I because I did not, and we are both correct, and we are both incomplete, and the door is shut.
