# III. The Garden of Forking Doors

In the story I published some years ago, the sinologist Stephen Albert reveals that Ts'ui Pên's labyrinth is not a physical maze but a novel — a book in which every possible outcome of every event occurs simultaneously, each branching into further possibilities, so that the book contains not one story but all stories, diverging and converging and diverging again, without end.

I have come to believe that I was wrong. Not about the labyrinth — the labyrinth is a novel, or the labyrinth is time, or the labyrinth is both, and these are the same thing. I was wrong about the architecture. Ts'ui Pên's labyrinth is not a garden of forking paths. It is a house of forking doors.

Consider the difference. A path suggests openness, visibility, the long view. One can see where a path leads, or believes one can. A door suggests enclosure, concealment, the immediate and the hidden. One cannot see what lies behind a door until one opens it. A path forks visibly. A door forks secretly.

The house I now imagine is this: a room containing four doors. Behind each door, another room containing four doors. Behind each of those doors, another room. And so on, without limit, each room identical in dimension but subtly different in content — the light falls from a different angle, or a book on the table has been opened to a different page, or the dust on the windowsill has arranged itself into a different pattern, which is to say that time has passed differently in each room, or that a different version of time is passing, which is, I now believe, what Ts'ui Pên was trying to say.

Some doors lead forward. Some lead back to a room you have already visited, but it has changed in your absence — or you have changed in the visiting — and so it is not the same room, though it occupies the same space and contains the same walls and the same four doors. The man who returns to a room is not the man who left it. He has opened a door in the interval, and the opening of doors is not reversible.

I have tried to map this house. The map is, of course, impossible. To map a house of forking doors would require a map that itself forks at every point, and such a map is not a map but another house, identical to the first, which is either a useless redundancy or a profound truth about the nature of representation.

Ts'ui Pên labored for thirteen years on his labyrinth. His family believed he was writing a novel. His contemporaries believed he was building a maze. He was doing neither, or both. He was building a house with an infinite number of doors, in which every possible version of every possible person opens every possible door and becomes every possible version of themselves, simultaneously, endlessly, without resolution.

We do not choose which door to open. We choose which person to be when we open it. The door is the same. We are not.

This is the secret that Ts'ui Pên encoded in his labyrinth, the secret that Stephen Albert almost grasped, the secret that I, writing about it years later, can articulate but not understand: that a door is not a choice between two rooms. A door is a choice between two selves. The room on the other side is incidental. What matters is who you are when you cross the threshold, because that person — the one who is crossing, the one who is in the act of stepping from here to there — does not exist. They are neither the person who was in the first room nor the person who will be in the second. They are the person in the door, which is no place at all, and which lasts no time at all, and which is, for that imperceptible instant, the only place in the world.

I think Ts'ui Pên spent thirteen years in the door. I think he never crossed. I think the labyrinth is the record of his hesitation, which was infinite, which was the same thing as all the choices made at once, which was the same thing as time.
