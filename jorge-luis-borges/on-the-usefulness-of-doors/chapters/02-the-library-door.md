# II. The Library Door

There is a library that contains every possible book. I have described it elsewhere, in a text that was itself contained in that library long before I wrote it, a fact that disturbs me less than it once did. What I did not describe — what I failed to consider, with an omission that now seems to me either careless or providential — is the door.

The library, as I conceived it, is composed of hexagonal galleries, connected by hallways and ventilation shafts, extending in every direction without limit. Each gallery contains twenty shelves, each shelf thirty-two books, each book four hundred and ten pages. The books contain every possible combination of the twenty-five orthographic symbols. Somewhere in this library is a faithful catalogue of it. Somewhere is a book consisting entirely of the letter M. Somewhere is the true story of your death. Somewhere is a book that differs from that story by a single comma, which changes nothing, or everything.

But consider the doors.

Each gallery connects to the next through a doorway. Each doorway is identical to every other doorway — the same height, the same width, the same absence of distinguishing marks. The librarians, who have wandered these galleries for centuries, have developed no system for telling one door from another, because there is no system to develop. The doors are not merely similar. They are, in every measurable respect, the same.

And yet behind each door is a different room, containing different books, leading to different doors, leading to further rooms. The librarian who steps through one door rather than another has, without knowing it, selected one infinity over another. This is the particular cruelty of identical doors: they make choice invisible. One believes one is merely walking. One is, in fact, deciding everything.

I have imagined the librarian who discovers this. She has been searching, as all librarians search, for the catalogue — the book that will impose order on the chaos, that will tell her which book is in which gallery, that will transform the library from an accident into a system. She has searched for decades. She has opened thousands of doors. And one evening, in a gallery indistinguishable from every other gallery, she realizes that the catalogue, if it exists, is behind a door she has already passed through, or behind a door she has not yet reached, and that she will never know which, because the doors are identical.

She sits down. She does not weep. Weeping is for those who have lost something specific. She has lost something far worse — the possibility of knowing whether she has lost anything at all.

Somewhere in the library — and this is a mathematical certainty, not a fantasy — there is a book that describes the door she is about to open. It describes the weight of her hand on the handle, the angle of her wrist, the particular quality of the air that moves from one gallery to the next as the door swings open. It describes the books on the shelves of the room she is about to enter. It describes her face as she reads them.

There is another book, adjacent to it or infinitely distant from it, that describes the door she decided not to open — the one to her left, or behind her, or the one she passed three galleries ago without noticing. This second book describes the books she would have found, the knowledge she would have gained, the person she would have become. It describes a version of her that is, in every meaningful sense, a different person, leading a different life, in a library that is the same library and yet is experienced as entirely different, because a library is not its books but the order in which you read them, and the order in which you read them is determined by the doors you open.

There is a third book — and this is the one I find most terrible — that contains only the word *door*, repeated without variation for four hundred and ten pages. It means nothing. It is gibberish. And yet it is the most accurate book in the library, because it contains the only word that matters in a library of infinite books, which is the word for the thing that separates you from them.

This is the cruelty of the library: not that it contains everything, but that it contains everything behind identical doors.

The librarians have proposed various solutions. Some have suggested marking the doors — with chalk, with blood, with the particular scuff of a boot heel dragged deliberately across the threshold. But the library is infinite, and any system of marks is finite, and a finite system applied to an infinite space is not a system but a gesture, and the library does not respond to gestures.

Others have proposed destroying doors. But a library without doors is not a library. It is a corridor, and a corridor contains nothing. It merely connects.

The wisest librarians — and I use the word with the full weight of its irony — have proposed ignoring the doors entirely. They walk through them without looking, without choosing, without deliberation. They accept that every door is every other door, and that the room beyond is the room they were meant to find, because there is no room they were meant to find, because meaning is a quality of the searcher and not of the search.

I find this wisdom intolerable. But I suspect it is correct.
