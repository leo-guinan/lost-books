# I. The First Door

Before the wheel, before fire, before the first word spoken by the first human mouth into the first human ear, there was a door.

This is not metaphor. The anthropologists have confirmed what the mythmakers always knew: that the earliest human technology was not a tool for striking or cutting or carrying, but a stone rolled across the mouth of a cave. A thing placed in an opening. A barrier between here and there. The first human invention was not a way to change the world but a way to divide it.

I have spent some years considering this fact, and I have come to regard it as the most important fact in the history of our species. Before we could speak, we could step through. Before we could name the world, we could separate it into two worlds — the world on this side, and the world on that side — and we could choose between them. The door preceded language because the door preceded choice, and choice preceded everything.

The Babylonians understood this. In the *Kitāb al-Abwāb*, the Book of Doors, which the Persian scholar Ahmad ibn Khalaf attributed to the priests of Ur in his twelfth-century commentary on architectural thresholds, the first door was not built but discovered. The god Anu — or the figure whom the fragmentary tablets call Anu, though the name may be a corruption of an older word meaning simply "the one who stands between" — found a crack in the firmament. On one side was the ordered world. On the other side was everything else, which is to say: possibility, chaos, the unformed. Anu did not create the crack. He placed himself in it. He became the door.

This is the first and perhaps the only theology of doors: that the door is not an object but a position. A door is what happens when someone stands between two spaces and decides which one to enter.

The Greeks, characteristically, gave the idea a face. Two faces, in fact. Janus, whom the Romans inherited and renamed, is always depicted looking in two directions — forward and back, inside and outside, toward the future and toward the past. He is the god of beginnings, of endings, of transitions, of doorways. His temple in Rome was open in times of war and closed in times of peace, which is an inversion of every domestic door in history: we close our doors against war, against cold, against the stranger. The temple of Janus was closed only twice in seven hundred years of Roman civilization. One may draw from this whatever conclusions one wishes about civilization.

Homer knew what doors were for. In the nineteenth book of the *Odyssey*, Penelope speaks of two gates through which dreams must pass to reach the sleeping world. The gate of ivory lets through the dreams that deceive. The gate of horn lets through the dreams that come true. The distinction is architectural: truth and falsehood are not qualities of the dream itself but of the door through which it enters. The same dream, passing through a different gate, becomes its opposite. This suggests something about doors that I have always found vertiginous — that the door does not merely connect two rooms. It transforms whatever passes through it. You are one thing on this side. You are another thing on that side. The door is the mechanism of the transformation.

The Jewish tradition inscribes this knowledge on the doorpost itself. The mezuzah — a small case containing verses from Deuteronomy, affixed to the right side of the doorframe — serves as a reminder to those who pass beneath it. A reminder of what? The verses speak of loving God with all one's heart, which is a way of saying: be complete when you cross. Do not leave part of yourself behind. But of course one always leaves part of oneself behind. That is what a door does. It divides not only space but the person who crosses it. The self that enters is not the self that exits. The mezuzah marks the place where this small death occurs and sanctifies it, which is the only honest response to a door — to acknowledge that it is, in its quiet way, lethal.

I have studied the doors of many civilizations, and I have arrived at a theory that I suspect is either trivially obvious or profoundly true, which is often the same thing. A civilization can be understood entirely by its doors.

A culture that builds arches believes in welcome. A culture that builds walls with no doors believes in fear. A culture that builds revolving doors believes in neither — only in the circulation of bodies, which is commerce.

The Egyptians built doors for the dead — false doors, carved in stone, leading nowhere, or rather leading to the place where the dead go, which is nowhere we can follow. These doors were painted with extraordinary care. They had lintels and jambs and thresholds, all rendered in meticulous detail on flat stone. They were, in every respect, perfect doors, except that they could not be opened. One may argue that this makes them the most honest doors ever built. Every door promises a passage it may not deliver. The Egyptian false door simply makes this promise explicit, and then breaks it explicitly, which is a form of integrity rare in architecture and rarer still in life.

The Chinese, according to the geomancer's tradition recorded in the *Yangzhai Sanyao*, understood that a door is a mouth. The house breathes through it. A door that faces the correct direction inhales fortune; a door that faces the wrong direction exhales it. This is not, I think, superstition. It is observation dressed in the clothing of metaphor. A door does determine what enters and what leaves. A door that opens onto a garden admits light, air, the scent of jasmine. A door that opens onto a wall admits nothing. The Chinese merely took this observation to its logical and perhaps its illogical conclusion.

I could continue. The doors of cathedrals, designed to make the entering body feel small. The doors of prisons, designed to make the exiting body feel impossible. The doors of bedrooms, which are the most private doors, and therefore the most frightening, because what they enclose is not a room but a self, and a self observed is a self transformed, and we are back again at the gate of horn and the gate of ivory, and the question of which door the truth comes through.

But I have written enough about other people's doors. The chapters that follow concern doors that I have imagined, and doors that I have remembered, and doors that I have stood before without opening, which is perhaps the most common relationship a person has with a door, and the one least discussed.

I will say only this, as a final preliminary: a door is the smallest possible unit of narrative. A story requires a change of state. A character must go from one condition to another. A door is the architectural expression of this requirement — the minimum apparatus needed for a thing to become a different thing. Every story ever told is a door. Every door ever built is a story.

The rest is passage.
