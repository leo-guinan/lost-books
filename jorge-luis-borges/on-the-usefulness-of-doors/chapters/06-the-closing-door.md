# VI. The Closing Door

There is a sound that a door makes when it closes.

It is not the sound of wood against wood, or of metal against metal, though it contains these. It is the sound of separation — the acoustic signature of a space becoming two spaces. It is the only sound I know that means both ending and beginning simultaneously, because a door closing behind you is the past becoming inaccessible, and the future becoming the only direction, and both of these things happen in the same instant, in the same sound, which lasts less than a second and contains an entire metaphysics.

You cannot reopen a door that has closed. You can open a door that resembles it, in the same wall, in the same frame. You can turn the same handle. But it will be a different door, because you are a different person, and the room behind it will be a different room, because you have been in it, and a room that has been occupied is never again the room it was before. Occupation changes a room the way reading changes a book — the words are the same, but the space between them has been filled with the reader's attention, which leaves traces that are invisible and indelible.

The Zohar speaks of a gate that opens once and does not open again. The sages interpreted this as the gate of repentance, which is available to each soul only once. I suspect it is simpler than that. I suspect it is any door, every door. Each one opens once. What follows is a different door wearing the same face.

The rooms are getting smaller now. I can feel it.
